# Ch 1: Engineers do not lack intuition, they lack vocabulary

## Hook

Most engineers get told they "lack product sense" and what it actually means is that they made the move but could not name it. Naming is the unlock.

## Core idea

Engineers exercise product-adjacent reasoning every week: root-cause debugging is problem framing, API design is user empathy, build-vs-buy is tradeoff analysis. The reason product sense feels mysterious is that nobody named these moves as product thinking, so the engineer reads them as "just good engineering" and the product room reads the silence as absence. The reasoning is there. The vocabulary is not.

## Quotes

- Gergely Orosz, *The Product-Minded Software Engineer*, 2019: "Product-minded engineers are developers with lots of interest in the product itself. They want to understand why decisions are made, how people use the product, and love to be involved in making product decisions." (Source: `blog.pragmaticengineer.com/the-product-minded-engineer/`)
- Jules Walter, *How to develop product sense*, Lenny's Newsletter: "Product sense is the skill of consistently being able to craft products that have the intended impact on their users." It relies on empathy and creativity as its two pillars. (Source: `lennysnewsletter.com/p/product-sense`)
- Marty Cagan, *Empowered*: "An empowered engineer isn't someone that gets to figure out the best way to code something. An empowered engineer figures out the best way to solve a problem." (Source: `Empowered`, Cagan & Jones, 2020)

## Named examples

- Shopify's Jean-Michel Lemieux (then Head of Engineering) described product engineers as those who engage with the "why" actively and have a "thirst for using technologies to leapfrog human/user problems". Cited in Orosz's essay.
- Stripe, Linear, and Figma are the canonical engineer-led products where the founders have gone on record that engineering-side product intuition replaced much of traditional PM work for the first several years. The lack was never intuition — it was a culture that named the engineer's product calls as decisions rather than implementation details.

## Engineering analog

Every engineer who has stopped a team from shipping a feature by asking "but what problem does this actually solve" has done product framing without calling it that. The move is identical to refusing to land a bug fix without reading the stack trace first, just applied to a product spec instead of a test failure.

## Anti-pattern to catch

The reader defaulting to "I just need to learn the frameworks" rather than "I need to name the move I already make." The chapter rejects "you are missing a skill" and replaces it with "you are missing a label for the skill you already have."

## Suggested diagram

A two-column illustration: on the left, engineering moves (root-cause debugging, API design, architecture options, PR with rollout plan), each as a small card. On the right, the same moves labeled with their product-sense name (Frame, empathy, Width-before-Depth, falsifiable recommendation). A bridge arrow labeled "same move, different room" connects the columns.
