# Ch 5: Four common failure modes in product-sense interviews

## Hook

Interviewers can usually tell within the first two minutes whether the candidate is a product thinker or a framework reciter. The four failure modes below are what tips them off.

## Core idea

Exponent's 2024 PM Interview Report names product sense as the number-one reason candidates fail PM interviews at FAANG companies, cited in 43% of rejection debriefs. The failure is rarely lack of knowledge. It is four recognizable patterns that show up in the first five minutes: skipping straight to solutions, framework recitation, optimizing the wrong problem, and not naming a tradeoff. This chapter names each pattern with a concrete interviewer-side observation and gives the reader the recovery move.

## The four failure modes (for the chapter to name)

1. **Solution-first**: candidate hears "improve Spotify" and, within 30 seconds, is describing features. The interviewer has not heard a user, a problem, or a framing yet. This is the Why-First Check skipped.
2. **Framework recitation**: candidate opens with "I'll use the CIRCLES framework" and works through C-I-R-C-L-E-S mechanically. The interviewer can predict every section because they have heard the same scaffolding forty times.
3. **Optimizing the wrong problem**: candidate accepts the stated question as the real question. "How would you improve Uber's driver app" gets answered for drivers, when the interviewer wanted to see the candidate reframe the question around the real problem (driver retention, regulatory pressure, rider experience, or a combination).
4. **No named tradeoff**: candidate picks a solution and argues for it without naming what the solution gives up. Every real product decision has a sacrifice. Answers that only describe the upside read as advocacy, not analysis.

## Named examples

- **CIRCLES framework (Product School)**: the most recited framework in PM interview prep, originally from Lewis Lin's *Decode and Conquer*. It is not a bad framework; it is the most abused one. Interviewers at Meta, Google, and Stripe have all written that hearing "CIRCLES" in the first sentence is a negative signal because it almost always precedes mechanical execution.
- **Meta PM interviews**: Rohan Katyal's Medium essay "How I prepared for Meta PM interviews: Product Sense" (2023) explicitly names framework recitation as the thing to avoid and walks through how to replace it with a conversational reframing move.

## Engineering analog

The same four failure modes show up in technical interviews: jumping to code without clarifying requirements (mode 1), reciting a design-patterns checklist (mode 2), building the wrong system (mode 3), and picking an architecture without naming the tradeoffs between consistency and availability or between cost and latency (mode 4). Engineers who have sat on the interviewer side of a system-design round will recognize all four instantly.

## Anti-pattern to catch

The reader concluding "frameworks are bad, I should improvise" and walking in with no structure at all. The chapter must land the nuance: frameworks are scaffolding for thinking, not substitutes for it. Use CIRCLES as a checklist in your head, not a script out of your mouth.

## Suggested diagram

A 2x2 grid. Rows: "what the interviewer sees" vs "what the candidate thinks they are doing". Columns: "surface level" vs "real move". Each of the four failure modes occupies one cell, with the recovery move in small text. Color: primary green for the correct move, red for the failure mode.
