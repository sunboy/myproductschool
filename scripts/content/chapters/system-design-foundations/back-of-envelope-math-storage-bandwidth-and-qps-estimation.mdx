  "body_mdx": "## The Conversion That Changes Everything\n\nEvery capacity problem starts the same way: someone says \"we expect a million users\" and the room nods. Nobody asks what that means for infrastructure. Back-of-envelope math is how you translate vague scale requirements into concrete infrastructure decisions, and the first tool is the simplest one.\n\nDivide daily requests by 86,400 (seconds in a day) to get average QPS. Then multiply by a peak multiplier.\n\n```\naverage QPS = (DAU × actions per user per day) ÷ 86,400\npeak QPS    = average QPS × peak multiplier\n```\n\nPeak multiplier is where engineers consistently underestimate. A predictable SaaS product runs 2x. A ride-sharing app during evening rush hits 5-10x. An e-commerce flash sale can spike 100x. Twitter's 500 million daily tweets come out to 5,800 average QPS, but the actual system is sized for 7,000 because of predictable morning peaks. Designing for average means the system fails exactly when users notice most.\n\n## Storage: Three Numbers You Always Multiply\n\nStorage estimates have a mechanical structure. Get them wrong by omitting one of these three multipliers and your estimate is off by an order of magnitude.\n\n```\ntotal storage = data per operation\n              × operations per day\n              × retention period\n              × replication factor\n```\n\nTwitter is a clean worked example. A tweet is roughly 300 bytes. 500 million tweets per day. Retain for 5 years. Replicate 3x for durability.\n\n```\n300 bytes × 500M tweets × 365 × 5 years × 3 replicas\n= 300 × 5×10⁸ × 1,825 × 3\n= ~825 TB raw → ~2.5 PB with replicas\n```\n\nThe research puts the text-only figure at 140 GB/day or 50 TB/year. Add media at 100x tweet size and you're at 10 TB/day, 4 PB/year, for images and video alone. The multipliers compound fast.\n\nReplication factor is the one engineers forget first. Standard durability requires 3x. If you're estimating storage for a database and skipping replication, you're underestimating by 3x before you've done anything else wrong.\n\n## Bandwidth: Bits Versus Bytes\n\nThe most consistent calculation mistake in system design interviews: conflating bits and bytes.\n\nNetwork bandwidth is measured in megabits per second (Mbps). Storage is measured in megabytes (MB). A 100 Mbps connection delivers 12.5 MB/s, not 100.\n\n```\nbandwidth (Mbps) = data per operation (in megabits) × QPS\n                 = data per operation (in megabytes) × QPS × 8\n```\n\nAdd a 1.3-2x safety margin. Real-world throughput is 70-90% of theoretical maximum because of protocol overhead, TCP acknowledgments, and congestion. Advertised bandwidth is a ceiling, not a guarantee.\n\nNetflix's CDN numbers illustrate why this matters in practice. 250 million daily active users, 500 million play starts per day, with video bitrates averaging around 3-8 Mbps per stream. The math on concurrent streams at peak (say, 5x the average of 5,800 QPS) produces egress requirements that would be catastrophic to serve from a single origin. Netflix's answer is Open Connect, their custom CDN, which achieves a 98% cache hit rate. Only 2% of requests touch origin servers. The bandwidth problem at the edge is solved by caching aggressively close to users.\n\n## Read-to-Write Ratio Shapes the Architecture\n\nOnce you have QPS and storage estimates, the ratio of reads to writes determines which scaling levers you pull.\n\nMost systems are not balanced. Content-heavy applications run at 100:1 reads to writes or higher. Even \"write-heavy\" systems like messaging platforms are typically 10:1 read-heavy when you account for message history retrieval. Twitter's own numbers show 75,000 QPS for reads versus 3,500 QPS for writes at average load.\n\n```mermaid\nflowchart LR\n    A[\"Estimate QPS & Storage\"] --> B{\"Read:Write Ratio\"}\n    B -->|\"10:1 to 100:1\"| C[\"Read Replicas\\nCDN/Cache\\nDenormalize reads\"]\n    B -->|\"~1:1 or write-heavy\"| D[\"Write sharding\\nLSM-tree storage\\nQueue-based writes\"]\n    C --> E[\"Horizontal read scaling\"]\n    D --> F[\"Partitioned write scaling\"]\n```\n\nThis ratio is what tells you whether to invest in read replicas and CDN layers versus write sharding and queue buffers. Stripe processes 500 million API requests daily across 2,000 MongoDB shards. That's not an accident of scale, it's a deliberate architectural decision driven by the write throughput requirements of payment processing, where a failed write costs real money.\n\n## Putting It Together: A Five-Minute Estimation\n\nA social photo app: 50 million DAU, each user uploads 2 photos per day and views 100.\n\n**QPS:**\n- Write QPS: (50M × 2) ÷ 86,400 ≈ 1,160 writes/sec\n- Read QPS: (50M × 100) ÷ 86,400 ≈ 57,900 reads/sec\n- Peak writes (3x evening spike): ~3,500 QPS\n- Peak reads (3x): ~174,000 QPS\n- Read:write ratio: ~50:1\n\n**Storage (5-year retention, 3x replication):**\n- Average photo: 1 MB compressed\n- Daily: 50M × 2 × 1 MB = 100 TB/day\n- 5 years × 365 × 100 TB × 3 replicas = ~547 PB\n\n**Bandwidth (egress for reads):**\n- 57,900 reads/sec × 1 MB = 57.9 GB/sec = ~464 Gbps average\n- Peak: ~1.4 Tbps\n\nThat egress number immediately tells you: you need a CDN, aggressive caching, and thumbnail serving for the feed. Nobody is pulling full-resolution photos in a scrollable feed. The back-of-envelope calculation surfaces the constraint before you've written a single line of code.\n\n## Where Estimates Go Wrong\n\nThe three failure modes, in order of frequency:\n\nFirst, designing for average load. The system works perfectly in load tests and fails at 8pm on a Tuesday. Always multiply by peak.\n\nSecond, forgetting replication in storage math. Your estimate is 3x too optimistic before accounting for any other error.\n\nThird, mistaking bits for bytes in bandwidth calculations. A 1 Gbps uplink handles 125 MB/s of actual data, not 1,000 MB/s. This makes a 4x difference in how many links you need.\n\nThe numbers in this chapter are not reference tables to memorize. They're anchors for reasoning. When a system design conversation starts with \"we have 10 million users,\" the right next move is to reach for 86,400 and start dividing. Everything downstream, the database sharding strategy, the CDN investment, the write queue architecture, follows from that first calculation."
