  "body_mdx": "## Start With the Ratio, Not the Stack\n\nBefore you pick a database, choose a replication strategy, or debate NoSQL vs SQL, you need one number: your read-to-write ratio. That single figure shapes every major architectural decision that follows.\n\nMost database workloads land around 10:1 reads to writes. A product page view, a social media timeline load, a search result render, these all read far more than they write. But that baseline hides enormous variance. Netflix serving video metadata to 200 million subscribers operates at ratios far beyond 10:1. An observability platform ingesting metrics from thousands of servers flips the ratio entirely, with writes outnumbering reads 100:1 or more.\n\nThe architecture you need for each of these is not a variation. It's a different design.\n\n## Read-Heavy Systems: The Caching Imperative\n\nWhen reads dominate, the primary database becomes a bottleneck fast. The answer is to put something cheap between the database and the clients.\n\nNetflix's EVCache system handles 400 million operations per second against 14.3 petabytes of cached data. The cache hit rate is 98%. That means for every 100 requests, only two ever reach the origin servers. The system serving the 2024 Tyson-Paul fight to tens of millions of concurrent viewers wasn't scaling the database, it was scaling the cache.\n\nThis is the defining characteristic of read-heavy architecture: reads go to replicas and caches; writes go to a primary. The primary is precious. You don't expose it to read traffic.\n\n![The Read/Write Ratio and Why It Determines Your Architecture](/content-images/system-design-foundations/the-read-write-ratio-and-why-it-determines-your-architecture.png)\n\nRead replicas extend this pattern to the database layer. A social platform with 50 million daily active users running 10 timeline reads per user generates around 6,000 read queries per second at average load, with peaks at 2-5x that. You can't serve 30,000 read QPS from a single primary. You add read replicas, route reads to them, and reserve the primary for writes.\n\nThe cost: replication lag. Replicas trail the primary by milliseconds to seconds. A user who posts a comment might not see it immediately if their next request hits a lagging replica. For most products, this is acceptable. For financial ledgers or ticket inventory, it is not.\n\nCQRS (Command Query Responsibility Segregation) takes this split further. The write model stays normalized and correct; the read model is a denormalized projection optimized for queries. These can live in the same database or separate ones. The key idea is that the data shape for reading is different from the data shape for writing, and forcing them to share a schema makes both worse.\n\n## Write-Heavy Systems: Durability First, Reads Second\n\nFlip the ratio and the problem inverts.\n\nAn observability platform ingesting metrics from a production fleet is write-heavy by nature. Every server emits CPU, memory, and latency samples every few seconds. Reads are mostly aggregations, \"average latency over the last hour, grouped by endpoint\", and they happen far less frequently than writes.\n\nCaching helps almost nothing here. You can't cache data that changes continuously. What matters is write throughput and durable storage.\n\nTime-series databases like ClickHouse handle this with append-only write paths that avoid random I/O. Instead of updating rows, they append new entries. Partitioning by time (hourly or daily chunks) makes retention cheap: deleting data older than a year becomes a metadata operation, not a table scan. ClickHouse runs sub-second aggregation queries over billions of rows.\n\nLSM-tree databases (RocksDB, LevelDB, Cassandra) take the same philosophy further. Writes land in a write-ahead log and an in-memory buffer. Periodic compaction flushes them to disk in sorted runs. Random writes become sequential writes, and sequential writes are the fastest thing a disk does. The tradeoff: reads become more expensive, since data might be spread across multiple levels.\n\nFor write-heavy workloads, indexing strategy matters too, but in the opposite direction from read-heavy systems. Every index you add on a write-heavy table is a tax on every write. A table with 8 indexes on a high-throughput insert path is a table with 8 extra write operations per row. Index only what you must.\n\n## When the Ratio Forces a Hard Consistency Choice\n\nSome systems can't afford any consistency relaxation, regardless of ratio. Concert ticket booking is the classic case. It doesn't matter that reads outnumber writes, the system must guarantee that two users can't purchase the last seat simultaneously.\n\nThis is the CAP theorem made concrete. In a distributed system, network partitions happen. When they do, you choose between consistency (refuse requests until partition heals) and availability (serve requests with potentially stale data). Ticket inventory is a CP system: during a partition, reads may block, but you cannot sell a ticket twice.\n\nMost read-heavy systems choose AP. If a recommendation is slightly stale, no one gets hurt. Financial and inventory systems choose CP. The business consequence of wrong data is too high.\n\nThe common misconception is that CAP forces you to sacrifice one property forever. It doesn't. You only make the choice during a partition. Outside of partitions, a well-designed system can satisfy all three.\n\n## Estimating Your Ratio Before You Build\n\nBack-of-the-envelope math is the fastest way to validate architectural assumptions. For a hypothetical social platform:\n\n```\nRead QPS:  50M DAU × 10 timeline reads/day ÷ 86,400 sec = ~5,800 read QPS\nWrite QPS: 50M DAU × 1 post/day ÷ 86,400 sec = ~580 write QPS\nRatio: ~10:1\nPeak:  2-5x average → provision for 12-29k read QPS\n```\n\nThat calculation tells you immediately: read replicas and a caching layer are required. The primary database is not the scaling story.\n\nFor an observability platform:\n\n```\nWrite QPS: 100k servers × 10 metrics/sec = 1M writes/sec\nRead QPS:  10 engineers querying dashboards = ~100 read QPS\nRatio: ~10,000:1 (writes dominate)\n```\n\nNo amount of read replica configuration helps here. You need a write-optimized database and a completely different storage strategy.\n\n## The Number That Drives Everything Else\n\nThe read-to-write ratio is not one input among many. It's the first filter. Get it wrong and every subsequent decision, database choice, indexing strategy, replication topology, consistency model, is built on the wrong foundation.\n\nWhen someone asks why Netflix built EVCache instead of just scaling their database, or why Datadog uses a time-series database instead of PostgreSQL, the answer is the same: they knew their ratio. Start there."
