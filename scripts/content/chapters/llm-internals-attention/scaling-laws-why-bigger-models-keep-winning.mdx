{"hook_text": "The Chinchilla paper showed that the ML community had been building models too large for their training budgets. Optimal performance requires roughly 20 training tokens per parameter, a ratio that flipped how labs allocate compute between model size and dataset size.", "body_mdx": "In 2020, OpenAI's scaling laws paper (Kaplan et al.) showed that language model loss decreases predictably as a power law of model size, dataset size, and compute. The key finding: if you have a fixed compute budget, you should put most of it into model parameters.\n\nDeepMind ran that math in 2022 and showed OpenAI was wrong.\n\n## The Kaplan Scaling Laws\n\nKaplan et al. fit a power law to cross-entropy loss across dozens of model sizes:\n\n```\nL(N) = (N_c / N)^(alpha_N)\nL(D) = (D_c / D)^(alpha_D)\n```\n\nWhere $N$ is parameter count, $D$ is training tokens, and $\\alpha$ values are empirically fit constants. The finding: loss scales more steeply with parameters than with data. Given a fixed compute budget $C$, allocating most of it to model size (with dataset size kept proportional but smaller) minimizes loss.\n\nThis justified GPT-3 (175B parameters, trained on roughly 300B tokens). It also justified the strategy of training models once and deploying them widely, because bigger models appeared to be the path to better performance.\n\n## Chinchilla: The Correction\n\nDeepMind's Hoffman et al. (2022) trained over 400 models at different sizes and token counts, holding compute fixed. Their finding:\n\n**Compute-optimal training requires roughly equal scaling of model size and dataset size.**\n\nThe optimal token count is approximately 20x the parameter count:\n\n```\nD_optimal ≈ 20 * N\n```\n\nFor a 70B parameter model: optimal training data ≈ 1.4 trillion tokens.\nFor a 7B parameter model: optimal training data ≈ 140 billion tokens.\n\nGPT-3 (175B parameters) was trained on ~300B tokens. Chinchilla's formula says it should have been trained on ~3.5T tokens for that parameter count, or alternatively, the 175B parameters were excessive for a 300B token training budget. DeepMind trained a 70B model (Chinchilla) on 1.4T tokens and it outperformed GPT-3.\n\n```mermaid\ngraph LR\n    A[Fixed Compute Budget] --> B[Kaplan: Maximize Parameters]\n    A --> C[Chinchilla: Balance Parameters and Tokens]\n    B --> D[GPT-3: 175B params, 300B tokens]\n    C --> E[Chinchilla: 70B params, 1.4T tokens]\n    E --> F[Outperforms GPT-3 on most benchmarks]\n```\n\n## Why Labs Ignored Optimal Training Anyway\n\nPost-Chinchilla, an interesting split emerged. Inference cost becomes the dominant variable once you're in deployment.\n\nA smaller model trained on more data achieves the same loss as a larger model trained on less data. But at inference time, the smaller model is faster and cheaper to serve. Meta's Llama models leaned heavily into this: Llama 1 7B was trained on 1T tokens, roughly 7x the Chinchilla-optimal amount for its size. Llama 2 7B used 2T tokens. Llama 3 8B used 15T tokens.\n\nThe deliberate over-training strategy: sacrifice training efficiency to get a smaller model that's cheaper to run at scale. The loss ends up similar to what you'd get from a larger model, but on hardware that costs 5-10x less per token generated.\n\nMistral 7B (2023) pushed this further: a 7B model competitive with Llama 2 13B on most benchmarks, smaller, cheaper, faster.\n\n## The Power Law in Practice\n\nScaling laws let you predict performance before running the full training job. Given:\n- Compute budget $C$ (in FLOPs)\n- Empirical loss measurements from small runs\n\nYou can estimate the loss you'll achieve at full scale. This is how labs justify multi-billion dollar training runs: they fit the power law at small scale and extrapolate.\n\nThe key scaling law equation (simplified Chinchilla form):\n\n```\nL(N, D) = E + A/N^alpha + B/D^beta\n```\n\nWhere $E$ is the irreducible entropy of the language, $A$, $B$ are fit constants, and $\\alpha \\approx 0.34$, $\\beta \\approx 0.28$ from the paper.\n\nFor a compute budget $C \\approx 6ND$ (the approximation that training a model with $N$ parameters on $D$ tokens uses $6ND$ FLOPs):\n\n```\nN_opt = C^0.49   # roughly sqrt(C)\nD_opt = C^0.51   # roughly sqrt(C)\n```\n\nThe compute-optimal frontier is roughly: scale parameters and tokens together at approximately equal rates.\n\n## Emergent Capabilities\n\nA surprising finding from scaling: some capabilities appear to emerge discontinuously as model size increases. Models below a threshold show near-zero performance on a task; models above it show dramatically better performance.\n\nBenchmarks that showed these patterns include:\n- Chain-of-thought reasoning (emerged around 100B parameters)\n- Few-shot learning (improved dramatically from 7B to 70B)\n- Instruction following (dramatically improved with RLHF post-training, but base capability scales)\n\nThe \"emergence\" framing has been challenged. Schaeffer et al. (2023) argued that many apparent emergent capabilities are artifacts of discontinuous evaluation metrics: if you use a metric that only rewards full correctness, gradual capability improvements look like a sudden jump. With smoother metrics, capabilities grow continuously.\n\nThis matters for planning: if capabilities are truly emergent, you can't predict them from smaller runs. If they're measurement artifacts, scaling law extrapolation should work all the way up.\n\n## Beyond the Power Law: Data Quality\n\nScaling laws fit on web crawl data. The constants change dramatically with data quality.\n\nFalconRefinedWeb showed that a model trained on high-quality filtered web data could match or exceed a model trained on 5-10x more raw data. The lesson: the Chinchilla formula's 20 tokens/parameter is approximate, and the constant depends heavily on data quality.\n\nCodex and its successors showed that models trained specifically on code datasets develop qualitatively different capabilities than models trained on equivalent compute of web text. The distribution of training data is a design choice with non-obvious effects that scaling laws don't fully capture.\n\n## What Scaling Laws Don't Predict\n\nScaling laws predict cross-entropy loss on held-out text. They don't directly predict:\n- Performance on specific downstream tasks\n- How well RLHF post-training will improve alignment\n- Whether the model will learn to use tools reliably\n- Hallucination rates\n- Compositional reasoning ability\n\nA model that follows the power law on loss may still fail badly on tasks requiring multi-step reasoning, precise factual recall, or code generation with specific constraints. Scaling law extrapolations need to be combined with task-specific evaluation at smaller scales to be trusted for production decisions.\n\nThe practical takeaway: scaling laws are reliable enough to justify compute allocation decisions at the billions-of-dollar level, but they're not a substitute for evaluating models on the tasks you actually care about."}
