{"hook_text": "Getting stakeholders to agree on the selection criterion before seeing the options is the only way to prevent criterion drift. Once people see the options, they change the criterion to match their preference.", "body_mdx": "## The Problem With Showing Options First\n\nA common planning meeting structure: the PM presents the roadmap candidates with supporting data, stakeholders debate the options, and eventually the group reaches a decision by voting or by whoever has the most authority. This process feels collaborative. It is mostly theater.\n\nThe problem is sequencing. When people see the options first, they immediately start evaluating from their own perspective. The sales leader mentally filters for \"what closes deals faster.\" The engineering manager filters for \"what creates less technical debt.\" The growth lead filters for \"what moves the DAU chart.\" Everyone forms a preference within the first few minutes.\n\nWhen the selection criterion is introduced later in the conversation, each person evaluates the proposed criterion against their pre-formed preference. If the criterion doesn't support their preferred option, they challenge the criterion. Criterion drift begins. The group spends the next 45 minutes arguing about which criterion should govern the decision rather than applying a criterion to the decision.\n\nThe fix is to establish criteria before revealing options. This requires some discipline and a different meeting structure, but it changes the entire downstream dynamic.\n\n## Why Criterion Drift Happens\n\nCriterion drift is a form of motivated reasoning. The mind works backward from a conclusion: \"I want feature X to win, therefore the criterion that should govern this decision is the one that makes feature X the best choice.\" This is not malicious. It's how human cognition works under mild self-interest.\n\n```mermaid\nsequenceDiagram\n    participant Meeting as Planning Meeting\n    participant SL as Sales Lead\n    participant EM as Engineering Manager\n    participant GL as Growth Lead\n\n    Meeting->>SL: Here are the 5 options...\n    Meeting->>EM: Here are the 5 options...\n    Meeting->>GL: Here are the 5 options...\n    SL-->>SL: Forms preference: Option B (closes deals)\n    EM-->>EM: Forms preference: Option D (reduces debt)\n    GL-->>GL: Forms preference: Option A (moves DAU)\n    Meeting->>Meeting: \"What criterion should govern?\"\n    SL-->>Meeting: \"We should optimize for revenue impact\"\n    EM-->>Meeting: \"Long-term health matters more than short-term metrics\"\n    GL-->>Meeting: \"Growth is the priority at our stage\"\n    Meeting->>Meeting: 45 minutes of criterion debate...\n    Note over Meeting: No decision reached, or HiPPO wins\n```\n\nCriteria established after options are presented will almost always reflect the room's preferences rather than a principled analysis. The criteria debate becomes a proxy war for the options debate.\n\n## Establishing Criteria First: The Process\n\nThe pre-criteria approach requires splitting the planning process into two separate conversations.\n\n**Conversation 1: What are we trying to maximize?**\n\nBefore any options are discussed, the group answers: given our current situation, what is the most important thing for this cycle's decisions to produce? The answer should be specific. Not \"growth\" but \"activation rate for users who complete onboarding in the first 7 days.\" Not \"revenue\" but \"expansion revenue from existing enterprise accounts.\"\n\nThe criteria question should also include constraints. What is off-limits? What are we willing to sacrifice? A team optimizing for activation rate should be explicit that it's willing to defer retention features if they conflict. Making the constraint explicit prevents it from being relitigated during option evaluation.\n\n**Conversation 2: Which option best serves the criteria?**\n\nOnce criteria are established and documented, the options are introduced. The evaluation question is now narrower: which option best satisfies the criterion we already agreed on? Stakeholders can still advocate for their preferences, but the frame has shifted. Arguing against the top-scoring option now requires challenging the criterion that was already agreed upon, which is harder and less common.\n\n## What to Do When Stakeholders Can't Agree on Criteria\n\nSometimes the room can't agree on criteria either. This is useful information. If stakeholders cannot agree on what the team is trying to produce, the problem is not prioritization. The problem is strategy.\n\nA team that can't agree on whether the priority criterion is activation rate or enterprise revenue has a strategy problem, not a roadmap problem. The prioritization meeting should not try to resolve this. It should escalate it.\n\nPractically: the facilitator should name the disagreement explicitly. \"It sounds like we have two different views on whether this team's primary goal is acquisition or retention. That's a leadership decision and it needs to be made before we can sequence the roadmap. Let's table the prioritization and schedule a separate session with [appropriate decision-maker].\" This feels like slowing things down. It is actually speeding things up, because the alternative is a prioritization decision made on an unresolved strategic question.\n\n## Single Criterion vs. Weighted Multi-Criteria\n\nSimpler is better. A single criterion is easier to apply consistently and harder to game. When criteria are weighted across multiple dimensions, stakeholders can argue about the weights rather than the decision. The criterion weighting debate replaces the criterion selection debate, and nothing is gained.\n\nThat said, some decisions legitimately involve multiple constraints. A release timing constraint may be non-negotiable. A budget cap is a hard limit. In these cases, treat hard constraints as filters (binary pass/fail) and apply a single primary criterion to the items that pass the filters.\n\n```\nCriteria hierarchy (recommended structure):\n\n1. Hard filters (binary - must pass all):\n   - Fits within available engineering capacity this cycle\n   - Does not require a third-party dependency we don't have\n   - No regulatory risk\n\n2. Primary criterion (the tie-breaking optimization target):\n   - Which option maximizes 7-day activation rate?\n\n3. Tiebreaker (for options that score equivalently on primary):\n   - Which option creates less technical maintenance burden?\n```\n\nThis structure doesn't eliminate judgment, but it organizes the judgment into stages where stakeholders agree on the structure before applying it.\n\n## Documenting Criteria Decisions\n\nThe criteria agreement should be written down before the options are evaluated. This sounds obvious and is consistently skipped. Without documentation, the criteria that were agreed upon informally during the first conversation will be reinterpreted or expanded during the second conversation.\n\nA short document, even a shared note, that captures: \"For this cycle, we're optimizing for 7-day activation rate. We agreed we're willing to defer retention features and mobile-specific work to focus on this. We're not considering items that require more than 4 weeks of effort.\" This is enough to anchor the second conversation.\n\nIf a stakeholder tries to introduce a new criterion during option evaluation, the documented criteria give the facilitator a clean reference point: \"That's a valid consideration, but we agreed we'd optimize for activation rate this cycle. Is there a reason to revisit that decision before we evaluate options?\" Revisiting is allowed, but it's an explicit choice with a process cost, not a casual drift.\n\n## The Facilitator's Role\n\nThe criteria-first process doesn't run itself. Someone needs to hold the structure. In most teams, this is the product manager, but it can be any meeting participant with enough standing to pause the conversation when it drifts.\n\nThe facilitator's primary job is sequencing enforcement: keep criteria out of the options conversation and options out of the criteria conversation. The secondary job is naming disagreements without letting them become covert, unresolved conflicts that contaminate the decision. Both require more meeting discipline than most planning sessions currently have, but the quality of decisions that follow is materially different."}
