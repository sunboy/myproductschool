It's about which problem the next feature is actually solving, and whether that problem is worth the six weeks it costs.", "body_mdx": "## Where Engineers Actually Sit in Prioritization\n\nThe standard framing is that prioritization happens upstream, PMs decide what to build, and engineers decide how to build it. This framing is wrong in practice, and engineers who accept it are opting out of conversations where their input would change the outcome.\n\nEngineers hold several types of information that are uniquely relevant to prioritization and that are almost never formally included in the prioritization process. They know how long things actually take, versus how long they're assumed to take. They know which parts of the codebase are fragile, which improvements would make subsequent work faster, and which dependencies make certain orderings of work much cheaper than others. They see system behavior at a level that product managers and executives don't.\n\nThis information changes prioritization decisions materially when it's surfaced. When it isn't surfaced, decisions get made on partial information and the people with the most relevant technical context sit in planning meetings feeling like witnesses rather than participants.\n\n## The Question That Opens the Conversation\n\nThere's a question that changes the nature of a prioritization conversation when asked early: \"What problem are we actually solving?\"\n\nNot \"what feature are we building?\" Not \"what does the spec say?\" The specific question: what user problem, business problem, or technical problem will be different after this work is complete, and how will we know?\n\nThis question does several things at once. It forces the team to articulate a mechanism (not just a feature). It creates a falsifiable outcome that can be checked against reality. It opens space for engineers to point out when the proposed solution doesn't connect to the stated problem in the way the requester thinks it does.\n\n```mermaid\nflowchart TD\n    A[\"Feature Request:\\n'Build a notification center'\"] --> B{\"What problem\\nare we solving?\"}\n    B --> C[\"Users miss important\\nactions when away from app\"]\n    B --> D[\"Support volume from\\n'I didn't know about X'\"\n    B --> E[\"Activation drop-off\\nat day 7\"]\n    C --> F[\"Is a notification center\\nthe right solution to this?\"]\n    D --> G[\"Is a notification center\\nthe right solution to this?\"]\n    E --> H[\"Is a notification center\\nthe right solution to this?\"]\n    F --> I[\"Maybe: but email\\ndigest might be faster\\nand more effective\"]\n    G --> J[\"Probably: but in-app\\nalerts might work\\nwithout the infra\"]\n    H --> K[\"Probably not: this is a\\nonboarding problem,\\nnot a notification problem\"]\n    style A fill:#fef3c7\n    style I fill:#d1fae5\n    style J fill:#d1fae5\n    style K fill:#fca5a5\n```\n\nThe same feature request produces completely different answers depending on what problem it's supposed to solve. Engineers who ask the problem question before estimating work are doing the team a service.\n\n## The Effort-Scope Conversation\n\nMost feature requests arrive with an implicit assumption about scope. Someone says \"add a bulk import feature\" and imagines a simple CSV upload. The engineering estimate for a simple CSV upload might be two weeks. The engineering estimate for a bulk import feature that handles validation errors, handles large files asynchronously, provides status feedback, and doesn't break existing single-import flows is six weeks.\n\nThis is not a failure of estimation. It's a failure to specify scope before estimating. Engineers are often asked to estimate scope-ambiguous requests and they sometimes give a low estimate based on the simple interpretation, which later balloons when the full requirements emerge.\n\nThe right conversation to have before estimating is: what is the minimum version of this that solves the problem? And what does the full version look like?\n\nThis conversation has two benefits. First, the minimum version might be sufficient, and the team saves four weeks. Second, the cost difference between the minimum and full versions is now explicit, and the PM or stakeholder can make an informed tradeoff.\n\n```\nScope conversation structure:\n\nFeature request: \"Bulk import\"\n\nQuestion 1: What's the minimum that solves the problem?\n  → \"Users need to upload a CSV and have their contacts imported.\"\n  → Minimum version: single-file CSV, no async, basic validation\n  → Estimate: 2 weeks\n\nQuestion 2: What does the full version look like?\n  → Async processing, progress UI, error recovery, large file support, format options\n  → Full estimate: 6-8 weeks\n\nQuestion 3: Does the minimum version solve the problem well enough?\n  → If yes: build minimum. Backlog the enhancements.\n  → If no: define what specific gap the extra weeks close.\n```\n\nEngineers who consistently surface this conversation structure get better scope decisions. They also build a reputation for understanding the product, which creates more access to earlier stages of the prioritization process.\n\n## Surfacing Technical Debt as a Prioritization Input\n\nTechnical debt rarely appears in prioritization discussions as a first-class item. It's either invisible (not in the backlog at all) or underweighted (\"we'll get to that eventually\"). The consequence is that debt accumulates until it causes a crisis, at which point the team does emergency remediation under pressure.\n\nThe framing that gets technical debt into prioritization discussions is cost multiplier framing. Not \"we should clean up the authentication module\" but \"the authentication module's current state adds 30% to the estimate of every feature that touches user sessions, which includes the next four items on the roadmap.\"\n\nThis reframes debt from a maintenance concern (easy to defer) to an opportunity cost concern (has a specific current cost that's being paid every sprint). PMs and executives respond differently to cost multiplier framing than they do to abstract \"we should clean this up\" framing.\n\n## The Sequence Dependency Conversation\n\nNot all prioritization decisions are independent. Some items, when done first, make subsequent items significantly cheaper or faster. Others, when done first, create constraints that make subsequent items harder.\n\nEngineers often see these dependencies and don't surface them explicitly. \"If we build the notification system before the activity feed, the notification system ends up coupled to the feed's data model, which makes the feed harder to change later. If we do it the other way, both are cheaper.\" This is sequencing information with a concrete cost implication, and it belongs in the prioritization conversation, not in a Slack message after the plan is set.\n\nThe question worth asking in planning: are there any sequencing dependencies between the items we're considering that would change the estimates if we did them in a different order?\n\n## The Conversation About What's Not Feasible This Cycle\n\nEngineers sometimes know that something on the roadmap is not actually achievable in the stated timeframe without cutting corners that create future problems. Saying this is uncomfortable. Not saying it is worse.\n\nThe useful framing is not \"we can't do this\" but \"here's what we'd have to cut to hit this timeline, and here are the consequences of each cut.\" This turns a constraint delivery into a decision input. The PM or executive can then make an informed tradeoff rather than discovering the constraint when the feature ships with technical debt baked in.\n\n## Building the Habit of Asking\n\nThe conversations described above are not difficult. They don't require executive access or special authority. They require the habit of asking the right questions at the right stage.\n\nBefore estimating: what problem does this solve?\nBefore scoping: what's the minimum version that solves it?\nWhen technical debt is relevant: what does this cost in multiplier terms?\nWhen sequencing matters: is there an ordering that makes this cheaper?\nWhen timelines are aggressive: what tradeoffs are implied by this timeline?\n\nEngineers who ask these questions are not overstepping. They're supplying information the prioritization process needs but often doesn't have. The product sense that gets rewarded in engineering careers is precisely this: understanding why the work matters, not just how to build it."}
