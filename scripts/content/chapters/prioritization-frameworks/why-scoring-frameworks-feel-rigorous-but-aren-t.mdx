{"hook_text": "RICE scores create the appearance of rigor, but Reach, Impact, and Confidence estimates are almost always informed guesses that confirm the decision already made.", "body_mdx": "## The Formula Doesn't Create the Certainty\n\nWhen a team commits a prioritization decision to a spreadsheet, something shifts in the room. The argument is no longer about preferences; it's about the numbers. That shift feels like progress. It usually isn't.\n\nScoring frameworks like RICE (Reach, Impact, Confidence, Effort), ICE, and weighted criteria matrices share the same structural problem: they turn qualitative judgment into numerical inputs, then perform arithmetic on those inputs to produce a ranked output. The math is real. The inputs are not.\n\nThis matters because the framework is often used as a social tool, not an analytical one. The goal is to get the room to agree. The spreadsheet gives cover for a decision that was already made informally. When the numbers come back wrong, the inputs get adjusted until they come back right.\n\n## Garbage In, Ranked Garbage Out\n\nConsider how RICE scores are built in practice. Reach is a 30-day user estimate. Teams routinely inflate this because they're optimistic about adoption curves or unsure which user segment counts. Impact is a multiplier, usually 0.25 to 3.0, guessing how much the feature affects the metric being optimized. These values are chosen from a handful of buckets with names like \"low,\" \"medium,\" and \"high.\"\n\n```mermaid\nflowchart LR\n    A[\"Gut Feeling\\n(initial preference)\"] --> B[\"Choose Reach\\n(estimate)\"] --> C[\"Choose Impact\\n(multiplier)\"] --> D[\"Choose Confidence\\n(a guess about the estimate)\"] --> E[\"Calculate RICE Score\"] --> F[\"Confirm initial preference\"]\n    style A fill:#fef3c7\n    style F fill:#fef3c7\n    style E fill:#d1fae5\n```\n\nConfidence is the most honest-looking and least honest number in the formula. It's described as the probability that Reach and Impact are accurate. In reality, nobody has that data. If a team had enough evidence to calculate confidence empirically, they probably wouldn't need a prioritization framework; they'd just build the thing with the best evidence.\n\nEffort is the only variable with some grounding: engineering teams have actual estimates, though those are often optimistic by a factor of two or three.\n\n## Why the Framework Still Gets Used\n\nFrameworks persist because they solve a real problem, just not the one they appear to solve.\n\nPrioritization is a political problem as much as a technical one. When two teams disagree about what to build, and both teams have executives backing them, the decision needs a process both sides can accept. A scoring matrix gives everyone a way to participate without directly attacking the other team's priorities. It externalizes the decision.\n\nThis is not nothing. Getting a room of stakeholders to agree on criteria, even loosely, is better than letting whoever shouts loudest win. The framework creates a conversation structure where none existed.\n\nThe problem comes when teams mistake the output for analysis. They present RICE scores as if they derived from data. They use them to justify decisions to leadership without disclosing that the inputs are estimates stacked on estimates.\n\n## The Precision Is Fake\n\nA RICE score of 42.3 vs. 38.7 is not meaningful. Neither is a ratio of 1.09. The inputs have error bars wide enough to swap the rankings without changing any real-world fact. But the decimal points make the numbers look precise, and precise numbers feel like evidence.\n\nThis produces two failure modes:\n\n**False confidence:** The team builds the top-ranked item believing it was rigorously selected. Nobody revisits the assumptions. When the feature underperforms, the framework takes no blame because it produced a number, not a prediction.\n\n**Gaming the framework:** When teams realize that the framework produces whatever the inputs dictate, they start reverse-engineering the desired answer. The inputs get adjusted to reach the right output. This is worse than not using the framework at all, because it adds a layer of false legitimacy.\n\n```\nCommon input manipulation patterns:\n\nReach    → rounded up to nearest order of magnitude\nImpact   → chosen from \"high\" because the feature is exciting\nConfidence → set to 80% because that feels confident\nEffort   → estimated in story points, then converted to weeks optimistically\n\nResult: every major initiative scores similarly; tiebreaking happens in negotiation\n```\n\n## What Frameworks Actually Measure\n\nWhen a team fills out a scoring sheet honestly, the scores mostly reflect two things: how excited the team is about an idea, and how recently the idea was discussed. Freshness bias is real. An idea that came up in last week's planning meeting has vivid details and strong advocates. An idea from six months ago has lost its sponsor.\n\nFrameworks amplify the current state of advocacy, not the underlying merit of ideas.\n\n## Using Frameworks Without Being Fooled by Them\n\nNone of this means scoring frameworks are useless. They can serve a few legitimate purposes:\n\n**Forcing comparability.** Making everyone answer the same questions about every candidate forces advocates to think explicitly about Reach or Effort they might otherwise ignore. The act of completing the template surfaces assumptions.\n\n**Flagging outliers.** A feature that scores extremely low on every dimension probably shouldn't be in the top tier. A feature with a single extreme score (massive effort, low reach) is worth scrutinizing. Frameworks catch obvious mistakes, not marginal ones.\n\n**Creating a shared vocabulary.** Agreeing that \"high reach\" means at least 50,000 users per month gives the team a common reference point. This is useful independent of whether the final RICE number is meaningful.\n\nThe key is treating framework outputs as conversation starters, not decisions. When a score comes back surprising, the right response is to question the inputs, not to accept the output. When scores cluster tightly, the right response is to acknowledge that the framework can't distinguish between them and to make the call on other grounds.\n\nFrameworks reduce the search space. They don't replace judgment. Using them as if they do is where teams go wrong.\n\n## What to Do Instead\n\nThe most honest approach is to separate two questions: what do we believe about this opportunity, and what do we believe about this estimate.\n\nState the assumptions explicitly. Write down the Reach estimate and the evidence for it. Write down the confidence level and what would change it. Then make the call, and acknowledge that the call is based on judgment informed by (not derived from) the analysis.\n\nThis is more uncomfortable than a spreadsheet. It requires someone to own the decision without hiding behind a formula. That discomfort is appropriate. Prioritization is a judgment call. The goal is to make better judgment calls, not to make judgment calls that look like they aren't judgment calls."}
