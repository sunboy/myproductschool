{"hook_text": "RICE Confidence is the number that controls the outcome. A 20% confidence estimate drops a high-reach idea from priority 1 to priority 5, and almost nobody has real data to set it accurately.", "body_mdx": "## The Variable That Swings Everything\n\nRICE is (Reach × Impact × Confidence) / Effort. When you work through the formula, Confidence acts as a straight multiplier on everything above the line. Set Confidence to 80% instead of 40% and the score doubles. That's not a small adjustment. That's the difference between building something now and putting it in the backlog for a year.\n\nMost RICE tutorials spend a paragraph on Confidence. They describe it as \"how sure are you of the estimates above?\" and suggest ranges like 50% for rough estimates, 80% for fairly confident, and 100% for \"sure things.\" Then they move on. The implicit message is that Confidence is a minor calibration knob.\n\nIt isn't. It's the dominant variable, and almost nobody has principled criteria for setting it.\n\n## What Confidence Is Supposed to Capture\n\nIn the original Intercom formulation, Confidence captures the degree of certainty in the Reach and Impact estimates. If a team is highly confident in both numbers, Confidence is high. If the estimates are speculative, Confidence is low.\n\nThis framing requires that teams have a prior belief about how accurate their estimates are, which requires them to track how accurate past estimates were, which almost no team does systematically. So Confidence becomes a vibes score dressed up as a probability.\n\n```mermaid\nflowchart TD\n    A[\"Estimate Reach\\n(users/month)\"] --> D\n    B[\"Estimate Impact\\n(0.25 to 3.0)\"] --> D\n    C[\"Estimate Confidence\\n(THIS controls the score)\"] --> D\n    D[\"Multiply A × B × C / Effort\"] --> E[\"RICE Score\"]\n    C --> F{\"How is Confidence set?\"}\n    F --> G[\"Gut feeling about\\nhow confident we feel\"]\n    F --> H[\"Reverse-engineered to\\nmatch desired ranking\"]\n    F --> I[\"Copied from a previous\\nproject without data\"]\n    style C fill:#fca5a5\n    style F fill:#fca5a5\n```\n\n## Three Ways Confidence Gets Corrupted\n\n**The anchoring problem.** Teams set Confidence after discussing Reach and Impact. By that point, they've already spent twenty minutes building the case for the feature. Anchoring to a high Confidence feels natural because the team is already bought in. A neutral observer assessing only the evidence might land at 40%. The team lands at 80%.\n\n**The type confusion problem.** Confidence in the estimate and confidence in the feature's value are different things, but teams conflate them constantly. \"I'm pretty confident this will work\" is a statement about feature quality. \"I'm 70% confident that 50,000 people per month will use this\" is a statement about an estimate. Both get collapsed into the same number.\n\n**The status quo problem.** High Confidence is rewarded in the formula. This creates an incentive to claim high confidence for projects with strong advocates and low confidence for projects that don't have a champion. The Confidence estimate becomes a proxy for internal politics.\n\n## A Real Example of Confidence Range Effects\n\nAssume a feature with Reach = 80,000 users, Impact = 2.0 (high), and Effort = 4 person-weeks.\n\n```\nImpact score (before effort) = 80,000 × 2.0 = 160,000\n\nConfidence 20% → RICE = (160,000 × 0.20) / 4 = 8,000\nConfidence 50% → RICE = (160,000 × 0.50) / 4 = 20,000\nConfidence 80% → RICE = (160,000 × 0.80) / 4 = 32,000\nConfidence 100% → RICE = (160,000 × 1.00) / 4 = 40,000\n```\n\nThe RICE score varies by a factor of 5 across the plausible range of Confidence. Meanwhile, a smaller feature with half the Reach but set to 80% Confidence beats the large feature set to 40% Confidence. The Confidence estimate, not the underlying opportunity, determines the ranking.\n\n## What Calibrated Confidence Actually Looks Like\n\nCalibrated probability estimates require feedback loops. A team that tracks its Reach estimates against actual outcomes, quarter over quarter, can start to say things like \"our Reach estimates tend to be 2x optimistic, so we discount by 50%.\" This is real calibration.\n\nWithout that data, the closest honest approach is to distinguish between evidence types:\n\n- **Validated by user research or A/B test data:** This can support a higher Confidence, maybe 70-80%, because there's direct evidence.\n- **Inferred from usage patterns or funnel data:** Moderate Confidence, 40-60%. The evidence is indirect but traceable.\n- **Strategic intuition or stakeholder conviction:** Low Confidence, 20-40%. The team believes in the idea but the evidence is weak.\n- **\"We haven't looked yet but it seems big\":** Very low Confidence, 10-20%. This is guessing with optimistic framing.\n\nMost teams set the majority of their features at 80% by default. By the calibration table above, 80% should be rare.\n\n## The Effort Denominator Has Its Own Problems\n\nEffort is the one variable in RICE that feels most grounded because it comes from engineering estimates. But engineering estimates have systematic biases that compound with Confidence errors.\n\nFamous results from software estimation research: projects estimated at 10 days take an average of 18-25 days when controlling for scope creep. Feature estimates made before design is complete underestimate by 30-60%. Reference class forecasting (comparing to similar past projects) consistently outperforms bottom-up estimation.\n\nWhen Confidence is overstated and Effort is understated, the numerator is inflated and the denominator is deflated. The RICE score can be 3-4x higher than a calibrated estimate would produce. That's not a scoring artifact. That's the dominant source of roadmap failure.\n\n## Making RICE Less Wrong\n\nFew teams are going to replace RICE with a calibrated forecasting system. But a few practices make RICE less misleading:\n\n**Set Confidence last and explicitly justify it.** Before assigning a Confidence value, the facilitator should ask: \"What evidence do we have for the Reach and Impact estimates?\" If the honest answer is \"intuition,\" the Confidence should be 30% or lower.\n\n**Use a constrained vocabulary with definitions.** Pre-define what 20%, 50%, and 80% mean in terms of evidence type. Post it on the wall. Apply it consistently.\n\n**Run a pre-mortem on the top-ranked items.** After scoring, pick the top 3 features and ask: \"What would have to be true for this to score incorrectly?\" This surfaces Confidence errors before they become roadmap commitments.\n\n**Track and review.** Once a quarter, pull the estimates from three months ago and compare against actuals. Where were Reach estimates off? What does that imply for current Confidence values? Without this loop, Confidence never improves.\n\nRICE is a reasonable structure for structured comparison. Confidence is where it goes wrong, and Confidence is where most teams spend the least time. Fixing that doesn't require abandoning the framework. It requires treating the formula's most powerful variable with the skepticism it deserves."}
