  "body_mdx": "## The Decision That Keeps Getting Made Twice\n\nEvery engineering organization eventually discovers that build vs. buy is not a one-time decision. It's a recurring conversation with compounding consequences. The team that builds a custom auth system in 2022 will be defending it in 2025 when a dedicated auth vendor ships a feature they need in two days instead of two sprints.\n\nThe framework is simple to state and hard to apply: build when the capability is core to your competitive differentiation, buy when it's commodity, use open source when you need control over the software without absorbing the full build cost. The difficulty is in being honest about which category a given capability actually belongs to.\n\n## Differentiation vs. Commodity\n\nThe first question is whether the capability you're considering will make your product better in a way that matters to users. Not better in an abstract engineering sense. Better in a way that users notice, that drives retention, that represents something a competitor can't easily copy.\n\nPayment processing is not differentiation for most companies. It's commodity infrastructure. Stripe figured this out by watching companies burn months building payment integrations that looked the same as everyone else's. The companies that built custom payment systems spent engineering time that didn't produce user-visible improvement. They also inherited a compliance surface area they were poorly equipped to maintain.\n\nContrast that with Figma building a custom WebGL rendering engine. Browser-based collaborative design tools in 2012 were slow. Figma's bet was that real-time multiplayer editing at acceptable performance required custom graphics infrastructure. That wasn't commodity. It was the product. Building it was the only path to the differentiating experience.\n\nThe test: if a competitor bought the same vendor you're evaluating, would you be at a disadvantage? If the answer is no, it's commodity. Buy it.\n\n## The True Cost of Building\n\nEngineers systematically underestimate build cost because they count development time and ignore everything after launch.\n\n```\ntrue_build_cost = initial_development\n                + ongoing_maintenance (typically 15-20% of build cost per year)\n                + opportunity_cost (features not built while this was)\n                + integration_debt (connecting custom system to everything else)\n                + knowledge_concentration_risk (what happens when the author leaves)\n```\n\nThe maintenance line is where most estimates fail. A custom notification system built in three months doesn't cost three months. It costs three months plus six weeks per year in perpetuity, plus the cognitive load of whoever owns it, plus the migration effort when you eventually replace it.\n\nThe opportunity cost line is where product-minded engineers catch something others miss. Every sprint your team spends building internal tooling is a sprint not spent on the user-facing product. At a small company, that trade is often fatal. The feature you didn't build because your team was building infrastructure was the feature users would have paid for.\n\n## Open Source: The Third Path\n\nOpen source occupies a distinct position in the decision: it gives you control over the software without the full cost of authoring it from scratch, but it transfers maintenance responsibility to you in a different form.\n\nThe calculus changes based on maturity and community size.\n\n```mermaid\nflowchart TD\n    A[\"Capability needed\"] --> B{\"Core differentiator?\"}\n    B -->|\"Yes\"| C[\"Build\"]\n    B -->|\"No\"| D{\"Commodity vendor exists?\"}\n    D -->|\"Yes\"| E{\"Acceptable price + lock-in?\"}\n    E -->|\"Yes\"| F[\"Buy\"]\n    E -->|\"No\"| G{\"Mature OSS with active community?\"}\n    D -->|\"No\"| G\n    G -->|\"Yes\"| H[\"Use open source\"]\n    G -->|\"No\"| I{\"Build cost justified?\"}\n    I -->|\"Yes\"| C\n    I -->|\"No\"| J[\"Defer or descope\"]\n```\n\nPostgres is mature, has a large community, and gets security patches fast. Running Postgres is a reasonable choice even for small teams, though you're absorbing operational complexity. Running a niche open source message queue with three contributors and two-year-old commits is a different calculation. You get control but also full ownership of every bug, every security vulnerability, every performance regression.\n\nThe questions for open source specifically: How active is the maintainer community? What happens if the project is abandoned? Can your team read and contribute to the codebase if needed? Is the license compatible with your commercial constraints?\n\n## When the Decision Gets Revisited\n\nThe decision made at ten engineers rarely holds at a hundred. This is not a failure of the original decision. It's a consequence of context changing.\n\nAt ten engineers, building a custom analytics pipeline made sense because the team had specific data needs and no vendor offered quite the right product. At a hundred engineers, the custom pipeline is now owned by a team of two who can't take vacation at the same time, and there are three commercial products that would cover 90% of the use case at a fraction of the maintenance burden.\n\nThe trigger for revisiting is usually one of four things: the vendor market improved (commodity solutions now exist for what you built), team composition changed (original authors left), maintenance cost exceeded vendor cost, or the capability drifted from the core product as the business evolved.\n\nStripe's developer tooling team has a documented heuristic for this: if a vendor can solve the problem within one order of magnitude of the cost of building it, and it's not differentiating, buy it. The order-of-magnitude buffer accounts for the real costs that don't show up in the initial estimate.\n\n## The Vendor Lock-in Trap\n\nThe buy-side failure mode is deeper integration than the decision warranted. Lock-in is not inherently bad. Lock-in is bad when it constrains decisions you'll need to make later.\n\nA vendor managing your email sending infrastructure creates mild lock-in: you could switch, it would cost a few sprints, but it's not existential. A vendor owning your core data model creates severe lock-in: the switching cost is a multi-quarter migration that may never happen.\n\nThe lock-in test: if this vendor doubled their price tomorrow, what would the migration cost? If the answer is \"more than two quarters of engineering,\" you've accepted deep lock-in. That's not automatically wrong, but it should be explicit. The teams that get into trouble are the ones who accepted deep lock-in without realizing it.\n\nOpen source is sometimes chosen specifically to avoid vendor lock-in, but it substitutes one form of dependency for another. You're not locked into a vendor's pricing, but you're locked into the software's design decisions, which can be harder to escape than a contract.\n\n## Applying the Framework\n\nA useful practice: before any significant build decision, write down what would have to be true for this to be the right choice. Then check, six months later, whether those conditions held.\n\nFor build: this capability must be core to what users pay for, no vendor offers an acceptable version, and the team that builds it will own it long-term.\n\nFor buy: the problem is solved, the pricing is sustainable at scale, the lock-in is understood and acceptable, and the integration cost is counted.\n\nFor open source: the project is mature, the community is active, the license is compatible, and the team can contribute to or fork the codebase if needed.\n\nThe framework doesn't make the decision for you. It structures the reasoning so the decision is made explicitly rather than by default."
