  "body_mdx": "## The Wrong Frame\n\nMonolith vs. microservices is usually debated as a technical question: which architecture produces better performance, better scalability, better fault isolation? These are real considerations, but they're secondary to the question that actually determines whether the decision works out: what does your team look like, and how do teams need to coordinate?\n\nConway's Law is forty years old and still more predictive of system architecture than any technical framework: organizations design systems that mirror their communication structure. A team organized around a shared codebase will tend toward a monolith. Teams organized around bounded domains will tend toward services. The causality runs both ways, but the team structure is usually more durable than the architecture decision.\n\nThe teams that have the most trouble with microservices are the ones that adopted the architecture before their organization was structured to operate it. The teams that have the most trouble with monoliths are the ones whose organization grew past the point where a shared codebase enables coordination.\n\n## What Microservices Actually Solve\n\nMicroservices address a specific set of organizational and operational problems:\n\n**Independent deployment.** When fifty engineers need to ship code, coordinating a single deployment pipeline becomes a bottleneck. Services let teams deploy independently. The value is proportional to team count. For three engineers, independent deployment solves no problem they have.\n\n**Technology heterogeneity.** Different problems are sometimes better served by different technology stacks. A recommendation engine might benefit from Python's ML ecosystem while the API layer stays in Go. Microservices allow this where a monolith requires a single-stack commitment.\n\n**Isolated failure domains.** A service that fails takes down its consumers, not the entire application. This is real fault isolation, and it matters at scale. It also introduces new failure modes: network partitions, service degradation cascades, and distributed tracing complexity that a monolith doesn't have.\n\n**Team autonomy.** Ownership is clearer when the service boundary maps to a team boundary. The payments team owns the payments service. They make the technical decisions, set their own deployment cadence, and are accountable for the service's behavior.\n\nNotice that none of these benefits are primarily technical. They're organizational benefits that produce technical consequences. The failure to notice this is why \"we want to scale\" is a bad reason to adopt microservices.\n\n## What Microservices Cost\n\n```mermaid\nflowchart TD\n    subgraph Monolith\n        A[\"Team makes a change\"] --> B[\"Single test suite\"] --> C[\"Single deployment\"] --> D[\"Done\"]\n    end\n    subgraph Microservices\n        E[\"Team makes a change\"] --> F[\"Service test suite\"]\n        F --> G[\"Contract tests with\\ndependent services\"]\n        G --> H[\"Service deployment\"]\n        H --> I[\"Monitor distributed\\ntracing for downstream effects\"]\n        I --> J[\"Done\"]\n    end\n    style Monolith fill:#0d2e2e,color:#14b8a6\n    style Microservices fill:#1e293b,color:#94a3b8\n```\n\nThe operational overhead of microservices is substantial and scales with service count. You need service discovery. You need a distributed tracing layer. You need a way to manage secrets across services. You need a deployment orchestration system (Kubernetes is the industry default, and it has a steep learning curve). You need runbooks for the failure modes that don't exist in a monolith: a service that's up but degraded, a dependency that's timing out intermittently, a network partition between services in the same data center.\n\nSmall teams absorb this overhead slowly. At ten engineers, two people end up owning the infrastructure complexity, and they become a bottleneck for everything else. At a hundred engineers with dedicated platform and SRE teams, the overhead is distributed and the independence benefits are real.\n\nAmazon's famous \"two-pizza teams\" heuristic was a team-size guideline before it was an architecture guideline. Bezos's 2002 services mandate was issued to an organization that had hundreds of engineers working in the same codebase with coordination problems that were visibly slowing the company. The mandate was the right call for that organization at that size. It would have been premature at fifty people.\n\n## The Monolith Is Not a Failure State\n\nShopify runs most of its commerce platform on a Ruby on Rails monolith with millions of transactions per day. They've invested heavily in making that monolith modular and fast, but they haven't fragmented it into hundreds of services. The reasoning is explicit: their team structure benefits from shared code and conventions, their deployment reliability is high, and the coordination cost of a fragmented service mesh would exceed the coordination cost of shared code.\n\nStack Overflow runs on a monolith serving millions of developers. Their argument is operational: a well-optimized monolith can handle enormous scale without the distributed systems complexity that microservices introduce.\n\nBoth organizations have made a deliberate choice based on team structure and operational capability, not trend or aspiration. The monolith is a legitimate architecture at any scale when the team structure and operational context support it.\n\n## Team Topologies as the Decision Frame\n\nTeam Topologies, the framework from Skelton and Pais, offers a more precise way to think about this than raw team size. The decision heuristic is:\n\nIf teams are **stream-aligned** (each focused on a user-facing value stream) and need to move independently, services with clear ownership boundaries enable that independence. The service mesh mirrors the org chart.\n\nIf teams are in a **complicated subsystem** model (a few specialized teams serving many others), those subsystems may naturally become services. A data platform team, an ML inference team.\n\nIf the team is small enough that the coordination cost of a shared codebase is lower than the operational cost of distributed services, stay in the monolith. Add modularity without distribution.\n\nThe inverse Conway maneuver is the practical application: design your organization to produce the system architecture you want, then let the architecture follow naturally. Teams reorganized into bounded domain ownership will naturally produce service boundaries. Teams reorganized around shared infrastructure will naturally produce monolithic layers.\n\n## When to Move From Monolith to Services\n\nThe signals that a monolith is becoming a coordination problem:\n\n**Deployment bottlenecks.** Releases are coordinated across many teams, and the coordination cost is measurable. Teams are blocked on each other.\n\n**Ownership ambiguity.** No team feels clear ownership over large parts of the codebase. Changes in one area break things in areas that feel unrelated.\n\n**Scaling mismatch.** Specific parts of the system need to scale independently at resources the rest of the system doesn't need. A monolith that needs to scale its image processing at 10x the rate of its API layer is a candidate for extraction.\n\n**Team size crossed a threshold.** Somewhere between fifty and a hundred engineers working in a shared codebase, the coordination overhead of shared deployment and shared conventions starts costing more than the operational simplicity saves.\n\nExtraction works better than greenfield rewrites. Identify the seam that has the clearest ownership boundary and the most pressing coordination problem. Extract that service. Learn the operational overhead. Decide whether the next extraction is worth it based on real experience, not projection.\n\nThe architecture decision is a team decision wearing technical clothes. Get the team structure right first, and the architecture follows."
