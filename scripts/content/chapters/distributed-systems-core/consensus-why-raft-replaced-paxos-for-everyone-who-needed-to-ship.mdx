  "body_mdx": "## The Problem Paxos Never Solved\n\nLeslie Lamport published \"The Part-Time Parliament\" in 1989. It took nine years for the paper to get accepted because reviewers found it too whimsical. When it finally ran in 1998, engineers tried to implement it and discovered the real problem: the algorithm is concise on paper and genuinely difficult to translate into correct code.\n\nPaxos describes how a set of acceptors, proposers, and learners reach agreement on a single value. It says almost nothing about how to build a replicated log, handle leader changes, or recover from partial failures. Those gaps are where real systems live, and every team filled them differently. Google's Chubby team, who built the lock service that eventually inspired etcd, documented their experience: the algorithm required thousands of lines of C++ and took years to get right.\n\nDiego Ongaro and John Ousterhout read that paper and asked a pointed question. What if understandability was a first-class design goal?\n\n## What Raft Actually Does Differently\n\nRaft divides the consensus problem into three separable subproblems: leader election, log replication, and safety. You can understand each piece independently. Paxos does not decompose this way; the components are deeply entangled.\n\nThe leader election protocol works like this. Every node starts as a follower. If a follower does not hear from a leader within the election timeout window (randomized between 150-300ms to prevent simultaneous elections), it becomes a candidate, increments its term, and requests votes. A candidate wins if it receives votes from a majority. The majority requirement is the key invariant: only one leader can exist per term.\n\n![Consensus: Why Raft Replaced Paxos for Everyone Who Needed to Ship](/content-images/distributed-systems-core/consensus-why-raft-replaced-paxos-for-everyone-who-needed-to-ship.png)\n\nLog replication follows a strict append-only discipline. The leader receives a client write, appends it to its local log, and sends AppendEntries RPCs to all followers. Once a majority acknowledges, the entry is committed. The leader then applies it to its state machine and tells followers to do the same. Log entries pass through three states: Appended, Replicated (majority confirmed), Committed. The write-ahead approach means committed entries are durable even if the leader crashes immediately after committing.\n\nThe quorum math is simple: to tolerate f failures, you need 2f+1 nodes. A three-node cluster handles one failure; a five-node cluster handles two. etcd defaults to a 100ms heartbeat interval and a 1000ms election timeout, which Raft's paper specifies as a 10x ratio, enough headroom to survive network hiccups without triggering false elections.\n\n## Who Actually Ships Raft\n\nThe etcd Raft library (go.etcd.io/etcd/raft) is the most widely deployed Raft implementation in production. It powers etcd itself, which stores all of Kubernetes cluster state: every ConfigMap, Deployment, Pod, and Service record passes through it. When etcd is unavailable, Kubernetes is completely unavailable. This is not a theoretical dependency.\n\nCockroachDB took a different approach to scale. Rather than a single monolithic Raft group like etcd, each data range gets its own consensus group. A single CockroachDB node participates in hundreds of thousands of Raft groups simultaneously. Each group has three or five members distributed across fault domains. The paper on scaling Raft (CockroachLabs, 2023) describes this as the key architectural decision that allowed horizontal sharding without giving up strong consistency per range.\n\nHashiCorp Consul uses Raft for distributed state with a single leader. Split-brain protection works the same way as Kubernetes: if a network partition divides the cluster, nodes in the minority partition cannot elect a leader and refuse writes. This is the correct behavior. The minority partition going read-only is not a bug; it is the consistency guarantee working as designed.\n\n## The Operational Realities\n\nLog compaction is the operational problem Raft papers underemphasize. A cluster processing 100 log entries per second accumulates 3.15 billion entries annually, roughly 315 GB at 100 bytes per entry. Without snapshotting, followers joining late or falling behind must replay the entire log. Raft solves this with periodic snapshots of the state machine, after which the log prefix can be discarded. The implementation challenge is that snapshot serialization, network transfer, and disk I/O can spike under load, and lagging followers sometimes struggle to install one snapshot before the next one starts.\n\nThe election timeout parameter is the tuning knob most operators get wrong. Set it too low and the cluster degrades into constant elections under load (Raft calls this a \"disruption\" scenario). Set it too high and recovery after a real leader failure takes 10+ seconds of unavailability. The Raft paper's guidance is broadcastTime * 10 as a minimum. Research from 2024 (Dynatune, BALLAST) explores adapting the timeout dynamically based on real-time RTT measurements, which reduces false positives in variable-latency environments.\n\nOne fault model boundary engineers regularly miss: Raft requires 3f+1 nodes to tolerate f Byzantine failures, not 2f+1. Raft is a crash fault-tolerant protocol. It assumes honest nodes. If you expect compromised replicas (blockchain, federated systems, adversarial environments), you need PBFT or a similar Byzantine-tolerant protocol. For internal infrastructure behind your firewall, the crash fault model is the right one.\n\n## What Paxos Is Still Good For\n\nGoogle Spanner uses Paxos for inter-datacenter replication across five or more regions. It provides strong consistency with readable replicas at latencies around 10ms for reads and higher for commits. Azure Storage also uses Paxos. Both choices predate Raft's publication and both teams have deep expertise in distributed systems implementation. For organizations without that expertise or that timeline, Raft is the correct default.\n\nThe Ongaro user study is worth taking seriously. After learning both algorithms, 33 of 43 students answered more Raft questions correctly than Paxos questions. That is 77% versus roughly 23%. For most engineering teams, algorithm correctness in production tracks closely with how well the team understood what they were implementing.\n\nThe practical answer on algorithm selection: if you are building on etcd, CockroachDB, or Consul, you are already using Raft. If you are implementing consensus from scratch, Raft's separation of concerns makes it the one you can actually get right."
