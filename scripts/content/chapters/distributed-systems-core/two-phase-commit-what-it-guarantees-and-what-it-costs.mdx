  "body_mdx": "## The Protocol Is Simple. The Failure Mode Is Not.\n\nTwo-phase commit solves a real problem: how do you atomically update records across multiple databases or services? The protocol has two rounds. In the Prepare phase, the coordinator asks every participant \"can you commit?\" Each participant writes the changes to a durable log, acquires locks, and replies yes or no. If all say yes, the coordinator sends Commit in phase two and participants make the changes permanent. If any say no, the coordinator sends Rollback.\n\n![Two-Phase Commit: What It Guarantees and What It Costs](/content-images/distributed-systems-core/two-phase-commit-what-it-guarantees-and-what-it-costs.png)\n\nOn paper this is tight. In practice, one failure scenario breaks the guarantee: the coordinator crashes after participants vote yes but before the Commit message arrives.\n\nNow every participant is stuck. They've voted yes, written their changes to disk, acquired locks, and are waiting for a decision. They cannot unilaterally commit (what if another participant voted no?). They cannot unilaterally rollback (what if the coordinator already sent Commit to someone else before crashing?). They sit and wait, holding locks, blocking every other operation that touches those rows.\n\nThis is not a design flaw in a particular implementation. The University of Washington formalized this as a theorem in 1985 with the FLP impossibility result: no deterministic consensus algorithm can guarantee termination in an asynchronous system with even one faulty process. 2PC hits this wall directly. You cannot simultaneously have atomicity, partition tolerance, and progress. Pick two.\n\n## What the Coordinator Failure Actually Looks Like\n\nThink of 2PC like a bank transaction requiring sign-off from a central compliance officer. Tellers across five branches have each frozen a customer's account pending confirmation. If the compliance officer disappears mid-approval, every teller is stuck holding frozen accounts until the officer comes back or the bank manually resolves the situation. No teller can proceed safely alone.\n\nIn production, this manifests as lock contention. The participants hold row-level or table-level locks until the coordinator recovers. If the coordinator is down for minutes, those locks block every read and write against the affected records. If it's down for hours, you have an incident. The Azure East US2 networking configuration loss in 2025 lasted 50 hours and left dependent services unable to make forward progress because the coordination layer was unavailable.\n\nThe common workaround is to time out and rollback if the coordinator doesn't respond. But this introduces a new problem: if the coordinator recovered and sent Commit to some participants before you rolled back, you now have an inconsistency. You've traded blocking for a split-brain.\n\n## Where 2PC Is Still the Right Tool\n\nNone of this means 2PC is wrong. It means it's a specific tool with a specific cost.\n\n2PC works well when:\n\n- The coordinator and participants are in the same failure domain (same data center, same managed database cluster)\n- The transaction window is short (milliseconds, not seconds)\n- You genuinely need atomicity across systems you control\n\nPostgreSQL's distributed extensions, MySQL's XA transactions, and most RDBMS-based saga coordinators use 2PC internally for cross-shard commits. In these systems, coordinator failure is rare and coordinator recovery is fast. The blocking window is measured in seconds, not hours.\n\nThe problem arises when engineers reach for 2PC across service boundaries over a network they don't control. Transactions crossing partitions in distributed databases already show much higher abort rates and unpredictable performance compared to single-partition transactions. Add an unreliable coordinator and the cost compounds.\n\n## The Alternatives and What They Give Up\n\nThe alternatives to 2PC each buy availability by accepting weaker consistency.\n\n**Saga pattern** breaks a distributed transaction into a sequence of local transactions, each with a compensating rollback transaction. A payment service commits its change, then an inventory service commits its change. If inventory fails, the payment service runs its compensation transaction. You get eventual consistency with no blocking. The cost: compensating transactions are hard to write correctly, especially when the failure happens mid-saga. You also lose isolation: partial state is visible to other requests while the saga is in flight.\n\n**Outbox pattern** writes the side effect (a message, a downstream call) to a local table in the same transaction as the primary change, then a background process delivers it. No distributed coordination at all. Kafka's exactly-once semantics uses a version of this: idempotent producers (default in Kafka 3.0+) maintain a sequence number per (producer, topic, partition) tuple at the broker, so retries are deduplicated without a coordinator. The cost is that delivery is at-least-once with deduplication, not truly atomic.\n\n**Three-phase commit** adds a pre-commit phase to eliminate the blocking window. In theory, participants can reach a decision even if the coordinator fails. In practice, it's sensitive to network partitions in ways that make it worse in real deployments. It's rarely used outside of academic contexts.\n\n## The Decision You Actually Have to Make\n\nThe honest question when designing a distributed write is: what is the actual failure rate of my coordinator, and how long does recovery take?\n\nIf your coordinator is a managed PostgreSQL primary with automated failover and the replica can be promoted in under 10 seconds, 2PC's blocking window is tolerable for most use cases. If your coordinator is a service running in Kubernetes and restarts take 45 seconds, that's a 45-second lock hold time on your database rows during every coordinator crash. That's not a theoretical problem.\n\nAt cloud scale, cross-zone packet loss runs 0.1-1%, turning a 1ms hop into 200ms+ with exponential backoff. Every 2PC prepare round is a synchronous network call. Model your worst-case latency before you commit to the protocol.\n\nThe practical guidance: use 2PC within a single database cluster where the coordinator is the database primary itself. For cross-service writes, use the outbox pattern with idempotent consumers. For anything requiring strong consistency across independent services over a WAN, accept that you're fighting a theorem and design compensating logic instead.\n\n2PC doesn't fail because engineers implement it wrong. It fails because the environments engineers deploy it into regularly violate its assumptions."
