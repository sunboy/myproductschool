Contamination is common, rarely disclosed, and almost never correctable after the fact.", "body_mdx": "## The Benchmark Industrial Complex\n\nA model scores 90% on MMLU and the press release calls it \"superhuman.\" What the press release doesn't mention: the training data included web crawls of test prep sites, Reddit threads with answer keys, and GitHub repos containing the benchmark itself. The model memorized answers. It didn't learn anything.\n\nThis is dataset contamination, and it affects nearly every major benchmark. The problem isn't that researchers are dishonest. It's that contamination is structurally hard to prevent at scale and almost impossible to audit retroactively.\n\n## What Contamination Actually Looks Like\n\nContamination happens when examples from a benchmark appear, in any form, in the pretraining or fine-tuning corpus. Common vectors:\n\n- **Verbatim overlap**: The exact question and answer appear in a crawled webpage.\n- **Paraphrase overlap**: The question is reworded but the answer choices are identical.\n- **Answer key leakage**: A blog post, forum thread, or textbook solution manual contains the correct answers.\n- **Few-shot contamination**: Evaluation prompts are included as few-shot examples in fine-tuning.\n\nGPT-4's technical report acknowledged \"potential contamination\" on several benchmarks without quantifying it. Most reports don't even go that far.\n\n## Saturation: The Other Problem\n\nEven without contamination, benchmarks expire. Once enough models score above 90% on a benchmark, it stops differentiating between them. The test is too easy for the frontier.\n\nMMEBench tracked this pattern: MMLU went from frontier-relevant to saturated within two years of GPT-4's release. HellaSwag saturated even faster.\n\n```\nBenchmark lifecycle:\n\n  t=0: Benchmark published, frontier models score ~70%\n  t=6m: State of the art climbs to 80%, training data includes\n         benchmark-adjacent content\n  t=18m: Top models cluster at 88-92%, differences unmeasurable\n  t=24m: New benchmark needed; old scores still quoted in press\n```\n\nSaturation and contamination often accelerate each other. As models improve on a benchmark, more training data targeting that benchmark enters circulation, which inflates scores further.\n\n## How to Detect Contamination\n\nSeveral methods exist, none of them perfect.\n\n**N-gram overlap analysis**: Compute token n-gram overlap between training data and benchmark examples. A 13-gram overlap rate above 1% is a signal. The challenge: most labs don't release training data.\n\n**Membership inference attacks**: Given a model and a candidate example, can you determine whether the example was in training? This works probabilistically but requires model access (logits, not just completions).\n\n**Canary injection**: Before training, insert synthetic \"canary\" examples into the training set with known answers. If the model gets canaries right at suspiciously high rates, contamination pathways are likely.\n\n**Performance variance analysis**: If a model performs dramatically better on one benchmark vs. structurally similar benchmarks, contamination is a plausible explanation.\n\n```mermaid\nflowchart LR\n    A[Training Corpus] --> B{N-gram Scan}\n    B -->|High overlap| C[Contamination Suspected]\n    B -->|Low overlap| D[Proceed with Caution]\n    C --> E[Membership Inference]\n    E -->|Confirmed| F[Discard Benchmark Results]\n    E -->|Inconclusive| G[Flag in Report]\n    D --> H[Saturation Check]\n    H -->|All models > 85%| I[Benchmark Retired]\n    H -->|Spread exists| J[Benchmark Still Useful]\n```\n\n## What \"Held-Out\" Actually Means\n\nMany benchmark papers describe their test sets as \"held out\" from training. This is true at the time of publication. It stops being true the moment the benchmark is published on the web and gets crawled.\n\nBenchmarks created before 2022 have been on the internet long enough to appear in Common Crawl snapshots used to train most major models. \"Held out\" in the paper means nothing for models trained after publication.\n\nSome recent benchmarks use dynamic evaluation: new examples are generated on demand, preventing any static set from leaking. LiveBench and DynaBench take this approach. They're harder to game but also harder to reproduce.\n\n## The Calibration Gap\n\nEven legitimate benchmark scores don't tell you what you actually need to know: will this model perform well on my task?\n\nMMELU covers 57 subjects. Unless you're building a general-purpose quiz app, most of those subjects are irrelevant. A model that dominates on STEM categories but struggles on legal reasoning looks excellent in aggregate. Your contract analysis use case will tell a different story.\n\nThe right response is not to ignore benchmarks entirely. It's to treat them as weak priors that need to be updated with task-specific evaluation. Use benchmarks to eliminate obviously weak candidates. Then run your own evals on the candidates that remain.\n\n## Practical Rules for Benchmark Interpretation\n\nWhen reading a benchmark result, ask:\n\n1. When was the benchmark published? If it predates the model's training cutoff, contamination is possible.\n2. What fraction of the training data is known? If the answer is \"we don't know,\" contamination cannot be ruled out.\n3. What's the score distribution? If all frontier models cluster within 2 points, the benchmark is saturated and the differences are noise.\n4. Is the benchmark task-adjacent to your use case? If not, the score tells you little about your problem.\n5. Did the authors perform contamination analysis? If not, treat the score as an upper bound.\n\nBenchmarks are useful signal in a world where training data is fully transparent and benchmarks are held out with cryptographic guarantees. We don't live in that world. Treat them accordingly."}
