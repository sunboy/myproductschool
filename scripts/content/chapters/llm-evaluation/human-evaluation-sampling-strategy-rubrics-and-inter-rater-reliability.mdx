Ambiguous rubrics produce noisy training signal, and noisy training signal produces unpredictable models.", "body_mdx": "## Human Eval Is Not Just Expensive LLM Eval\n\nHuman evaluation is often framed as the \"ground truth\" that automated metrics approximate. That framing is wrong. Human judgments are also variable, biased, and context-dependent. The difference is that human evaluators bring genuine comprehension and domain knowledge that no automated metric has. The challenge is making that judgment consistent enough to be useful.\n\nThe three problems to solve: who to sample, what rubric to give them, and how to measure whether they're applying it consistently.\n\n## Sampling Strategy\n\nRandom sampling from your output population is almost always the wrong choice. Most model outputs are fine. The interesting cases are at the edges: the outputs the model is most uncertain about, the outputs from underrepresented input categories, the outputs users are most likely to complain about.\n\nA stratified sampling approach:\n\n```\nSampling budget: 200 examples\n\n50 examples -- Random sample (baseline, no selection criterion)\n50 examples -- High-uncertainty outputs (low model confidence, if available)\n50 examples -- Category coverage (select to cover all major input types)\n50 examples -- Failure candidates (flagged by monitoring, user reports, other signals)\n```\n\nIf you don't have model confidence scores, proxy for uncertainty using output variance: run the same prompt multiple times with temperature > 0 and flag prompts where outputs vary significantly.\n\nFor the failure candidate bucket, don't wait for user reports. Run a cheaper automated filter (keyword detection, length anomalies, format violations) to pre-screen the full output population and then human-evaluate the flagged subset.\n\n## Rubric Design\n\nA rubric is a scoring guide that tells annotators exactly what each score level means for each dimension being evaluated. The most common failure mode in rubric design is defining score levels in abstract terms that annotators interpret differently.\n\nVague rubric (do not do this):\n\n```\nHelpfulness: 1=Not helpful, 3=Somewhat helpful, 5=Very helpful\n```\n\nConcrete rubric (do this instead):\n\n```\nHelpfulness:\n5 - Directly answers the question, provides specific actionable steps,\n    no irrelevant content\n4 - Answers the question, steps are clear, minor irrelevant content\n3 - Partially answers the question OR answers fully but with significant\n    irrelevant content\n2 - Identifies the right topic but does not answer the question\n1 - Off-topic, incorrect, or refuses without reason\n```\n\nThe test for a good rubric: give it to two annotators who have never seen it, have them label the same 10 examples independently, and compare results. If they agree on 9 of 10, the rubric is concrete. If they agree on 6 of 10, the rubric is ambiguous.\n\n## Inter-Rater Reliability\n\nInter-rater reliability (IRR) measures how consistently multiple annotators apply the same rubric. Cohen's kappa is the standard metric. It adjusts for chance agreement: two annotators who randomly assign scores would agree some fraction of the time by chance, and kappa corrects for that.\n\n```python\nfrom sklearn.metrics import cohen_kappa_score\n\nannotator_a = [3, 5, 2, 4, 3, 5, 1, 4, 3, 2]\nannotator_b = [3, 4, 2, 4, 3, 5, 2, 4, 3, 2]\n\nkappa = cohen_kappa_score(annotator_a, annotator_b)\nprint(f\"Cohen's kappa: {kappa:.3f}\")\n# 0.895 -> strong agreement\n```\n\nInterpretation benchmarks for kappa:\n\n```\nkappa < 0.4   -- Poor agreement. Rubric needs major revision.\nkappa 0.4-0.6 -- Moderate agreement. Rubric needs clarification.\nkappa 0.6-0.8 -- Substantial agreement. Rubric is usable with monitoring.\nkappa > 0.8   -- Strong agreement. Rubric is reliable.\n```\n\nThe 0.7 threshold is not arbitrary. Below 0.7, the noise in your labels is large enough that model training or evaluation decisions based on those labels will be systematically unreliable. Fine-tuning on noisy labels teaches the model to replicate annotator disagreement rather than actual quality.\n\n## Diagnosing IRR Failures\n\nWhen kappa is low, the diagnostic question is: which rubric items are driving the disagreement?\n\n```mermaid\nflowchart TD\n    A[Collect 2+ annotators' labels] --> B[Compute per-item agreement]\n    B --> C{Any item kappa < 0.5?}\n    C -->|Yes| D[Analyze disagreement examples]\n    D --> E{What's the pattern?}\n    E -->|Boundary cases| F[Add score-level examples to rubric]\n    E -->|Domain knowledge gap| G[Require annotator qualification test]\n    E -->|Dimension overlap| H[Split into separate rubric items]\n    E -->|Subjective criteria| I[Remove or operationalize the criterion]\n    F --> A\n    G --> A\n    H --> A\n    I --> A\n    C -->|No| J[Rubric is ready]\n```\n\nThe most common root causes:\n\n- **Boundary cases**: annotators disagree on whether an output is a 3 or a 4. Fix by adding anchor examples at each score level.\n- **Domain knowledge gap**: one annotator has domain expertise the other lacks. Fix by requiring annotators to pass a qualification test.\n- **Dimension bleed**: two dimensions in the rubric are correlated and annotators are collapsing them. Fix by making each dimension measure one thing.\n- **Inherently subjective criteria**: tone, naturalness, creativity. These are hardest. Either operationalize (define concrete signals) or remove from the rubric.\n\n## Annotation Workflow\n\nAn annotation workflow that produces reliable labels:\n\n1. **Training phase**: all annotators label the same 20 calibration examples before the real task begins. Review disagreements together. Clarify rubric ambiguities.\n2. **Overlap rate**: have multiple annotators label the same examples at a 20% rate throughout the project. Track kappa continuously, not just at the start.\n3. **Adjudication rule**: when two annotators disagree by more than one point on a 5-point scale, route that example to a senior annotator or domain expert.\n4. **Regular calibration sessions**: once per week, review 10 examples together. The rubric should be a living document that gets clarified as edge cases arise.\n\n## What to Do with the Labels\n\nHuman labels serve three purposes: setting performance thresholds, calibrating automated metrics, and providing signal for fine-tuning.\n\nFor threshold-setting, use the label distribution to set your minimum acceptable score. If 90% of human-labeled outputs score 4 or 5 on a 5-point scale, the model is production-ready. If 30% score 1 or 2, it isn't.\n\nFor metric calibration, compute the correlation between automated metric scores (ROUGE, BERTScore, LLM judge) and human labels. High correlation means the automated metric can substitute for human eval on similar distributions.\n\nFor fine-tuning, labels below a quality threshold become negative examples in preference optimization, and high-scoring outputs become positive examples. The quality of the fine-tuned model is bounded by the quality of the labels."}
