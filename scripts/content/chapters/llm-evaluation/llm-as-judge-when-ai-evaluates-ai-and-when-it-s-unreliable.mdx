{"hook_text": "LLM-as-judge is cheap and scalable but has position bias (favoring the first response) and self-enhancement bias. Calibration against human labels is required before you trust it.", "body_mdx": "## Why LLM-as-Judge Exists\n\nHuman evaluation is accurate but expensive. A team of annotators labeling 500 model outputs per day costs real money and takes real time. For rapid iteration, that speed is a bottleneck.\n\nLLM-as-judge routes evaluation through a language model. You give the judge model the original prompt, the model output, and a rubric. The judge returns a score or a preference (A is better than B). At scale, this costs cents per example and runs in seconds. That's why every major lab uses some form of it.\n\nThe problem is that LLM judges are systematically biased in ways that compound if you don't account for them.\n\n## The Two Dominant Biases\n\n**Position bias** is the tendency for a judge model to prefer whichever response appears first in the prompt. In pairwise evaluation (\"which of these two responses is better?\"), studies have shown that GPT-4 favors the first response roughly 60-70% of the time even when the second is objectively superior by human standards. The effect is large enough to corrupt ranking experiments entirely.\n\n**Self-enhancement bias** (also called self-preference bias) is the tendency for a model to prefer outputs similar to its own style and format. GPT-4 tends to rate GPT-4-style outputs higher. Claude tends to rate Claude-style outputs higher. This creates a measurement distortion when you use a top-tier model to judge outputs from models in the same family.\n\nAdditional biases that appear in practice:\n\n- **Verbosity bias**: Longer responses are rated higher, independent of content quality.\n- **Authority bias**: Responses that cite sources or use technical vocabulary score higher, even if the content is wrong.\n- **Sycophancy in judging**: The judge inflates scores for responses that agree with the premise in the prompt.\n\n## What Calibration Means\n\nCalibration is the process of measuring how well a judge model's scores correlate with human judgments, then correcting for systematic divergences.\n\nThe workflow:\n\n1. Collect 100-200 examples that span your quality distribution.\n2. Have humans label each example (pairwise preference or absolute score).\n3. Run your judge model on the same examples.\n4. Compute the correlation between human labels and judge scores.\n5. If the correlation is high (Spearman's r > 0.8), the judge is usable with caution. If it's below 0.6, the judge is not reliable for your task.\n\n```python\nfrom scipy.stats import spearmanr\n\nhuman_scores = [3, 2, 4, 1, 3, 2, 4, 5]  # from annotation\njudge_scores = [3, 3, 4, 2, 3, 2, 5, 5]  # from LLM judge\n\ncorr, pvalue = spearmanr(human_scores, judge_scores)\nprint(f\"Spearman r: {corr:.3f}, p-value: {pvalue:.4f}\")\n# Spearman r: 0.847, p-value: 0.0071  -> acceptable\n```\n\nCalibration tells you the judge is correlated with human judgment; it doesn't tell you the judge is right. If humans have inter-rater disagreements (below 0.7 kappa), calibrating to those labels transfers the noise into the judge.\n\n## Mitigating Position Bias\n\nThe standard mitigation for position bias is positional symmetry: evaluate each pair in both orders (A then B, and B then A), then aggregate.\n\n```python\ndef judge_pair(prompt, response_a, response_b, judge_fn):\n    # Forward order\n    score_forward = judge_fn(prompt, first=response_a, second=response_b)\n    # Reversed order\n    score_reversed = judge_fn(prompt, first=response_b, second=response_a)\n    \n    # If forward says A wins and reversed says B wins, results are consistent\n    # If both say the first response wins, position bias is likely\n    return {\n        \"forward\": score_forward,\n        \"reversed\": score_reversed,\n        \"consistent\": score_forward[\"winner\"] != score_reversed[\"winner\"]\n    }\n```\n\nIf the judge picks the first response in both orderings, the result is suspect. Treat it as a tie or escalate to human review.\n\n## Mitigating Verbosity Bias\n\nVerbosity bias is harder to correct structurally. Two approaches:\n\n**Truncation**: Truncate all responses to the same token count before judging. This controls for length but discards content that might matter.\n\n**Length-conditioned rubric**: Include an explicit instruction in the judge prompt: \"Do not penalize brevity. A concise correct answer is better than a verbose partially-correct answer.\" This helps but doesn't eliminate the bias.\n\n```mermaid\nflowchart LR\n    A[Model Output] --> B[LLM Judge]\n    C[Rubric] --> B\n    B --> D{Bias Check}\n    D -->|Position bias risk| E[Run Reversed Order]\n    D -->|Verbosity risk| F[Normalize Length]\n    E --> G[Aggregate Both Scores]\n    F --> G\n    G --> H{Calibration r > 0.8?}\n    H -->|Yes| I[Use Judge Score]\n    H -->|No| J[Human Review Required]\n```\n\n## When LLM-as-Judge Is Appropriate\n\nLLM judges work well for:\n\n- **Rapid iteration during development**: comparing prompt variants before investing in human eval.\n- **High-volume monitoring**: flagging outputs for human review based on quality signals.\n- **Relative ranking**: determining which of two models performs better on average across many examples, where individual errors average out.\n\nLLM judges are unreliable for:\n\n- **Absolute scoring**: assigning a definitive quality number to a single output.\n- **Detecting subtle factual errors**: judges often miss hallucinations that require domain knowledge to spot.\n- **Evaluating creative outputs**: quality criteria are personal and the judge's preferences are not your users' preferences.\n- **High-stakes decisions**: setting a performance bar for production release based solely on judge scores.\n\n## Structuring the Judge Prompt\n\nThe judge prompt design has a large effect on score quality. Principles that hold across tasks:\n\n- **Be explicit about what to ignore**: if you don't want length to influence scores, say so.\n- **Give a discrete scale**: a 1-5 integer scale produces more consistent results than a 1-100 float.\n- **Ask for reasoning before the verdict**: chain-of-thought before the score reduces inconsistency.\n- **Separate dimensions**: if you care about accuracy and helpfulness, score them separately, not as a single composite.\n\n```\nYou are evaluating a technical explanation for clarity.\nDo not factor in response length.\n\nFirst, identify:\n- Is the core concept stated correctly?\n- Are any claims misleading or ambiguous?\n\nThen rate clarity on a scale of 1 to 5:\n1 - Incomprehensible or incorrect\n2 - Mostly unclear\n3 - Understandable with effort\n4 - Clear\n5 - Exceptionally clear\n\nFormat: <reasoning>...</reasoning><score>N</score>\n```\n\nLLM-as-judge is a tool with a specific valid range. Inside that range, it's extremely useful. Outside it, it produces confident-sounding garbage. Knowing the difference is most of the job."}
