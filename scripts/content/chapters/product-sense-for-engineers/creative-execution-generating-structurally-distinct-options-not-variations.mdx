Most product thinking stops one option too early.", "body_mdx": "## The Illusion of Multiple Options\n\nWhen product teams generate options, they usually think they're generating distinct alternatives. Most of the time, they're generating variations on a single approach with different parameters.\n\nAn example: a team is trying to reduce time-to-first-value in their analytics product. They brainstorm:\n1. Add a getting-started checklist to the dashboard\n2. Make the getting-started checklist more prominent\n3. Send a getting-started email sequence on day 1, 3, and 7\n4. Create a video walkthrough of the main features\n\nAll four of these are the same option: guide users through existing features with clearer instructions. The parameters differ (checklist vs. email vs. video, more or less prominent) but the underlying model is identical. If the problem is that users don't find value because the product's core value proposition is unclear, no amount of getting-started guidance will fix it.\n\nStructurally distinct options require stepping back from the solution space and asking what fundamentally different ways exist to solve the problem.\n\n## Structural Dimensions\n\nStructural distinctiveness means varying along dimensions that change the underlying model of how the solution works, not just its parameters.\n\nFor the same analytics onboarding problem, structurally distinct options might look like:\n\n| Option | Underlying Model |\n|--------|------------------|\n| Getting-started checklist | Guide users through the existing product |\n| Pre-built dashboards for their role | Deliver value before any setup |\n| Import data and show instant insights | Remove setup friction entirely |\n| Pair new users with a customer from their industry | Social proof and peer education |\n| Narrow the product to one core insight for first 30 days | Reduce scope to the highest-value feature |\n\nEach row in that table represents a genuinely different theory of why users don't find value fast enough. Some options assume the problem is user confusion. Others assume the problem is setup burden. Others assume the problem is that the product's full feature set is overwhelming and users would benefit from a constrained entry point.\n\nYou can only test the right theory if you've generated options across multiple theories.\n\n## The Dimensions to Vary\n\nGenerating structurally distinct options requires deliberately varying along a set of dimensions. Not all of these will apply to every problem, but working through them forces exploration beyond the obvious:\n\n```mermaid\ngraph LR\n  A[Problem Space] --> B[Who does the work?]\n  A --> C[When does the action happen?]\n  A --> D[What is eliminated vs. improved?]\n  A --> E[What is the unit of value?]\n  A --> F[Who is the primary actor?]\n  B --> B1[User / Product / Team / Community]\n  C --> C1[Before / During / After the friction point]\n  D --> D1[Optimize existing vs. remove entirely]\n  E --> E1[Feature / Outcome / Relationship / Status]\n  F --> F1[Individual user / Group / External party]\n```\n\n**Who does the work?** Many products default to making the user do work they could automate, delegate to the product, or crowdsource to the community. When a product asks users to configure something, the structural alternative is to configure it automatically based on observable signals.\n\n**When does the action happen?** A friction point that appears at step 4 might be addressed by changing what happens at steps 1-3, not by making step 4 easier. Moving credit card entry after first value delivery is a structural change in timing, not a refinement of the payment flow.\n\n**What is eliminated vs. improved?** \"Make X faster\" and \"remove X entirely\" are structurally different. The first assumes X is necessary. The second questions the assumption. Many product improvements come from eliminating steps that seemed necessary until someone asked whether they were.\n\n**What is the unit of value?** If users currently derive value from individual features, a structural alternative is bundling features into outcomes. If users currently get value from individual interactions, a structural alternative is relationships that compound over time.\n\n## Why Engineers Default to Variations\n\nEngineers are trained to solve specified problems. When a problem is specified, finding the optimal solution to that specification is the task. Creative execution requires something different: questioning whether the specification is right before optimizing within it.\n\nThe engineering failure mode is treating the first reasonable interpretation of a problem as the correct interpretation and generating solutions within that frame. When someone says \"improve onboarding,\" the implicit frame is usually \"make the existing onboarding flow easier.\" That frame might be wrong.\n\nA useful discipline: before generating solutions, write down at least three different interpretations of the problem. An onboarding problem might be a product-market fit problem (wrong users are signing up), a setup friction problem (the right users can't complete configuration), a value delay problem (setup completes but users don't see value fast enough), or a retention problem masquerading as an onboarding problem (users who complete onboarding still churn at high rates).\n\nEach interpretation suggests different solutions. Collapsing to a single interpretation early means generating variations within that frame, even if it's the wrong frame.\n\n## The Rule of Three Structural Differences\n\nA practical test for whether you have structurally distinct options: can you explain, in one sentence each, how each option differs in its underlying model? If two options can't be distinguished by their underlying model, they're variations.\n\nIn practice, generating three genuinely structurally distinct options for any product problem is difficult. Four is very difficult. If generating two structurally distinct options takes effort, that's normal. The skill is built through practice.\n\nA useful exercise: pick a product you use daily and identify one thing about it that bothers you. Write down five solutions. Then categorize them by underlying model. You will almost certainly find that several of them collapse into one or two models. That observation is the practice. The discipline is then pushing yourself to find the models you haven't explored.\n\n## In Interviews\n\nCreative execution is one of the clearest signals interviewers look for in PM-track and senior+ engineering interviews. The ability to generate structurally distinct options signals that the candidate understands the problem space well enough to reason about it from multiple angles, not just to optimize within the first frame that came to mind.\n\nThe failure mode in interviews is generating a long list of variations quickly. This looks like creative energy but signals shallow problem understanding. Three genuinely distinct options, explained clearly, with the underlying model of each made explicit, is better than ten variations of the same approach.\n\nThe setup for generating distinct options in an interview is usually to spend more time on problem framing than feels comfortable. Before proposing anything, articulate at least two different ways to understand the problem. That discipline naturally generates structural diversity in the solutions that follow."}
