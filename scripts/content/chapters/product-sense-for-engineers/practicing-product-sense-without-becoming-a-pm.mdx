It requires asking one question about every ticket you pick up: what problem does this solve, and how will I know in four weeks whether it worked.", "body_mdx": "## The Development Path That Doesn't Require a Role Change\n\nThe most common misconception about developing product sense is that it requires becoming a PM. This leads engineers down one of two paths: they pursue PM roles they may not actually want, or they decide product sense is someone else's domain and stop developing it.\n\nBoth responses leave an engineer with less influence than they could have, less interesting work than they could be doing, and less ability to shape outcomes they care about.\n\nProduct sense is a capability, not a job description. Staff engineers, tech leads, and founding engineers who operate with strong product sense are among the most influential people in any organization. They make technical decisions that account for product constraints. They identify when a spec is solving the wrong problem before building starts. They can engage in strategic conversations with credibility because they understand both the technical and product sides of the decision.\n\nThe development path is the same regardless of title: deliberate practice on real work, with clear diagnostic feedback.\n\n## The Daily Practice Framework\n\nProduct sense is built by applying specific reasoning moves to real decisions, repeatedly, until the moves become reflexive. Four moves applied daily create compounding returns:\n\n```mermaid\ngraph LR\n  A[Ticket arrives] --> B[Frame: What problem does this solve?]\n  B --> C[For whom is this a problem?]\n  C --> D[What would success look like in 4 weeks?]\n  D --> E[What am I giving up to build this vs. something else?]\n  E --> F[Build]\n  F --> G[Review: Did the metric move? Why or why not?]\n  G --> A\n```\n\n**Frame the problem before touching the solution.** Every ticket, feature, and project was created because someone believed it would solve a problem. Finding that problem and validating that the proposed solution actually addresses it takes 10 minutes. Skipping this step is how teams build technically correct features that don't move any metric.\n\n**Name the user.** \"Users\" is not specific enough to reason from. \"Engineering managers at growth-stage startups who are evaluating developer tools on a 30-day trial\" is specific enough to reason from. The specificity of the user definition determines the quality of the product reasoning that follows.\n\n**Define success in advance.** Before picking up work, write down what metric would have moved, by how much, in what timeframe, if the work was successful. This feels unnecessary until you're writing the post-mortem and realize no one remembers what success was supposed to look like.\n\n**Name the tradeoff.** Every piece of work takes time and attention away from something else. The opportunity cost is a real tradeoff. Making it explicit once per project builds the habit of treating prioritization as a reasoning exercise rather than a process exercise.\n\n## Using Your Existing Work as the Practice Field\n\nEngineers don't need side projects to practice product sense. The work already on the table is the practice field. Three applications:\n\n**Pre-ticket questions.** Before starting any significant piece of work, write three sentences: the user and their goal, the problem being solved, and the metric that will confirm the work succeeded. Share this with the PM or tech lead. The responses are often clarifying, and the habit builds product reasoning without requiring any additional time.\n\n**Experiment on shipped features.** Most engineers ship features and immediately move to the next ticket. Spending one hour per sprint reading the analytics on features you shipped three months ago builds domain expertise faster than any other practice. You see what moved and what didn't. You see whether your original model of the problem was right. The feedback is specific and connected to decisions you made.\n\n**Feature post-mortems.** When a feature underperforms or is reverted, dig into why. Not to assign blame, but to build a model of what conditions produce good outcomes and which produce poor ones. Engineers who do this deliberately over two to three years develop a level of domain expertise that is genuinely unusual.\n\n## What to Read and What to Skip\n\nThe product literature is large and uneven. A significant portion of it is advice about PM career management, organizational dynamics, and communication frameworks. This is useful for PMs and marginally useful for engineers trying to develop product sense.\n\nWhat is actually useful:\n\nPrimary data over frameworks. Reading your own product's analytics, reading competitor reviews on G2 or app stores, and reading user interviews produces domain expertise. Reading about user research frameworks produces meta-knowledge about how to do user research. Both are useful; the primary data is more useful.\n\nCase studies that show the reasoning, not just the decision. The best case studies include the information the team had at the time they made the decision, the alternatives they considered, and what actually happened. Case studies that only describe what was built and that it succeeded are not useful for developing reasoning skills.\n\nBooks that explain specific reasoning models with real examples, not books that describe what product managers do or how to run a roadmap process.\n\n## The Interview Preparation That Actually Works\n\nIf the goal includes passing PM-track or senior-engineer interviews that assess product sense, the preparation that works is the same as the practice that works: repeated application of specific reasoning moves to real product situations.\n\nFour preparation habits that are high-value:\n\nPick one consumer product and audit it weekly. Track what changes, hypothesize why, and predict what will change next month. The predictive element forces reasoning rather than observation.\n\nPractice the full FLOW structure on products you know well. Frame the problem behind the current design. List structurally distinct alternatives. Optimize between them with named criteria. Win the decision with a specific because-Z. This structure matches the reasoning tested in most senior product interviews.\n\nFind products where you have domain expertise and analyze them deeply. The depth of reasoning available when you know a domain well is qualitatively different from surface-level product analysis. Interviewers can tell the difference.\n\nRecord yourself explaining a product decision. The places where you become vague or use filler words are the places where the reasoning is thin. This diagnostic is uncomfortable and highly effective.\n\n## The Compounding Dynamic\n\nProduct sense compounds in a way that most engineering skills don't. Technical skills can become obsolete as the technology shifts. Product sense, once built, applies across domains, companies, and roles because the underlying reasoning moves are domain-agnostic even if the specific knowledge is not.\n\nAn engineer who has developed strong taste, strategic thinking, creative execution, and domain expertise in one product context carries most of that into the next context. The domain knowledge refreshes. The reasoning skill compounds.\n\nThis is the long-term case for developing product sense: not that it makes you a better PM candidate, but that it makes your engineering work more valuable over time. The engineers who are most influential at senior levels are the ones who can connect technical decisions to outcomes with precision and credibility. That connection is made through product sense, applied consistently over a long enough period that it becomes part of how they think.\n\nStart with the next ticket. Ask what it solves and for whom. Write down what success looks like. That is the practice. Everything else follows from doing it repeatedly."}
