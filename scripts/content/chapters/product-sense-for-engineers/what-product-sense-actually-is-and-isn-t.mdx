{"hook_text": "Product sense is not taste, intuition, or creativity. It is a set of reasoning moves that can be learned, practiced, and measured. Engineers who dismiss it as \"soft\" are the ones who get surprised when they miss promotions.", "body_mdx": "## The Myth of the Born Product Person\n\nAsk most engineers what product sense is and you get one of two answers. Either it's something that PMs have and engineers don't, or it's a vague capacity for empathy and user intuition that you either possess or you don't. Both answers are wrong, and believing either one will hold you back.\n\nProduct sense is the ability to make good decisions about what to build with incomplete information. That's it. The incomplete information part is permanent. The good decisions part is learnable. Every experienced PM developed it through practice, not through personality.\n\nThe confusion comes from conflating the outputs of product sense with the skill itself. When a senior PM looks at a design and immediately sees that the core feature is solving the wrong problem, that looks like intuition. It isn't. It's pattern recognition built from hundreds of cycles of framing problems, choosing bets, watching outcomes, and adjusting. The same way a senior engineer's code review feels like a gut reaction but is actually compressed experience.\n\n## What Product Sense Actually Contains\n\nProduct sense is a bundle of four specific reasoning capabilities. Each one is distinct, each one is trainable, and each one has a failure mode that shows up predictably in interviews and on the job.\n\n```mermaid\ngraph TD\n  A[Product Sense] --> B[Taste]\n  A --> C[Strategic Thinking]\n  A --> D[Creative Execution]\n  A --> E[Domain Expertise]\n  B --> B1[Distinguishing tradeoffs from preferences]\n  C --> C1[Why-now and competitive context]\n  D --> D1[Structurally distinct options]\n  E --> E1[Named metrics, real baselines]\n```\n\n**Taste** is knowing whether a design decision is a real tradeoff or a personal preference. When someone says \"the onboarding flow feels too long,\" taste is what tells you whether that feeling is pointing at a friction problem that will damage conversion, or whether it's a comfort reaction to something that is actually appropriate for the complexity of the product.\n\n**Strategic thinking** is the ability to situate a decision in time and competitive context. Not just \"should we build this\" but \"should we build this now, given what competitors can do and what we uniquely can do.\" A strategic answer has a because-Z: we should build this feature because of this specific structural advantage or window that won't be available later.\n\n**Creative execution** is generating options that are structurally different, not just parametrically different. \"Make the button bigger\" and \"make the button more prominent\" are the same option. \"Redesign the flow so the action is the only thing on the screen\" is a different option. Creative execution is the ability to generate ideas that solve a problem in genuinely different ways.\n\n**Domain expertise** is knowing what a good metric looks like in the domain you're in. \"Improve retention\" is not a product decision. \"Improve day-7 retention from 30% to 45% over the next 60 days\" is a product decision, and it requires knowing that 30% is below-average for consumer apps in this category, that day-7 is the relevant inflection point, and that 60 days is a realistic window given current team velocity.\n\n## What Product Sense Is Not\n\nThree things get misidentified as product sense so often they deserve explicit correction.\n\n**User empathy is not product sense.** It's an input to product sense. Feeling what users feel is a data source. The reasoning moves you apply to that data source are the skill. An engineer who interviews 50 users and draws no structural conclusions from the patterns has a lot of empathy and no product sense. An engineer who interviews 5 users and extracts a clear problem frame has done something valuable.\n\n**PM knowledge is not product sense.** Knowing the RICE framework, or how to write a PRD, or how to run a sprint review is domain knowledge about PM craft. It is not the same as being able to make good decisions about what to build. Engineers who study PM frameworks hoping product sense will follow are studying the wrong thing.\n\n**Seniority is not product sense.** Ten years of building features doesn't automatically produce product sense. It produces experience. Experience becomes product sense only when it's reflected on. Engineers who have shipped a lot of things without asking why those things were prioritized have a lot of experience and possibly little product sense.\n\n## Why Engineers Specifically Struggle With It\n\nEngineering training optimizes for correctness. A problem has a right answer, a test will tell you if you found it, and deviation from correctness is a bug. Product decisions are structurally different: there is no right answer, the feedback loop is months long, and multiple reasonable choices exist for most decisions.\n\nThis creates a specific failure mode for engineers entering product discussions. They anchor too early. The first solution that satisfies the constraints feels like the answer because the habits built for solving technical problems reward finding a correct answer fast. In product, anchoring early means you've stopped exploring before you've understood the space.\n\nA second failure mode is mistaking precision for rigor. Engineers are comfortable with precise specifications. Product problems often have inherently imprecise inputs. The response to imprecision is not to demand more precision before engaging. It's to make the reasoning transparent so that when new information arrives, the decision can be updated.\n\n## The Practice Frame\n\nProduct sense is built through deliberate repetition of three things: framing problems before proposing solutions, generating multiple structurally distinct options before committing to one, and naming the metric that will tell you whether the decision was correct.\n\nNone of this requires a PM role. Every engineering ticket is an opportunity. Before picking up a piece of work, ask what problem it solves and for whom. Before proposing a technical approach, generate two alternatives that solve the problem differently. Before shipping, name the metric that will tell you six weeks from now whether this was worth doing.\n\nThe engineers who develop strong product sense do this habitually, not occasionally. The habit is more important than the sophistication of any individual application. The reasoning gets sharper through repetition.\n\n## A Starting Point\n\nThe clearest diagnostic for your current product sense level is this: when you pick up a ticket, can you explain what user problem it solves, for which user, and how you would know in four weeks whether the implementation addressed it?\n\nIf that question feels uncomfortable or unanswerable, that's where the practice starts. Not with empathy exercises or PM books. With that question, applied to the next ticket you pick up.\n\nThe chapters ahead go deep on each of the four components: taste, strategic thinking, creative execution, and domain expertise. Each chapter treats the component as a skill with a specific failure mode, a diagnostic, and a practice method. The goal isn't to make you think like a PM. It's to make you think with more precision about the decisions that determine whether the things you build actually matter."}
