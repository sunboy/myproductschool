{"hook_text": "\"Improve retention\" is not a product decision. \"Improve day-7 retention from 31% to 45% over 60 days\" is. The difference between them is domain expertise, and it is what separates candidates who seem product-fluent from candidates who actually are.", "body_mdx": "## The Vagueness Problem\n\nProduct conversations at most companies are full of statements that sound specific but aren't. \"We should improve engagement.\" \"The activation rate needs to go up.\" \"Users are churning too fast.\"\n\nNone of these statements contain enough information to act on. What counts as improvement? From where to where? In what timeframe? Compared to what baseline? Under what constraints?\n\nThe reason these statements feel acceptable in product discussions is that everyone in the room has implicitly agreed to treat vagueness as shared understanding. But when it comes time to make a decision or measure an outcome, the vagueness collapses. Everyone discovers they were imagining different specifics.\n\nDomain expertise is what makes specificity possible. You can't say \"day-7 retention of 45% is the goal\" if you don't know that the current rate is 31%, that the industry median for this category is 38%, that 45% would put the product in the top quartile, and that the infrastructure changes required to get there are achievable in 60 days with the current team.\n\n## What Domain Knowledge Covers\n\nDomain expertise for product purposes has three layers: baselines, signals, and levers.\n\n**Baselines** are the numbers that define normal performance in a domain. Consumer social apps at the growth stage typically see day-1 retention around 40-50%, day-7 around 20-30%, and day-30 around 15-20%. A team that doesn't know these numbers will celebrate a 25% day-7 retention rate in a context where it should be alarming them.\n\n**Signals** are the metrics that predict the outcome you care about before you can measure it directly. In consumer apps, early activation signals (did the user complete the core loop within the first session?) are better predictors of 30-day retention than any engagement metric collected after day 3. Knowing which signals lead which outcomes is the kind of domain knowledge that changes what you measure and when.\n\n**Levers** are the interventions that actually move the metrics. In B2B SaaS, reducing time-to-first-value in the trial period is a lever that moves trial-to-paid conversion. In marketplace products, supply-side density in the user's local area is a lever that moves both demand-side engagement and supply-side quality. Levers are domain-specific. What works in one product category may not transfer.\n\n## The Metric Specificity Test\n\n```\nVague claim:\n  \"We should improve our onboarding\"\n\nDomain-specific claim:\n  \"Day-1 activation (user completes their first [core action] within 24h\n   of signup) is at 34%. Industry median for productivity tools in this\n   segment is 52%. Closing half that gap would add approximately 400\n   activated users/month at current signup volume.\"\n\nDecision that follows:\n  \"We can test removing the required team invitation step from onboarding\n   and compare day-1 activation rates between cohorts. 3-week test with\n   ~600 users per arm gives us 80% power to detect a 5pp improvement.\"\n```\n\nNotice what changed. The vague claim requires no domain knowledge. The specific claim requires knowing current rates, category benchmarks, what constitutes a meaningful improvement given signup volume, and what an appropriately powered test looks like. The decision that follows is actionable because the claim was specific.\n\n## Domain Expertise Is Domain-Specific\n\nThis sounds obvious but has an important implication: domain expertise in one product category does not automatically transfer to another. The metrics that matter in consumer social are different from the metrics that matter in developer tools, which are different from the metrics that matter in B2B enterprise software.\n\n```mermaid\ngraph TD\n  A[Consumer Social] -->|Key metrics| A1[DAU/MAU ratio, D1/D7/D30 retention, friend graph density]\n  B[B2B SaaS] -->|Key metrics| B1[Trial-to-paid conversion, NRR, time-to-first-value, expansion ARR]\n  C[Marketplace] -->|Key metrics| C1[Supply density, take rate, repeat purchase rate, GMV per active user]\n  D[Developer Tools] -->|Key metrics| D1[Activation rate, API call volume, team expansion, integration depth]\n  E[E-commerce] -->|Key metrics| E1[Cart abandonment, repeat purchase rate, LTV by acquisition channel]\n```\n\nAn engineer who has spent 5 years building consumer apps and moves to a B2B SaaS company needs to deliberately acquire domain expertise in that new context. The reasoning skills transfer. The specific knowledge does not.\n\nThis is also why product interviews that ask candidates to solve problems outside their domain experience are intentionally difficult. The interviewer is testing whether the candidate can reason from first principles when they don't have domain knowledge, and whether they have the self-awareness to acknowledge the limits of what they know.\n\n## Building Domain Expertise Deliberately\n\nDomain expertise accumulates faster when it's pursued deliberately rather than absorbed passively. Four practices that accelerate it:\n\n**Read the numbers regularly.** Many engineers never look at product analytics outside of the features they own. Even spending 30 minutes a week reading the company's core dashboards builds an intuitive understanding of what normal looks like across the product.\n\n**Benchmark externally.** Most domains have publicly available research on benchmarks: SaaS conversion rates, mobile retention curves, marketplace liquidity thresholds. Knowing where the company sits relative to these benchmarks changes how you interpret internal numbers.\n\n**Trace the lever chain.** For every metric you care about, understand what the three most powerful levers are and how each works mechanically. This is the difference between knowing that \"D7 retention is low\" and knowing that \"D7 retention is driven by whether users have at least 3 connections in their first session, and the current onboarding flow doesn't push toward that.\"\n\n**Post-mortem correctly.** When an experiment or feature launch produces unexpected results, the analysis that follows is where domain expertise is formed or missed. A post-mortem that concludes \"it didn't work\" builds no expertise. A post-mortem that concludes \"the 12% improvement in D7 retention was concentrated in users who completed the tutorial, which suggests the problem is tutorial completion rate rather than tutorial content\" builds a lever model.\n\n## Domain Expertise in Interviews\n\nIn PM and senior engineering interviews, domain expertise shows up in specificity. When a candidate says \"I would measure retention\" and stops there, they're signaling that they know the word retention matters but don't know the specific contours of how retention behaves in this domain.\n\nWhen a candidate says \"for a consumer app in this category, I'd track D1, D7, and D30, with D7 as the primary metric because it's the inflection point where users either form a habit or drop off, and I'd set success at 35% D7 because that's roughly the median for social apps and represents a significant improvement from the 20% that's common in early-stage products without a deliberate retention strategy,\" they are demonstrating domain expertise.\n\nThe specificity is not about being impressive. It is about being useful. Decisions made against specific targets are testable and adjustable. Decisions made against vague goals drift.\n\nDomain expertise is a moat because it takes time to acquire and because it compounds. Every experiment you analyze, every post-mortem you run, every benchmark you study adds to a model of how outcomes are produced in a specific domain. That model is what makes your product opinions valuable rather than generic."}
