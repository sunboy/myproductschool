{"hook_text": "Sympathy says 'I understand how you feel.' Cognitive empathy says 'I understand what you're trying to accomplish, what's blocking you, and what success looks like from your position.' Product decisions require the second one.", "body_mdx": "## Two Ways of Understanding Other People\n\nEmpathy gets used as a catch-all term in product and design work, which obscures a distinction that matters a lot in practice.\n\nSympathy is emotional resonance. It's feeling what someone else feels, or at least recognizing and relating to it. Sympathy is important in customer support, in how a product communicates during errors, and in situations where users are frustrated or confused. But sympathy by itself doesn't tell you what to build.\n\nCognitive empathy is something different. It's the ability to construct an accurate model of another person's goals, constraints, and decision-making process. It's not about how they feel. It's about what they're trying to accomplish, what information they have, what they believe to be true, and what success looks like from their position.\n\nFor product work, cognitive empathy is the operative skill. You can feel deeply for users who struggle with your product and still build the wrong fix. Cognitive empathy is what tells you what fix would actually work.\n\n## What \"Simulating Goals\" Means in Practice\n\nTo apply cognitive empathy to a product decision, the exercise is to build a mental simulation of the user's perspective at the moment of interaction. This simulation has specific components:\n\n**Goal**: What is the user trying to accomplish right now? Not in general. At this specific moment, on this specific screen.\n\n**Context**: What has happened immediately before this moment? What are they coming from? What are they going to next?\n\n**Constraints**: What are the limits on their behavior? Time, technical knowledge, organizational authority, available information.\n\n**Success criteria**: What does a good outcome look like from their position? How will they know the task is done?\n\n**Failure modes**: What would make this interaction feel like a failure to them, even if the product worked correctly?\n\n```mermaid\nflowchart TD\n    A[User Arrives at Interface] --> B[Their Goal]\n    A --> C[Their Context]\n    A --> D[Their Constraints]\n    B & C & D --> E[Their Decision-Making]\n    E --> F[Action Taken]\n    F --> G{Matches Success Criteria?}\n    G -- Yes --> H[Positive outcome]\n    G -- No --> I[Negative outcome / churn]\n```\n\n## Sympathy vs. Cognitive Empathy: A Concrete Comparison\n\nHere's the same user complaint analyzed through both lenses:\n\n**User complaint**: \"The deploy process is confusing and takes too long.\"\n\nSympathetic response: \"That sounds frustrating. We want our users to have a smooth experience. We'll look into improving the UI.\"\n\nCognitively empathetic response: \"This user is a mid-level engineer deploying to production, probably under time pressure from a release deadline. They have a mental model of what a deploy should look like from previous tools they've used. The confusion isn't about the steps being complex. It's about the steps not matching their expected model. 'Too long' might mean the actual duration, or it might mean a lack of feedback during the process that makes it feel longer than it is.\"\n\nThe sympathetic response generates goodwill. The cognitively empathetic response generates hypotheses that can be tested and acted on.\n\n## Why Engineers Often Default to Sympathy\n\nEngineers building products are usually expert users of their own tools. They know the codebase, the infrastructure, the intended behavior. This expertise makes it hard to simulate the perspective of someone who doesn't have that background.\n\nThis is called the curse of knowledge: once you know something, it's very difficult to accurately imagine not knowing it. An engineer who knows exactly how the deploy pipeline works cannot easily simulate what it's like to encounter it for the first time without that context.\n\nSympathy is the fallback when cognitive simulation is hard. \"I would also find it confusing\" is an emotional proxy for actually modeling what the user knows and doesn't know.\n\nThe fix isn't to suppress expertise. It's to actively reconstruct the user's information state at the moment of interaction. What do they see? What terminology do they know? What have they already tried? What do they believe to be true about how the system works?\n\n## Simulating Multiple Stakeholders\n\nCognitive empathy doesn't apply to just the direct user. Every product decision has multiple stakeholders with different goals, constraints, and success criteria.\n\nFor an internal developer tool:\n- The engineer using it wants fast task completion without reading docs\n- The engineering manager wants visibility into team activity and blockers\n- The security team wants audit logs and access controls\n- The platform team wants low support burden and clear error messages\n\nA change that improves cognitive empathy for one stakeholder can actively hurt another. Surfacing more detail for the engineering manager might add cognitive load for the engineer. Adding audit logging might add latency that breaks the engineer's flow.\n\nSimulating all relevant stakeholder perspectives before making a design decision doesn't guarantee the right answer. It does prevent the mistake of optimizing for one perspective while ignoring others.\n\n## Building the Simulation Without User Research\n\nUser research is the most reliable way to populate a cognitive empathy simulation. But it's not always available, especially during early design or rapid iteration.\n\nIn the absence of research, the technique is to make the implicit explicit. Write down your assumptions about the user's goal, context, constraints, and success criteria. Then examine each assumption:\n\n- Is this assumption based on observed user behavior, or is it based on what we'd want to be true?\n- What would have to be different about the user's situation for this assumption to be wrong?\n- If this assumption is wrong, how does that change the recommended design?\n\nThis doesn't produce the same quality of insight as actual user data. But it does convert vague feelings of \"I think users would want this\" into falsifiable hypotheses, which is a prerequisite for learning anything.\n\n## The Practical Output\n\nCognitive empathy, applied consistently, produces a specific kind of output: product decisions framed in terms of user goals and constraints rather than in terms of features and implementation.\n\nInstead of: \"We should add a progress bar to the deploy screen.\"\n\nTry: \"Users deploying to production need continuous feedback about whether the process is working or stuck. A progress bar does this if the stages are meaningful to the user. But if the deploy has 15 internal stages they don't understand, the bar won't reduce anxiety. What would actually help is surfacing the one or two signals they care about: did the build pass? Is the rollout percentage increasing?\"\n\nThe difference is the simulation. The first recommendation starts from the feature. The second starts from the user's goal and works backward to what would actually serve it."}
