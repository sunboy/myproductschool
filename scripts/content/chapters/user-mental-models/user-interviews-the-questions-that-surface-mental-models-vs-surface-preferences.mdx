{"hook_text": "When you ask users what they want, they tell you their preferences. When you ask them what they did last time, they tell you their behavior. Only one of those reliably predicts what they'll actually do.", "body_mdx": "## The Problem With Asking What People Want\n\nUser interviews feel like the most direct path to understanding users. Ask them what they need, listen to the answers, build accordingly.\n\nThe problem is that users are not reliable reporters of their own future behavior. When asked \"what would you want in a tool like this?\", they describe an idealized version of their behavior, not their actual behavior. They say they'd use the advanced analytics features when in practice they've never opened the analytics tab in any tool they use.\n\nThis isn't dishonesty. It's how humans work. Asked about hypotheticals, we describe what we wish we did, not what we do. This is why \"if you build it, they will come\" fails so often: the product gets built for the user people imagine themselves to be, not the user they actually are.\n\nThe alternative is to interview not for preferences but for behavior and mental models.\n\n## Two Categories of Interview Questions\n\nEvery user interview question falls into one of two categories:\n\n**Preference questions** ask what users want, think, or would do in hypothetical situations:\n- \"Would you find it helpful if we added X?\"\n- \"How important is Y to you?\"\n- \"What features would you like to see?\"\n- \"If we changed Z, how would that affect you?\"\n\n**Behavioral questions** ask what users have actually done in real situations:\n- \"Walk me through the last time you tried to do X.\"\n- \"What did you do right before you started that task?\"\n- \"What happened when you got to the step where Y wasn't working?\"\n- \"What tools were you using at the time?\"\n\nPreference questions produce feature requests. Behavioral questions produce mental models.\n\n```mermaid\nflowchart LR\n    A[Interview Question Type] --> B{Preference or Behavioral?}\n    B -- Preference --> C[Idealized self-report]\n    B -- Behavioral --> D[Actual past behavior]\n    C --> E[Feature wish list]\n    D --> F[Mental model data]\n    E --> G[High risk of building unused features]\n    F --> H[Predicts actual product usage]\n```\n\n## The Core Technique: Past-Behavior Probing\n\nThe most reliable interview technique for surfacing mental models is asking about specific past events. Not hypotheticals. Not general patterns. Specific, recent events.\n\n**Opening question**: \"Tell me about the last time you [task related to the problem space].\"\n\nFrom there, the role is to follow the story chronologically and ask clarifying questions that reveal what the user was thinking, not just what they did.\n\nUseful follow-up probes:\n- \"What made you decide to do that?\"\n- \"What were you expecting to happen?\"\n- \"What happened instead?\"\n- \"What did you do next?\"\n- \"Was there a moment where you weren't sure what to do?\"\n- \"What did you think the system was doing at that point?\"\n\nNotice that none of these ask what the user would want in a better version of the experience. They're all asking what the user's mental model was during the actual experience. That's the data that's useful for product decisions.\n\n## What Mental Model Data Actually Looks Like\n\nA user's mental model is their internal representation of how a system works. It's not the same as how the system actually works. It's what they believe to be true, based on their past experiences with similar systems and the signals the product gives them.\n\nMental model data surfaces in specific interview moments:\n\n**Vocabulary mismatches**: The user uses a different word for the thing than the product does. \"I went to the account section\" when the product calls it \"profile\". This means their mental model uses different categories.\n\n**Unexpected actions**: The user tries to do something that seems inefficient or wrong from an expert perspective. \"I always export the data to CSV first, then paste it into the spreadsheet.\" Why? Because their mental model of how to get data out of the tool doesn't include the built-in export to sheets feature. The feature exists but the model doesn't include it.\n\n**Attribution errors**: The user attributes a behavior to the wrong cause. \"Sometimes it's slow right after I add a new team member.\" This is probably unrelated, but the user has formed a causal model that says team member addition causes performance issues. Their behavior will be shaped by this model.\n\n**Workarounds as signals**: Any workaround is evidence of a gap between the product's model and the user's model. They're not doing the workaround because they enjoy extra steps. They're doing it because the product doesn't support their actual mental model of how the task should work.\n\n## Structuring an Interview to Get Mental Model Data\n\nA 45-minute interview structured for mental model research looks like this:\n\n**Minutes 1-5: Context setting.** Who are they? What's their role? What does their day-to-day look like in the problem space? This isn't small talk. This is the background that makes their mental model make sense.\n\n**Minutes 5-35: Past behavior story.** One specific, recent event. Walk through it chronologically. Go slow. Ask about every decision point. Resist the temptation to ask about preferences during this section.\n\n**Minutes 35-45: Light preference probing.** Only after the behavioral section. \"Given what you described, what would have made that easier?\" This is where preference questions are useful, because they're now grounded in a specific real experience rather than a hypothetical.\n\nThe temptation is to jump to the preference questions early because they feel more actionable. The behavioral section feels slow and indirect. But the behavioral section is where the useful data is.\n\n## Reading the Signals During the Interview\n\nThe goal during a behavioral interview is to listen for the moments when the user's mental model is revealed. These moments often don't sound significant:\n\n- \"I thought I had to...\" (past tense belief, possibly wrong)\n- \"I didn't know you could...\" (gap in mental model)\n- \"I assumed that...\" (inference, possibly incorrect)\n- \"I figured if I did X, then Y would happen\" (causal model)\n- \"I wasn't sure if...\" (uncertainty in the model)\n\nEach of these is a signal worth following up on. \"You mentioned you weren't sure if clicking that would save your work. What were you expecting to happen?\"\n\nThe answer tells you not just what happened in that specific session but what the user's model of the product's behavior is. And that model is what you're designing for.\n\n## The Synthesis Step\n\nAfter several interviews, the synthesis is not \"users want features A, B, and C.\" It's \"users have a mental model where X works like Y, when it actually works like Z. This mismatch causes them to take inefficient paths and miss the functionality that would serve them.\"\n\nThat framing generates very different design solutions. Instead of adding a feature, you might need to surface an existing capability more prominently. Instead of adding more options, you might need to reduce the vocabulary mismatch so users can find what they're looking for. Instead of adding a tutorial, you might need to change the interaction so it matches the mental model users bring from adjacent tools they already know.\n\nThe product isn't the source of truth for how it works. The user's model is the product's constraint."}
