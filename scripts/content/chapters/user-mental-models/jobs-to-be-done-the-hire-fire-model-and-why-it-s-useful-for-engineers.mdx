{"hook_text": "Users don't buy a product. They hire it to get a job done, and they'll fire it the moment something else does the job better. JTBD reframes every feature decision from 'what do users want?' to 'what are they trying to accomplish?'", "body_mdx": "## The Drill Analogy Everyone Uses, and Why It Actually Works\n\nThere's a story, often attributed to Theodore Levitt, about a hardware store. People don't buy quarter-inch drills. They buy quarter-inch holes. The drill is a means. The hole is the job.\n\nThis is the core of Jobs-to-be-Done theory: users hire products to accomplish a specific goal in their lives. When the product does the job well, they keep it. When something else does the job better, they fire it.\n\nFor engineers, this framing is immediately useful because it changes the unit of analysis. Instead of asking \"what feature should we build?\", the question becomes \"what job is the user trying to get done, and how completely does our product do that job?\"\n\n## What a Job Actually Looks Like\n\nA job has a specific structure. It's not a preference or a feature request. It's a statement of what the user is trying to accomplish, in their own context, with their own constraints.\n\nA well-formed job statement follows this pattern:\n\n```\nWhen [situation], I want to [motivation/goal], so I can [expected outcome].\n```\n\nExamples:\n- When I'm debugging a production incident at 2am, I want to find the root-cause log line without reading 10,000 lines, so I can resolve the issue before the on-call engineer escalates.\n- When I'm onboarding a new engineer, I want to share the exact commands that set up the dev environment, so the new hire can be productive on day one without asking me for help.\n- When I'm reviewing a PR from a junior engineer, I want to leave specific, actionable feedback on each problematic line, so they understand what to change without a back-and-forth.\n\nNotice what's missing: no mention of a specific UI, feature, or product capability. The job is about the outcome, not the mechanism.\n\n## The Hire/Fire Model\n\nProducts get hired and fired. The language is intentional. It implies performance.\n\n```mermaid\nflowchart LR\n    A[User has a job to do] --> B{Current solution adequate?}\n    B -- Yes --> C[Keep using current product]\n    B -- No --> D[Search for better solution]\n    D --> E[Hire new product]\n    E --> F{Does it do the job better?}\n    F -- Yes --> G[Stick with it]\n    F -- No --> H[Fire it / look again]\n```\n\nThe hire decision happens when a user switches to a product because it does their job better than the alternative. The fire decision happens when it stops doing the job well enough, or when something else does it better.\n\nThis matters for engineers because it means product quality is relative to alternatives, not absolute. A product that does the job at 80% of the competition's quality will get fired. A product that does the job at 110% will get hired, even if it has worse UI, fewer features, or costs more.\n\n## Functional, Social, and Emotional Dimensions\n\nEvery job has three layers:\n\n**Functional**: The literal task. \"Make a hole in the wall.\" \"Find the root cause of the bug.\"\n\n**Social**: How the user wants to be perceived by others while doing the job. \"Look competent during the incident.\"\n\n**Emotional**: How the user wants to feel. \"Not be anxious when the pager goes off.\"\n\nEngineers often design only for the functional dimension. But products get hired and fired based on all three. Slack wasn't hired because email was broken. It was hired partly because it made teams feel more connected (emotional) and partly because it made teams look more modern (social).\n\nThis doesn't mean every product needs to solve emotional and social jobs explicitly. It means that when a product fails to do the job, the failure might not be functional. Users might reject a technically superior tool because it makes them look incompetent to colleagues during the transition period.\n\n## How Engineers Use JTBD in Practice\n\n**Reframe feature requests.** When a product manager says \"users want a dark mode\", JTBD asks: what job does dark mode do? The answer might be \"reduce eye strain during late-night coding sessions\". That reframing opens up alternatives: system-level dark mode support, better contrast ratios, reduced animation. The job is the constraint, not the feature.\n\n**Evaluate tradeoffs.** When choosing between two implementation approaches, ask which one does the user's job better. If the job is \"find a bug quickly\", an implementation that adds a 200ms latency to the search tool might fail the job even if every other metric looks fine.\n\n**Spot switching opportunities.** If users are hiring workarounds (copying data into spreadsheets, using a third-party tool alongside yours, maintaining internal wikis), that's a job your product isn't doing. Each workaround is a fired product and a hiring opportunity.\n\n**Write better acceptance criteria.** \"Users can search logs\" is a feature. \"Users can identify the root cause log line for a p0 incident within 90 seconds\" is a job statement. Acceptance criteria written as job statements are much harder to ship mediocre implementations against.\n\n## Common Mistakes\n\nThe most common JTBD mistake is conflating the job with the solution. \"Users want to filter by date\" is not a job. It's a proposed mechanism. The job might be \"understand what changed in the past 24 hours\". Date filtering is one solution to that job. A diff view, an activity feed, or a change log might do the job better.\n\nThe second mistake is defining the job too broadly. \"Make communication easier\" is not a job. It's a market category. Jobs need to be specific enough that you can measure whether the product does them well.\n\nThe third mistake is ignoring context. The same user doing the same task in two different situations has two different jobs. \"Check my messages\" before a meeting is a job of \"confirm who's attending so I can prepare\". \"Check my messages\" during lunch is a job of \"catch up on what I missed so I don't feel out of the loop\". The product that serves one job well might actively fail the other.\n\n## A Starting Point for Any Feature Discussion\n\nBefore the next technical design document or sprint planning session, try writing down the job the feature is supposed to accomplish. Use the \"when/want/so I can\" format. Then ask: how would we know if the product is doing this job well? What metric would move?\n\nIf the answers are vague, the feature probably isn't well-defined yet. That's a much cheaper thing to discover in a design doc than in production."}
