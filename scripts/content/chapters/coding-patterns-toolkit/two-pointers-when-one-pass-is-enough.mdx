  "body_mdx": "## The Invariant Is Everything\n\nTwo pointers doesn't work because of clever coding. It works because of a mathematical property: for some problems, if you know a pair at positions `(i, j)` fails in a particular direction, every pair in that direction also fails. That's the monotonicity invariant, and without it, you don't have a two-pointer solution. You have two variables and an O(n²) loop.\n\nThe proof is mechanical. The two pointers start `n` positions apart and converge. Each iteration moves at least one pointer one step. Total iterations: at most `n`. That's your O(n) guarantee.\n\nThe hard part is convincing yourself, for a specific problem, that you've actually found the invariant and not just gotten lucky on test cases.\n\n## Container With Most Water: Seeing the Invariant Clearly\n\nLeetCode #11 is the textbook case. Given an array of heights, find two lines that contain the most water. Area = min(height[left], height[right]) × (right - left).\n\nStart with pointers at both ends. Now you have a choice: move `left` forward or `right` backward. Which one?\n\nMove the pointer at the **shorter** line. Here's why: the container's height is capped by the shorter line. If you move the taller line's pointer, width decreases and height cannot increase (the shorter line is still the cap). Moving the taller pointer can only reduce area. Moving the shorter pointer might find something taller, which could compensate for the reduced width.\n\n```python\ndef maxArea(height: list[int]) -> int:\n    left, right = 0, len(height) - 1\n    best = 0\n    while left < right:\n        area = min(height[left], height[right]) * (right - left)\n        best = max(best, area)\n        if height[left] < height[right]:\n            left += 1\n        else:\n            right -= 1\n    return best\n```\n\nThe invariant: once we decide `height[left]` is the bottleneck and skip it, no pair involving `left` at its current position can beat what we've already seen or will see. We don't need to check those pairs. That's the skipped work that makes O(n) possible.\n\n## Two Sum II: The Convergent Pattern\n\nLeetCode #167, sorted array version. Sum too small: move `left` right (increase the smaller number). Sum too large: move `right` left (decrease the larger number). Sum equals target: done.\n\n```\n[-2, 0, 1, 3, 5, 8]    target = 6\n  L                R    sum=6  → found\n```\n\nThe invariant is cleaner here because sorting makes it explicit. If `arr[left] + arr[right] < target`, then every pair involving `arr[left]` with a right pointer to the left of `right` is also too small (the array is sorted). Skip the entire row. Same logic for the other direction.\n\nThis is the pattern to internalize: the sorted order makes the monotonicity property obvious. When the array isn't sorted, you have to prove the invariant yourself.\n\n## 3Sum: Two Pointers as a Component\n\nLeetCode #15 is where engineers get tripped up. Finding all unique triplets summing to zero looks like a natural extension, but two pointers alone won't handle uniqueness.\n\nThe approach: sort the array O(n log n), then for each index `i`, run the Two Sum II convergent scan on the remainder. Total: O(n²).\n\nThe trap is duplicate handling. After finding a valid triplet, skip over duplicate values of both `left` and `right` before the next iteration. Fail to do this and you emit duplicates. Do it inside the wrong branch and you miss valid triplets. This is the kind of off-by-one thinking that trips up otherwise solid solutions.\n\n```python\ndef threeSum(nums: list[int]) -> list[list[int]]:\n    nums.sort()\n    result = []\n    for i in range(len(nums) - 2):\n        if i > 0 and nums[i] == nums[i - 1]:\n            continue\n        left, right = i + 1, len(nums) - 1\n        while left < right:\n            s = nums[i] + nums[left] + nums[right]\n            if s == 0:\n                result.append([nums[i], nums[left], nums[right]])\n                while left < right and nums[left] == nums[left + 1]:\n                    left += 1\n                while left < right and nums[right] == nums[right - 1]:\n                    right -= 1\n                left += 1\n                right -= 1\n            elif s < 0:\n                left += 1\n            else:\n                right -= 1\n    return result\n```\n\n## Trapping Rain Water: When Sorted Order Isn't Required\n\nLeetCode #42 is the proof that two pointers doesn't need a sorted array. The input is unsorted elevation data. The O(1) space two-pointer solution nonetheless works.\n\nThe water above any bar is determined by the minimum of the tallest bar to its left and the tallest bar to its right. Track `max_left` and `max_right` as you go.\n\n```\nPointer movement:\n  if max_left < max_right:\n      water += max_left - height[left]   (safe: we know right side has something taller)\n      left += 1\n  else:\n      water += max_right - height[right]\n      right -= 1\n```\n\nThe invariant: when `max_left < max_right`, the water above `left` is definitively bounded by `max_left`. We don't need to know what's between `left` and `right` on the right side, because we know something is at least as tall as `max_right`. That's enough to compute the water level exactly.\n\nCompare the three approaches to this problem:\n\n```\n                     Time    Space   When to prefer\nTwo pointers         O(n)    O(1)    Most cases\nMonotonic stack      O(n)    O(n)    When you need to track bar indices\nDP (prefix arrays)  O(n)    O(n)    Clearest to read, easiest to explain\n```\n\nIn an interview, the two-pointer O(1) solution is optimal. But if you can't reconstruct the invariant under pressure, the DP approach produces a clean correct answer. Getting to O(n) time matters more than shaving O(n) space.\n\n## When Two Pointers Doesn't Apply\n\nThe technique fails when the problem doesn't have the monotonicity property. An unsorted array with no exploitable structure gives you no basis for skipping pairs. Backtracking becomes necessary. You're back to O(n²).\n\nThe quick diagnostic:\n\n```\n1. Can you define what \"too small\" and \"too large\" mean for your pair?\n2. If (i, j) is too small, is every pair (i, j') where j' < j also too small?\n3. If (i, j) is too large, is every pair (i', j) where i' > i also too large?\n\nIf yes to all three → two pointers works. If not → sort first, or try a different approach.\n```\n\n## The Interview Pattern\n\nTwo-pointer problems at Google and Amazon cluster around a handful of recognizable shapes: sum problems on sorted arrays, container/water problems, and in-place array manipulation (removing duplicates, reversing). The signal interviewers look for isn't \"did they know the trick\" but \"can they explain why moving that pointer is safe.\"\n\nIf you move a pointer and can't state what you're eliminating from future consideration, you don't yet understand the solution. The explanation is the solution.\n\nStart with the brute force. State what property of the problem lets you skip work. Then code the two-pointer version as a direct consequence of that property. This sequence tells the interviewer you can reason about algorithm correctness, not just pattern-match to a template."
