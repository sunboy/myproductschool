It covers 56 tasks across retrieval, classification, clustering, re-ranking, and semantic similarity. A model's aggregate MTEB score is a useful prior for general-purpose embeddings, but it's a trap if you treat it as a proxy for your specific retrieval task.\n\nThe aggregate score is an average. A model can rank first by excelling on classification and clustering tasks while being mediocre on retrieval. If your application is retrieval, you should look at the retrieval sub-scores, not the aggregate. And even then, MTEB retrieval tasks are mostly English, mostly general web text. A model optimized on them may not generalize to code, legal text, biomedical literature, or multi-language corpora.\n\n## Dimensions: More Is Not Always Better\n\nEmbedding dimensionality is a design choice with real tradeoffs.\n\nHigher dimensions encode more information but cost more to store and query. A 1536-dim vector from OpenAI's `text-embedding-3-large` requires 6 KB per document (float32). At 100 million documents, that's 600 GB just for the raw vectors, before indexing. A 768-dim model halves that. A 384-dim model cuts it to 150 GB.\n\nThe relationship between dimensions and recall is not linear. For many retrieval tasks, 256-384 dimensions captures most of the useful signal. Beyond 1024, the marginal recall gain per additional dimension drops sharply, while storage and latency costs continue growing linearly.\n\n```\nDimension vs. Recall (illustrative, task-dependent)\n\n1.00 |                              *  *  *  *\n0.95 |                    *  *  *\n0.90 |             *  *\n0.85 |       *\n0.80 | *\n     +-----+-----+-----+-----+-----+-----+-----\n       64   128   256   512   768  1024  1536\n                   Dimensions\n```\n\nThe practical guidance: start with a 768-dim model and measure recall on a held-out query set from your domain. Only move to higher dimensions if recall is insufficient and you've already optimized chunking and indexing.\n\n## Domain Specificity vs. General Purpose\n\nGeneral-purpose models (BGE-large, E5-large, OpenAI's text-embedding-3) are trained on massive web corpora with instruction tuning. They transfer well to diverse tasks without fine-tuning.\n\nDomain-specific models trade breadth for depth:\n\n- **Code**: `code-search-net` style models, StarCoder embeddings, or CodeBERT. These are trained on (code, docstring) pairs and understand identifier naming conventions, comment patterns, and code structure that general models don't.\n- **Biomedical**: BioBERT, PubMedBERT. Trained on PubMed abstracts, they embed medical terminology with the correct neighbor relationships.\n- **Legal**: Legal-BERT, LexLM. Trained on case law and contracts, they separate near-synonym terms that are legally distinct but semantically proximate in general text.\n\nThe failure mode of using a general model in a specialized domain is that domain-specific terms end up embedded in a generic neighborhood. \"Pleading\" (legal) and \"pleading\" (emotional) embed similarly. \"Kernel\" (OS) and \"kernel\" (corn) embed similarly. Retrieval over these terms degrades precisely for the queries that matter most to your users.\n\n## How to Evaluate Before You Commit\n\nThe right evaluation process:\n\n1. Collect 200-500 query-document pairs from real or representative use cases. These should be pairs where you know the document is a good answer to the query.\n2. Embed queries and documents with each candidate model.\n3. For each query, rank all documents by similarity score and compute Recall@K (K=1, 5, 10) and Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR).\n4. Run this on all candidate models and pick the one that wins on your metric, not MTEB's.\n\n```mermaid\nflowchart TD\n    A[\"Collect eval set: 200-500 query-doc pairs\"] --> B[\"Embed with candidate model A\"]\n    A --> C[\"Embed with candidate model B\"]\n    B --> D[\"Compute Recall@10, MRR\"]\n    C --> D\n    D --> E{\"Which wins on your metric?\"}\n    E --> F[\"Pick winner\"]\n    E --> G[\"Consider fine-tuning loser on domain data\"]\n```\n\nThis process takes a few hours to set up and saves weeks of regret. Every team that skips it ends up rebuilding the index after shipping.\n\n## Instruction-Tuned vs. Symmetric Models\n\nSome models (E5, BGE-M3, Instructor) support instruction prefixes that shift the embedding depending on the task:\n\n```python\n# Asymmetric retrieval: query and passage get different instructions\nquery_embedding = model.encode(\n    \"Represent this query for searching relevant passages: \" + query\n)\ndoc_embedding = model.encode(\n    \"Represent this passage for retrieval: \" + document\n)\n```\n\nThis matters because retrieval is asymmetric. A short query and a long document should not be embedded the same way. Instruction-tuned models let you specify the asymmetry explicitly rather than relying on the model to infer it from length alone.\n\nFor symmetric tasks (deduplication, clustering, paraphrase detection), symmetric models without instruction tuning are simpler and often faster.\n\n## Multilingual Models\n\nmGTE, LaBSE, multilingual E5, and SONAR support 100+ languages in a single embedding space. Cross-lingual retrieval, where a query in French retrieves documents in English, is technically possible but quality degrades compared to same-language retrieval.\n\nThe practical question is whether to run separate monolingual models per language or a single multilingual model. Monolingual models outperform multilingual ones in their target language by 5-15% on typical benchmarks. The multilingual model wins on operational simplicity: one index, one model, no per-language routing.\n\nFor most production systems serving multiple languages, the monolingual quality advantage is worth pursuing only after the multilingual baseline is working and you can measure the gap on real traffic. Don't prematurely optimize for recall you can't yet measure."}
