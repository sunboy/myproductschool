{"hook_text": "Chain-of-thought prompting improves accuracy because intermediate reasoning steps constrain the output space, making wrong answers geometrically less likely.", "body_mdx": "## The Accuracy Problem Chain-of-Thought Solves\n\nOn a multi-step arithmetic or logical reasoning question, a language model prompted for a direct answer fails at higher rates than the same model prompted to reason step-by-step before answering. This is not a display effect. The reasoning tokens genuinely change the answer, not just the presentation of the same answer.\n\nWhy? Because each intermediate reasoning token becomes part of the context that conditions the next token. The model cannot contradict its own prior reasoning steps without that contradiction being visible in the token sequence. Correct intermediate steps make incorrect final answers mechanically less probable.\n\n## The Mechanism, Concretely\n\nCompare two generation paths for a math problem:\n\n**Direct answer (no CoT):**\n```\nPrompt: \"Roger has 5 tennis balls. He buys 2 more cans of 3 balls each. How many total?\"\nOutput: \"11\" (correct by accident) or \"8\" or \"10\", sampled from a diffuse distribution\n```\n\n**Chain-of-thought:**\n```\nPrompt: \"Let's think step by step. Roger has 5 tennis balls. He buys 2 cans of 3 balls each...\"\nOutput: \"2 cans × 3 balls = 6 new balls. 5 + 6 = 11. The answer is 11.\"\n```\n\nIn the CoT path, the token `\"6\"` (the product of 2 and 3) appears in context before the final answer. The model cannot now output a final answer that ignores those 6 balls without producing an incoherent token sequence. The intermediate tokens act as soft constraints.\n\n```mermaid\nflowchart TD\n    Q[\"Question tokens\"] --> R1[\"Reasoning step 1\"]\n    R1 --> R2[\"Reasoning step 2\"]\n    R2 --> R3[\"Reasoning step 3\"]\n    R3 --> A[\"Final answer\"]\n    R1 -->|\"constrains\"| A\n    R2 -->|\"constrains\"| A\n    R3 -->|\"constrains\"| A\n```\n\nRemove the intermediate steps and the answer token is sampled from a much wider distribution.\n\n## Zero-Shot CoT vs. Few-Shot CoT\n\nTwo main techniques:\n\n**Zero-shot CoT**: Add `\"Let's think step by step.\"` to the prompt. This phrase activates reasoning behavior without providing examples. It works because the phrase co-occurred with reasoning traces in training data.\n\n**Few-shot CoT**: Include 3-8 examples where each output includes a full reasoning trace before the answer. More reliable than zero-shot CoT, especially for novel task types where the model may not have strong priors about what \"step by step\" means.\n\nFor most production tasks, few-shot CoT with 3-5 carefully constructed examples outperforms zero-shot. Zero-shot is useful when context budget is tight or when the task is simple enough that the model's native reasoning is sufficient.\n\n## When CoT Helps and When It Doesn't\n\nCoT is not universally beneficial. It helps when:\n\n- The task requires multi-step reasoning (math, logic, planning, code generation)\n- The answer depends on intermediate facts that the model must compute, not recall\n- Errors are likely to cascade from early mistakes in a single-step answer\n\nCoT adds noise when:\n\n- The task is simple classification or single-step recall\n- Reasoning traces produce confident-sounding wrong intermediate steps (\"hallucinated CoT\")\n- Context budget is critical and intermediate tokens are not worth the cost\n\nHallucinated CoT is a real failure mode. The model produces a plausible-looking reasoning trace that leads to an incorrect conclusion, and the reasoning makes the wrong answer look more confident. Always validate CoT outputs on tasks where this risk is high.\n\n## Self-Consistency: Running Multiple Reasoning Paths\n\nSelf-consistency samples multiple reasoning paths at `temperature > 0` and takes a majority vote on the final answer. Because wrong answers are typically distributed across many incorrect possibilities while the correct answer concentrates, majority voting improves accuracy without changing the model.\n\n```\nPath 1: \"5 + 6 = 11\" → Answer: 11\nPath 2: \"2 × 3 = 6, 5 + 6 = 11\" → Answer: 11\nPath 3: \"2 cans × 3 = 6 new balls, 5 existing + 6 = 11\" → Answer: 11\nPath 4: \"5 + 2 = 7\" → Answer: 7 (wrong path)\n\nMajority vote: 11 (3/4 paths agree)\n```\n\nThis works because the correct reasoning path is often singular while error paths vary. Self-consistency costs N times the tokens and latency of a single call. Use it when accuracy is worth the price.\n\n## Constructing Good CoT Examples\n\nFor few-shot CoT, example quality determines output quality:\n\n1. Reasoning steps should be minimal and sufficient. Do not include steps that restate the question without advancing the calculation.\n2. Each step should be independently verifiable. If you cannot confirm a step is correct, the example is teaching the wrong path.\n3. The final answer should follow logically from the last step, not introduce new information.\n4. Use consistent terminology throughout. Switching terms mid-reasoning increases the probability the model generates a confused trace.\n\n```\n// Good: minimal, each step advances\n\"Total = existing + purchased\nPurchased = 2 cans × 3 balls/can = 6\nTotal = 5 + 6 = 11\"\n\n// Bad: restates without advancing\n\"Roger has 5 balls. He buys cans. Each can has 3 balls. He buys 2 cans.\nSo he buys some balls. The total is the original plus the new ones.\nThe answer is 11.\"\n```\n\n## Chain-of-Thought in Production\n\nIn production systems, reasoning tokens add cost and latency. Two patterns for managing this:\n\n**Strip the reasoning from the output**: Prompt the model to produce a structured output where the reasoning trace is a field separate from the answer. Parse only the answer field, but log the reasoning for debugging.\n\n**Use a smaller model for reasoning steps, larger for final answer**: Some architectures route reasoning to a cheaper model and only invoke the expensive model for the final synthesis. This reduces cost while preserving most of the accuracy gain.\n\nNeither pattern eliminates the token cost of generating the reasoning. They just control where that cost shows up in your system."}
