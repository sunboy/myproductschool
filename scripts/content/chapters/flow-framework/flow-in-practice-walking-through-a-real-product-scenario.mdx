The four moves aren't sequential steps, they're compressing force.", "body_mdx": "## The Scenario\n\nDriver availability in a mid-tier market has dropped 18% over the past six weeks. Rider wait times are up from an average of 4.2 minutes to 6.8 minutes. The operations team reports that driver sign-ups are holding steady but active hours per driver per week have declined from 22 to 17. A product review is scheduled for Friday. The expectation: come with a recommendation.\n\nThis is a real kind of problem. Not a toy example, not a simplified interview prompt. A metric is moving, multiple signals are present, and there's a time-boxed decision to make.\n\nHere's how FLOW works through it.\n\n## Move 1: Frame\n\nThe tempting frame is the obvious one: \"Driver availability is declining, and we need to fix it.\" That's not a frame. It's a restatement of the symptom.\n\nThe useful question: who is experiencing what friction, and what breaks if it stays broken?\n\nThe sign-up data holds steady. That rules out acquisition as the root problem. Active hours per driver are declining. That means drivers are joining but not driving as much as they used to. The problem is retention of engagement, not acquisition.\n\nWhy are drivers reducing active hours? A few hypotheses:\n\n1. Surge pricing behavior changed, making low-demand hours feel less worthwhile\n2. Competitor incentives are pulling drivers away during peak hours\n3. A recent app change increased friction in the driver experience\n4. Seasonal demand shift is creating dead zones where drivers are sitting idle without fares\n\nThe right frame requires picking the most likely hypothesis and testing it, or running a quick data pull to narrow the field. Suppose the data shows that the decline in active hours is concentrated in the 2pm to 6pm window, which historically had moderate demand. Driver ratings during that window are flat, which rules out service quality issues. Surge activations during that window have decreased.\n\n**Framed problem statement:**\n\nDrivers who historically relied on mid-afternoon demand are reducing active hours because surge events in the 2pm-6pm window have become rare. At current trends, wait times in this market will cross the 8-minute threshold within three weeks, which historically correlates with a 9% drop in rider rebooking rates.\n\nNow there's a named person (mid-afternoon drivers), concrete friction (surge events reduced, hours feel unprofitable), and consequence with a number (8-minute threshold, 9% rebooking risk).\n\n## Move 2: List\n\nFive structurally different options for addressing reduced mid-afternoon driver engagement:\n\n| # | Option | Mechanism | Primary stakeholder served |\n|---|---|---|---|\n| 1 | Guaranteed hourly minimum during 2pm-6pm | Financial floor for drivers willing to stay online | Drivers (financial certainty) |\n| 2 | Demand stimulation: rider promo pricing during off-peak | Increase ride volume to create organic demand for drivers | Riders (price), Drivers (fare volume) |\n| 3 | Predictive shift scheduling with opt-in bonuses | Show drivers when demand is forecast and pay bonus for pre-committed hours | Drivers (planning), Riders (availability) |\n| 4 | Rebalancing fleet allocation from high-supply to this market | Temporarily redirect driver acquisition incentives to this market | Drivers in other markets, Ops |\n| 5 | In-app streak rewards for consistent mid-afternoon hours | Non-financial engagement mechanic to build habit | Drivers (gamification) |\n\nThese are structurally different. Options 1 and 3 are supply-side financial interventions. Option 2 is a demand-side intervention that indirectly solves the supply problem. Option 4 is an operational reallocation. Option 5 is a behavioral mechanic.\n\nDuring listing, no evaluation happens. Option 4 might be expensive. Option 5 might not move the needle. Those are Optimize move questions, not here.\n\n## Move 3: Optimize\n\nThe problem was framed against a specific risk: wait times crossing 8 minutes within three weeks, threatening rebooking rates.\n\n**Named criterion:** Speed of impact. Which option can measurably increase mid-afternoon driver active hours within 14 days?\n\nGuardrails:\n- Cannot exceed $150k in incentive spend for the test period\n- Cannot require an app release (sprint cycle rules out 3-week ship time)\n- Must be reversible if it doesn't work\n\nApplying the criterion:\n\n```mermaid\ngraph TD\n    A[\"Option 1: Guaranteed hourly minimum\"] --> B[\"Fast to deploy via ops dashboard\\nHigh cost, ~$200k/month extrapolated\\nFails cost guardrail\"]\n    C[\"Option 2: Rider promo pricing\"] --> D[\"2-3 day activation\\nWithin budget\\nDemand response takes 1-2 weeks to change driver behavior\"]\n    E[\"Option 3: Predictive shift scheduling\"] --> F[\"Requires app release\\nFails ship-time guardrail\"]\n    G[\"Option 4: Market reallocation\"] --> H[\"Ops change, fast\\nAffects other markets negatively\\nNot reversible easily\"]\n    I[\"Option 5: Streak rewards\"] --> J[\"Requires app release\\nFails ship-time guardrail\"]\n    style B fill:#f97316,color:#fff\n    style D fill:#14b8a6,color:#fff\n    style F fill:#374151,color:#fff\n    style H fill:#374151,color:#fff\n    style J fill:#374151,color:#fff\n```\n\nOptions 1, 3, and 5 fail on guardrails. Option 4 is fast but has negative externalities and low reversibility. Option 2 is the only one that passes all guardrails and is fast enough to matter.\n\n**The sacrifice:** Rider promo pricing compresses margin per ride during the 2pm-6pm window. Average contribution margin drops from $1.82 to approximately $1.20 per ride during the promotion. That's acceptable because the alternative is an 8-minute wait time, which risks 9% rebooking rate decline with a much larger long-term cost.\n\n## Move 4: Win\n\n**Recommendation:**\n\nLaunch a 20% rider discount on rides requested between 2pm and 6pm in [market] for the next 21 days. Targeted via push notification to riders who have booked in this window in the past 90 days.\n\n**Criterion used:** Speed of impact on mid-afternoon driver active hours.\n\n**Primary metric:** Average driver active hours per week during 2pm-6pm window in [market].\n\n**Threshold:** Return to 20+ hours per active driver per week (current: 17).\n\n**Timeline:** 21 days from launch.\n\n**Counter-signal:** If total 2pm-6pm ride requests in [market] don't increase by at least 15% within 7 days, the demand stimulus isn't working and the financial exposure isn't worth continuing.\n\n**Owner:** [Name], operations lead for [market].\n\n**Full Win statement:**\n\n> Rider demand in the 2pm-6pm window is the missing variable. Driver hours fell because surge events dropped, which means drivers in this window stopped seeing profitable opportunities. Stimulating demand from the rider side is the fastest reversible intervention available without a code release. We will know it's working if ride requests in the window increase 15% by day 7. We will know the underlying driver behavior has changed if active hours return to 20+ per week by day 21. If neither happens, we've spent roughly $45k on a test that tells us demand stimulus isn't the lever, and we move to Option 3 in the next sprint.\n\n## What FLOW Produced\n\nAt the start of this scenario, the available information was: availability down 18%, wait times up, driver hours declining.\n\nWithout FLOW, a typical product review would produce: \"We should do something about driver engagement\" followed by a debate between the ops team's preferred solution (Option 1, guaranteed minimums) and the product team's preferred solution (Option 5, streak rewards). The debate would last 90 minutes and resolve by whoever had the most organizational influence, not by which option was best against an explicit criterion.\n\nWith FLOW:\n\n- The frame eliminated acquisition as the problem and focused on engagement retention in a specific time window\n- The list produced five structurally different options rather than two advocacy positions\n- The optimization eliminated three options on guardrails and one on reversibility, leaving one clear choice\n- The Win produced a falsifiable claim with a 7-day early warning signal\n\nTotal time for a prepared team: about 40 minutes. That's not because the framework is magic. It's because each move eliminates a class of confusion that usually shows up as circular debate.\n\n## FLOW Is a Compression Tool\n\nThe four moves don't generate ideas that wouldn't have existed otherwise. They compress the decision-making process by forcing sequence: diagnose before generating, generate before evaluating, evaluate against one criterion before recommending, recommend with a falsifiable claim.\n\nWithout that sequence, teams do all four things simultaneously in a free-for-all that produces debate rather than decisions.\n\nThe scenarios where FLOW matters most are the ones where the answer feels obvious. The obvious answer is almost always anchored on the first idea someone in the room had. FLOW forces the next four ideas into the open and makes sure the comparison is honest.\n\nThat's the whole thing. Frame, List, Optimize, Win. Four moves. One recommendation you can actually be held to."}
