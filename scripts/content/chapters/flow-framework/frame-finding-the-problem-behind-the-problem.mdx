{"hook_text": "A problem statement without a named user, a described friction, and a concrete consequence is just a vague complaint. Framing turns the complaint into something you can actually solve.", "body_mdx": "## What Framing Actually Means\n\nEvery product problem starts somewhere upstream of the place where most teams begin working. The request arrives, the tickets get created, the sprint fills up. The problem with this is that the request is usually a downstream artifact of something that happened further up the chain.\n\nFraming is the deliberate act of tracing from the artifact back to the source. It means pausing before solution mode and asking: whose problem is this, what exactly blocks them, and why does fixing it matter?\n\nThose three questions produce a problem statement. Without all three, you don't have a problem statement. You have a symptom description.\n\n## The Three-Part Structure\n\nA well-framed problem has this structure:\n\n```\n[Person] is trying to [accomplish X].\n[Blocker] is preventing them.\nIf unresolved, [business consequence].\n```\n\nThat's it. That's the whole template. What makes framing hard isn't the template, it's finding the right answers to fill it in.\n\nLet's walk through a real example. Suppose engagement on a messaging feature is declining. Here are two ways to frame it:\n\n**Weak framing:** \"We need to improve engagement on the messaging feature.\"\n\nThis is a metric goal, not a problem statement. It names no person, no friction, and no consequence beyond the abstract word \"engagement.\"\n\n**Strong framing:** \"Casual users who want to respond to a message are abandoning the thread because finding the right reply context takes more than three taps. At current abandonment rates, message initiation from new users drops 12% quarter over quarter, which risks the network effect the feature depends on.\"\n\nNow there's a named person (casual users), a concrete friction (three-tap discovery problem), and a consequence with a number attached (12% quarterly decline, network effect risk).\n\nThe strong version is actionable. The weak version is not.\n\n## Why Framing Gets Skipped\n\nTwo reasons dominate:\n\n**Framing feels slow.** Engineers and PMs who are wired for execution interpret framing as overhead. The request is already here, the team is ready to move, why spend two days diagnosing a problem everyone can see?\n\nThe answer: because the problem everyone can see is almost never the problem that needs solving. The visible symptom is the output of something that happened earlier. Fixing the symptom without finding the source means the symptom comes back.\n\n**The problem feels obvious.** When the answer seems clear, framing looks like ceremony. But the feeling of obviousness is a red flag, not a green light. It usually means the team has defaulted to the first explanation that fit, not the most accurate one.\n\n## The \"Why\" Chain\n\nThe fastest path to a real problem statement is iterating through why until the chain terminates.\n\n```mermaid\ngraph TD\n    A[\"Symptom: Message reply rates falling\"] --> B[\"Why? → Reply composer is buried under two menus\"]\n    B --> C[\"Why? → IA was designed for power users, not casual ones\"]\n    C --> D[\"Why? → No casual-user research was done during V1 design\"]\n    D --> E[\"Root problem: Casual users have no path to a core action\"]\n    style E fill:#0d2e2e,color:#fff\n    style A fill:#f97316,color:#fff\n```\n\nThe first \"why\" produces another symptom. The second or third usually reveals a structural cause. That's where the problem statement lives.\n\nFive whys is a useful heuristic. Three whys usually gets you somewhere meaningful. One why almost never does.\n\n## Framing the Right Person\n\nWho you frame the problem around changes everything about the solution space that follows.\n\nConsider two different framings of a customer support ticket surge:\n\n**Framing A:** \"Support agents are overwhelmed by the volume of billing inquiries.\"\n\n**Framing B:** \"Users who get an unexpected charge are unable to self-serve an explanation, so they default to support tickets.\"\n\nFraming A leads to solutions centered on the support team: better tooling, more agents, smarter triage.\n\nFraming B leads to solutions centered on the user: clearer invoice line items, a self-serve billing history, a help article at the moment of confusion.\n\nThe right framing depends on which problem actually needs solving. But you can't even have that conversation unless you've been explicit about who the person in the problem is.\n\n## The Scope Boundary\n\nA problem statement also needs a scope boundary: what's in and what's out.\n\nWithout a scope boundary, problems expand. \"Users can't find the reply button\" becomes \"the navigation is confusing\" becomes \"we need to redesign the whole app.\" Each expansion is plausibly connected to the original symptom, but none of them is the same problem.\n\nA scope boundary sounds like: \"We're focusing on the first-session experience for users who came through the SMS invite flow, not general navigation.\"\n\nThis isn't about being artificially narrow. It's about being precise enough that you can actually test whether you've solved the problem.\n\n## What a Frame Unlocks\n\nA good frame does two things that compound:\n\nFirst, it makes solution comparison possible. You can only compare solutions against a criterion if the criterion is explicit. Without a frame, every proposed solution is equally valid or invalid because there's no standard to measure against.\n\nSecond, it makes the work falsifiable. A problem statement with a concrete consequence gives you a measure of success. \"If we solve this, message initiation from new users should stop declining.\" That's a testable claim. Untestable claims are not useful.\n\n## A Common Framing Failure\n\nThe most common framing failure: writing a problem statement that describes the absence of a solution.\n\n**Bad:** \"Users don't have a way to filter search results.\"\n\nThis describes the absence of a feature (filters), not an underlying problem. The real problem is: \"Users searching for recent content are getting results from two years ago, leading them to abandon the search and go directly to competitor platforms.\"\n\nThe difference matters because \"users don't have filters\" has exactly one solution (add filters). \"Users are getting irrelevant results\" has many: better default ranking, explicit date filters, a recency toggle, a separate \"recent\" tab, improved indexing.\n\n## Framing Is Not a Meeting\n\nFraming doesn't require a workshop or a stakeholder alignment session or a discovery sprint. It requires a question and an honest answer.\n\nBefore the next ticket gets created, ask: who is experiencing this, what specifically blocks them, and what breaks if it stays broken? Write one sentence for each.\n\nIf you can write all three sentences cleanly, you have a frame. If any of them come out vague, keep pulling the thread.\n\nThe next chapter covers what happens once you have a frame: generating options without immediately anchoring on the first one that comes to mind."}
