The difference is falsifiability.", "body_mdx": "## What a Recommendation Actually Is\n\nAfter framing the problem, listing options, and applying a criterion, there's a moment where someone has to say: this is what we should do. That moment is the Win move.\n\nMost teams reach this moment and produce something like: \"We recommend improving the onboarding flow to increase retention.\" That sounds like a recommendation. It isn't. It's a direction, loosely stated. It has no success condition. It has no failure condition. Six months later, no one can agree whether it worked, because no one agreed in advance what \"worked\" would look like.\n\nA real recommendation makes a falsifiable claim: a statement that the world can confirm or deny. \"DAU/MAU should reach 0.45 within 60 days\" is falsifiable. Day 60 arrives, you check the number, and you know. Either the claim held or it didn't.\n\nFalsifiability is not about being right. It's about being testable. A falsifiable recommendation can be wrong and still be useful, because a team that makes explicit claims learns faster than a team that makes directional suggestions.\n\n## The Three Elements\n\nA falsifiable recommendation has three parts:\n\n```\n1. Metric:    What specific number are we watching?\n2. Threshold: What does success look like numerically?\n3. Timeline:  By when should we see that number?\n```\n\nAll three are required. Missing any one of them produces a non-falsifiable statement:\n\n- Metric only: \"We're watching DAU/MAU.\" (But where does it need to land?)\n- Threshold only: \"DAU/MAU should reach 0.45.\" (By when? If it takes three years, was that a success?)\n- Timeline only: \"We expect improvement within 60 days.\" (Improvement of what? From where to where?)\n\nThe full version: \"DAU/MAU should increase from 0.38 to at least 0.45 within 60 days of shipping the updated onboarding.\" Now someone can check.\n\n## Adding the Counter-Signal\n\nThe strongest version of the Win move also names a counter-signal: the leading indicator that would suggest the recommendation is failing before the main metric has time to move.\n\nThis matters because 60 days is a long time to wait to find out something isn't working. A counter-signal that shows up in two weeks gives the team an early warning.\n\n```\nWe will know this worked if DAU/MAU reaches 0.45 by day 60.\nWe will know it's failing if day-7 retention in the new onboarding cohort\nis below 0.28 by day 14.\n```\n\nThe counter-signal is a proxy that moves faster than the primary metric. Day-7 retention predicts DAU/MAU before DAU/MAU has had time to change. If the proxy is below threshold at day 14, the team can course-correct without waiting the full 60 days.\n\n## Specificity Is Defensibility\n\nThe most common objection to falsifiable recommendations: \"We can't predict exactly what the number will be.\" True. But the specificity of the claim is precisely what makes it defensible.\n\nCompare:\n\n**Non-specific:** \"We expect this to improve retention significantly.\"\n\nIf it improves by 1%, was that significant? If someone says no, the counter-argument is: \"Well, any improvement is significant.\" This conversation has no end.\n\n**Specific:** \"We expect DAU/MAU to increase from 0.38 to 0.45.\"\n\nIf it reaches 0.42, the team can have a useful conversation: Did the framing overestimate the impact? Did something else change during the 60-day window? Was the threshold right, or did we aim too high? These are productive questions that lead to sharper thinking next time.\n\nThe specific prediction, even when wrong, is more useful than the vague one, because it gives the team something concrete to reason against.\n\n```mermaid\ngraph LR\n    A[\"Recommendation made\"] --> B[\"Counter-signal check\\n(Day 14)\"] \n    B -->|\"Proxy below threshold\"| C[\"Early course-correct\"]\n    B -->|\"Proxy on track\"| D[\"Primary metric check\\n(Day 60)\"]\n    D -->|\"Metric hit threshold\"| E[\"Win confirmed\"]\n    D -->|\"Metric missed threshold\"| F[\"Post-mortem\\nAdjust frame or criterion\"]\n    style A fill:#0d2e2e,color:#fff\n    style E fill:#14b8a6,color:#fff\n    style C fill:#f97316,color:#fff\n    style F fill:#374151,color:#fff\n```\n\n## Domain Expertise Shows Up Here\n\nNaming a real metric and a credible threshold requires knowing the domain. This is where technical background becomes an advantage.\n\nAn engineer who has worked in consumer apps knows that a DAU/MAU above 0.4 is healthy for a social product, and that moving it 7 points in 60 days is aggressive but possible with an onboarding change. An engineer who hasn't internalized those benchmarks will either set a target too low (making it trivially achievable) or too high (making it impossible and demoralizing).\n\nThis is not about memorizing industry benchmarks. It's about knowing enough about how the metrics behave that the threshold you name is a real stretch goal, not a random number.\n\nWhen the domain isn't familiar, the fastest path is to look at historical data. What has moved this metric before? By how much? Over what timeline? Use that as a reference class for the current claim.\n\n## Ownership Is the Last Piece\n\nA falsifiable recommendation also needs an owner: one person who is accountable for checking the metric and calling the result.\n\nWithout an owner, falsifiable recommendations become rhetorical devices. The claim sits in a document somewhere. The day 60 passes without anyone checking. Eighteen months later, someone vaguely remembers that \"we shipped something for retention\" but can't say whether it worked.\n\nOwnership is not about blame. It's about ensuring the feedback loop closes. The person who makes the recommendation should be the person who reads the result and draws the conclusion. That person learns something. The team learns something. The organization builds a track record of whether its predictions hold.\n\n**The complete Win structure:**\n\n```\nRecommendation: [Specific action]\nCriterion used: [What we optimized for]\nPrimary metric: [What we're watching]\nThreshold:      [Success number]\nTimeline:       [By when]\nCounter-signal: [Early warning proxy]\nOwner:          [Who checks and reports]\n```\n\n## A Common Win Failure: Satisficing\n\nThe most common failure in the Win move is not vagueness. It's satisficing: picking the recommendation that is good enough rather than the one that is best given the criterion.\n\nThis happens when the team has gone through Frame, List, and Optimize, but by the time they reach Win, everyone is tired and there's pressure to land somewhere. The recommendation that gets written down is the one with the most consensus energy, not the one that scores highest against the named criterion.\n\nThe way to catch this: before writing the recommendation, ask out loud: \"Is this the option we identified as best under the criterion we named?\" If the answer is yes, great. If the answer is \"well, it's close enough,\" there's a drift worth acknowledging. Sometimes satisficing is correct (the difference between the top two options is genuinely small). Make that judgment explicitly rather than by default.\n\n## Making the Recommendation Land\n\nThe Win move is also a communication act. A technically correct recommendation that the decision-maker can't understand or trust won't get adopted.\n\nThe final version of the recommendation should pass a simple test: can the person who needs to approve this action understand, in one paragraph, what we're proposing, why this option over the alternatives, and how we'll know it worked?\n\nIf it takes more than a paragraph to answer those three questions, the recommendation isn't ready yet. Trim the frame, sharpen the metric, name the sacrifice, and try again.\n\nThe next chapter walks through a complete FLOW scenario from a real product context, so you can see how all four moves connect."}
