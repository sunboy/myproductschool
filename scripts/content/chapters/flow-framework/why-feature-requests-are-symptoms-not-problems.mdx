Your job is to take the costume off before deciding whether to build it.", "body_mdx": "## The PM Walks In With a Solution\n\nSomewhere in the history of every product disaster, there's a moment like this one: a product manager shows up at standup with a request. \"We need a notification badge on the activity feed.\" The engineers nod, create a ticket, and build it. Three sprints later, nobody uses it.\n\nThe post-mortem question is almost always the same: why did we build that?\n\nThe answer, almost always: because someone asked for it.\n\nFeature requests are not problems. They are proposed solutions. They contain an assumption baked in, that the requester has diagnosed the root cause correctly and selected the right fix. Most of the time, that assumption is wrong. Not because the person is uninformed, but because problem diagnosis is genuinely hard and most people skip it.\n\n## What a Feature Request Is Actually Saying\n\nWhen a stakeholder says \"we need a notification badge,\" what they are actually reporting is a symptom they experienced: users aren't returning to the activity feed after their first session. The notification badge is the cure they've already prescribed.\n\nThe symptom is the useful part. The prescription is not.\n\nA doctor who accepts a patient's self-diagnosis without examination isn't practicing medicine. A product team that accepts a stakeholder's feature request without examining the underlying problem isn't practicing product thinking.\n\nThe structure looks like this:\n\n```\nSymptom: Users aren't returning to the feed\n         ↓\nDiagnosis (skipped): Why aren't they returning?\n         ↓\nPrescription (presented as the request): Notification badge\n```\n\nYour job is to fill in the skipped step.\n\n## Why Engineers Fall Into This Trap\n\nThere's a specific reason engineers are particularly susceptible to this pattern. Engineers are trained to solve well-defined problems. \"Add a notification badge\" is a well-defined problem. \"Figure out why users aren't returning\" is not.\n\nWell-defined problems feel tractable. Ambiguous problems feel like someone else's job.\n\nBut here's what changes when you become more senior: the expected contribution shifts from solving defined problems to defining them correctly first. The engineers who get promoted to staff or principal are almost never the fastest coders. They're the ones who can look at a feature request and ask the question that makes everyone else in the room pause.\n\n## The Cost of Skipping Diagnosis\n\nBuilding from symptom-level requests has compounding costs:\n\n**You solve the wrong problem.** The notification badge ships. Users still don't return. Now you have a useless feature and the same underlying problem.\n\n**You optimize for the wrong signal.** Teams that build from requests measure whether the feature was built, not whether the underlying problem was solved. \"We shipped the badge\" becomes a success metric, and the actual user behavior gets ignored.\n\n**You accumulate surface area.** Every unexamined feature request adds complexity to the product. That complexity makes future changes harder, slower, and more expensive.\n\n## The Symptom-Problem-Solution Stack\n\nA more honest picture of how product problems actually work:\n\n```mermaid\ngraph TD\n    A[Observable Symptom] --> B[Root Problem]\n    B --> C[Solution Space]\n    C --> D[Specific Feature]\n    style A fill:#f97316,color:#fff\n    style B fill:#0d2e2e,color:#fff\n    style C fill:#14b8a6,color:#fff\n    style D fill:#374151,color:#fff\n```\n\nMost feature requests jump directly from A to D, skipping B and C entirely. The FLOW framework exists to force the skipped steps back into the conversation.\n\n## Recognizing a Feature Request in the Wild\n\nFeature requests come disguised in several forms:\n\n**The direct request.** \"We need a [feature].\" Easiest to spot. The fix: ask what problem this solves for which user.\n\n**The metric mandate.** \"We need to improve retention.\" Sounds like a problem statement, but it's a symptom. Retention declining is the observable output of some underlying friction. Ask where in the user journey the drop-off happens.\n\n**The competitor copy.** \"[Competitor] added [feature], we should too.\" This assumes the competitor solved the same problem you have. They might be solving a completely different one.\n\n**The user request.** \"Users keep asking for [feature].\" User requests are symptom reports. Users are excellent at describing pain. They are not reliable at diagnosing causes or recommending cures.\n\nHenry Ford's apocryphal quote about faster horses applies here: users articulate problems in terms of the solutions they can already imagine. The underlying problem, in that case, was that getting from A to B took too long.\n\n## The Single Question That Changes Everything\n\nWhen a feature request arrives, the highest-value question is:\n\n**\"What would a user have to be experiencing for this to be the right fix?\"**\n\nThis question forces a backward inference from prescription to symptom to diagnosis. It reveals whether the requester has actually traced the chain, or whether they started at D and hoped for the best.\n\nIf someone says \"we need a notification badge,\" and they can clearly answer that question, \"a user would have to be checking the app without knowing there's new content waiting,\" then you're in good shape. That's a real diagnosis.\n\nIf they can't answer it, or their answer reveals a different problem entirely, you've just saved the team a sprint.\n\n## Before FLOW, This Conversation\n\nThe next five chapters walk through a four-move framework for turning a symptom into a recommendation. But the prerequisite for all of it is the habit this chapter is about: stopping at the feature request and asking whether you've actually seen the problem.\n\nA feature request that gets built without examination is a bet with no thesis. You might win, and you'll call it product intuition. You'll lose more often than you win, and you'll blame the users for not appreciating what you built.\n\nThe engineers who compound their impact over time are the ones who make the thesis explicit. Not because they're more cautious, but because explicit theses are testable, and testable bets make teams smarter over time.\n\nStart there."}
