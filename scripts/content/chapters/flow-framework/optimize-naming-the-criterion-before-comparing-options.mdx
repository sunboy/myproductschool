{"hook_text": "Most teams pick their favorite option first, then invent the criterion that justifies it. Naming the criterion before touching the options is the entire discipline of optimization.", "body_mdx": "## The Criterion Comes First\n\nHere's how most product decisions actually happen: five options are on the table. Someone says, \"I think Option 3 is the most scalable.\" Someone else says, \"Option 2 is faster to ship.\" A third person says, \"Option 4 has been requested by three enterprise customers.\" Twenty minutes later, the team has talked about three different criteria without agreeing on any of them, and the decision goes to whoever argues most confidently.\n\nThis is not optimization. It's advocacy dressed up as analysis.\n\nReal optimization requires stating the criterion before any options are evaluated. Not after. Before. Because once options are on the table and people have started forming preferences, the criterion becomes a post-hoc justification rather than an actual standard.\n\n## What a Criterion Is\n\nA criterion is the single quality that matters most for this decision, at this moment, given the current context.\n\nCommon criteria in product decisions:\n\n- **Speed to user value**: Which option gets the fix in front of affected users fastest?\n- **Reversibility**: Which option is easiest to roll back if it doesn't work?\n- **Implementation cost**: Which option consumes the least engineering time?\n- **Revenue impact**: Which option most directly affects paid conversion or expansion?\n- **Metric impact**: Which option moves the specific metric this problem was framed against?\n\nNotice that these are in tension. The fastest option and the most reversible option are usually the same. The highest metric impact option and the lowest implementation cost option are usually not. A team that hasn't agreed on a criterion will fight about these tradeoffs forever because they're using different standards.\n\n## Naming the Sacrifice\n\nThe most rigorous version of the Optimize move has a specific shape:\n\n```\nWe are optimizing for [criterion].\nTo get that, we accept [sacrifice].\nThe sacrifice is acceptable because [reason].\n```\n\nEvery real optimization involves giving something up. Naming what you're giving up and why it's acceptable is what separates a decision from a preference.\n\nExample:\n\n> We are optimizing for how quickly we can learn whether personalized feed ranking increases day-7 retention. To get that, we accept a rough ranking model that won't work well for new users with sparse history. The sacrifice is acceptable because new users are 12% of our weekly active base, and we can address their experience in a follow-on if the hypothesis holds.\n\nThat's a real decision. It names the criterion, names the sacrifice, and gives a reason the sacrifice is acceptable. Someone reading this in six months will understand what was traded and why.\n\nCompare this to: \"We went with the personalization approach because it seemed like the most impactful option.\" That's not a decision record. It's a feeling.\n\n## The Criterion Reveals Hidden Assumptions\n\nWhen you name the criterion explicitly, disagreements surface immediately. This is useful.\n\n```mermaid\ngraph TD\n    A[\"Options on the table\"] --> B[\"Name the criterion\"]\n    B --> C{\"Team agrees?\"}\n    C -->|Yes| D[\"Apply criterion to compare options\"]\n    C -->|No| E[\"Surface the disagreement\"]\n    E --> F[\"Is the disagreement about strategy?\"]\n    F -->|Yes| G[\"Escalate to strategy decision\"]\n    F -->|No| H[\"Align on current context, then name criterion\"]\n    H --> D\n    style B fill:#0d2e2e,color:#fff\n    style D fill:#14b8a6,color:#fff\n    style G fill:#f97316,color:#fff\n```\n\nSuppose the framed problem is a 15% drop in payment completion rates in the checkout flow. The team generates five options. Then someone proposes optimizing for implementation speed and someone else argues the criterion should be maximum reduction in drop-off rate.\n\nThat's a real disagreement. It usually means one person believes there's a roadmap constraint that makes speed non-negotiable, and the other believes the metric impact is large enough to justify a longer timeline. Both are valid positions. But they can't both be the criterion.\n\nSurfacing this disagreement explicitly is progress. Leaving it implicit means the team will circle back to the same fight after the sprint is over.\n\n## Taste vs. Tradeoff\n\nThere's a distinction worth naming: the difference between a tradeoff and a preference.\n\nA tradeoff involves a real cost on one dimension to gain something on another. \"We take a 3-week implementation hit to get a solution that handles edge cases cleanly\" is a tradeoff. There's a real sacrifice with a real benefit.\n\nA preference is when someone says \"Option 2 just feels cleaner\" without naming what it costs or what it gains. Preferences are not invalid, but they are not tradeoffs. A team that confuses preference for tradeoff will make decisions that can't be explained to anyone outside the room.\n\nThe test: if you can't name what the sacrifice is, it's a preference. If the sacrifice is \"it costs more time\" but you can't say how much more or why that time matters, it's a soft preference. Push toward quantification.\n\n## Guardrails Are Not Criteria\n\nA guardrail is a constraint that no option can violate. A criterion is what you're maximizing across options that all satisfy the constraints.\n\nThe distinction matters:\n\n- **Guardrail**: No option can increase page load time by more than 200ms\n- **Criterion**: Among options that satisfy the guardrail, which maximizes conversion rate?\n\nTeams that mix up guardrails and criteria end up with confused comparisons. \"Option 3 is out because it's expensive\" is a guardrail check (if the budget is truly fixed). \"Option 3 is worse because it's expensive\" is a criterion comparison (cost is the thing we're optimizing against).\n\nList the guardrails first. Then apply the criterion. This produces cleaner comparisons.\n\n## Applying the Criterion to the Options\n\nOnce the criterion is named, the comparison is mostly mechanical. Score each option against the criterion. The highest score wins, subject to the guardrails.\n\nFor the checkout drop-off problem, if the criterion is \"fastest time to validate whether the fix works\":\n\n| Option | Time to test | Notes |\n|---|---|---|\n| Simplify form fields | 1 sprint | A/B testable immediately |\n| Add progress indicator | 1 sprint | Easy to instrument |\n| Redesign payment page | 4 sprints | Biggest potential impact, hardest to ship |\n| Add Apple Pay | 2 sprints | Only benefits iOS users |\n| Improve error messages | 1 sprint | Narrow scope, quick win |\n\nAgainst a speed-to-learn criterion, the first, second, and fifth options are all viable. The choice between them is a secondary decision, and that secondary comparison can be done with a different criterion (e.g., estimated impact size, or implementation risk).\n\nThis process is fast. A team with a clear criterion and five options can get to a recommendation in thirty minutes. Without a criterion, the same conversation takes three days and two meetings.\n\n## When the Criterion Itself Is Wrong\n\nSometimes the named criterion isn't actually what the organization cares about right now. Speed-to-learn is the right criterion in an early-stage product. Revenue impact is the right criterion when the product is mature and growth has plateaued.\n\nIf the team keeps rejecting the named criterion, it's usually a signal that the underlying strategy is unclear: what are we actually trying to achieve this quarter, and why? That's a strategy conversation, not an optimization conversation. It needs to happen before the criterion can be named confidently.\n\nThe Optimize move doesn't resolve unclear strategy. It surfaces it, which is the first step toward resolving it.\n\n## From Optimize to Win\n\nOnce one option is clearly preferable under the named criterion, the question is: how do you make a recommendation that can actually be held accountable?\n\nThat's the Win move. A recommendation isn't useful if it can't be tested. The next chapter covers what it takes to make a recommendation falsifiable."}
