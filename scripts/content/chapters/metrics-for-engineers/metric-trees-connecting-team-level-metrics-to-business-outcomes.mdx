Without it, a team can ship features that move their own number and hurt the company. With it, everyone can see whether local progress is adding up to global progress.",
  "body_mdx": "## What a Metric Tree Is\n\nA metric tree is a hierarchical decomposition of a business outcome into the component metrics that drive it. At the root is a high-level outcome, typically revenue, retention, or something like monthly active users. At the leaves are team-level metrics that individual teams actually control. The tree makes explicit how leaf metrics aggregate into the root.\n\nThe value is not in the diagram. The value is in the decomposition logic, which forces three things to be true simultaneously:\n\n1. Every team metric is traceable to a business outcome.\n2. Every business outcome is decomposable into metrics teams can act on.\n3. The math holds. If all leaf metrics improve, the root metric improves.\n\nWithout a metric tree, teams can improve their own numbers while the business outcome stays flat or declines. Feature adoption increases; revenue per user declines because adopted features cannibalize higher-margin ones. Support ticket deflection improves; retention declines because users who would have raised a ticket instead quietly churn. The local optima do not sum to a global optimum.\n\n## The Decomposition Method\n\nStart at the business outcome and apply multiplicative or additive decomposition recursively until reaching metrics that individual teams control.\n\n**Multiplicative decomposition** is common for revenue:\n\n```\nRevenue = Users × Conversion Rate × Average Revenue Per User\n```\n\nEach factor can be decomposed further:\n\n```\nUsers        = New Users + Returning Users\nConversion   = Trial-to-Paid Rate (for freemium)\nARPU         = Plan Mix × Average Price per Plan\n```\n\n**Additive decomposition** is common for user counts:\n\n```\nMAU (this month) = MAU (last month) + New + Reactivated - Churned\n```\n\nDecompose each component until reaching a metric a specific team can act on. \"Reactivated\" might become \"win-back campaign send rate × win-back conversion rate.\" Each factor is owned by someone.\n\n## A Worked Example\n\nConsider a B2B SaaS product. The company metric is Annual Recurring Revenue. Decompose it:\n\n```\nARR = Number of Accounts × ACV\n```\n\nDecompose each:\n\n```\nNumber of Accounts = New Accounts - Churned Accounts\nACV               = Plan Mix × Average Price\n```\n\nDecompose further:\n\n```\nNew Accounts   = Sales Qualified Leads × Win Rate\nChurned Accounts = Accounts at Risk × Churn Probability\nPlan Mix       = Seats × Feature Adoption Rate\n```\n\nNow ask: which teams own each leaf?\n\n- Sales team: Win Rate\n- Marketing team: Sales Qualified Leads\n- Customer Success: Accounts at Risk, Churn Probability\n- Product (growth): Seats (expansion within accounts), Feature Adoption Rate\n\n```mermaid\nflowchart TD\n    A[\"ARR\"] --> B[\"New Accounts\"]\n    A --> C[\"ACV\"]\n    A --> D[\"Churned Accounts\"]\n    B --> E[\"SQLs\\n(Marketing)\"]\n    B --> F[\"Win Rate\\n(Sales)\"]\n    C --> G[\"Seats\\n(Product Growth)\"]\n    C --> H[\"Feature Adoption\\n(Product Growth)\"]\n    D --> I[\"Accounts at Risk\\n(Customer Success)\"]\n    D --> J[\"Churn Probability\\n(Customer Success)\"]\n    style A fill:#0d2e2e,color:#fff\n    style B fill:#134e4a,color:#fff\n    style C fill:#134e4a,color:#fff\n    style D fill:#134e4a,color:#fff\n```\n\nEach team's metric is now visible in the tree. A change in feature adoption affects ACV, which affects ARR. The magnitude of the effect can be estimated: if average ACV is $12,000 and feature adoption increases by 10%, and that drives a 5% increase in seat expansion, the ARR impact is calculable.\n\n## Making the Math Work\n\nThe tree is a model, not a guarantee. For it to be useful, the relationships between levels need at least rough validation.\n\nFor each edge in the tree, ask: what is the historical relationship between this metric and the one above it? If feature adoption increases by 10%, what has happened to ACV historically? Even a rough estimate, derived from historical cohort analysis, makes the model useful for prioritization.\n\n```\nFeature adoption → ACV relationship:\n  Cohorts with >3 features adopted: ACV = $15,200 avg\n  Cohorts with ≤3 features adopted: ACV = $11,400 avg\n  Delta: ~$3,800 (33% premium)\n  Rough model: every 1pp increase in adoption → ~$380 ACV impact per account\n```\n\nThese estimates will be wrong. They will be less wrong than no estimate at all. And they force the team to think about whether the metric they are optimizing is actually connected to the business outcome, and by approximately how much.\n\n## The Prioritization Use Case\n\nOnce the tree has rough coefficients, it answers prioritization questions directly.\n\nShould the product team focus on feature adoption or seat expansion? Run the math:\n\n```\nOption A: Increase feature adoption by 5pp across 1,000 accounts\n  → ~$380 ACV impact × 1,000 accounts × 5pp improvement = $1.9M ARR potential\n\nOption B: Increase seats per account by 0.5 for 1,000 accounts at $4,000/seat\n  → 500 seats × $4,000 = $2.0M ARR potential\n```\n\nThe numbers are rough. But the tree makes the question answerable. Without it, the choice defaults to whoever argues most persuasively in the planning meeting.\n\n## Common Mistakes in Metric Trees\n\n**Missing the denominator.** Teams track absolute numbers instead of rates. \"We added 500 new accounts\" sounds great. \"We added 500 new accounts while churning 600\" looks different. Rate metrics tend to be more stable and more informative than absolute counts in trees.\n\n**Leaf metrics too far from team control.** A leaf metric is not useful if the team cannot directly influence it. \"Market demand\" is not a leaf metric. \"Sales qualified leads from content marketing\" is. Every leaf must connect directly to a team action.\n\n**No feedback loop from root to leaves.** A metric tree that is defined once and never updated becomes stale as the business model evolves. Set a cadence to review whether the decomposition logic still holds.\n\n**Metric trees used as reporting theater.** The tree is a planning and prioritization tool. If it is built and then used only for quarterly slide decks, it has not changed how the team works. The test is whether teams reference it when deciding what to build next.\n\n## Connecting to North Star\n\nNorth Star Metrics are the most commonly misused framing in product. The idea is sound: identify the single metric that best predicts long-term business success and orient the company around it. The execution tends to fail because teams treat the North Star as the only metric that matters.\n\nThe metric tree is what makes a North Star operational. The North Star sits at or near the root of the tree. Teams at the leaves cannot act on it directly. The tree shows them which leaf metric they own, how it connects to the North Star, and what movement in their metric implies for the overall number.\n\nWithout the tree, the North Star is a poster on the wall. With the tree, it is a navigational instrument.\n\n## When to Build One\n\nA metric tree is most valuable when:\n\n- The company has more than two or three teams working independently.\n- Teams are showing local metric improvements but business outcomes are stagnant.\n- Prioritization debates are happening without a shared quantitative framework.\n- New features are being shipped without a clear line to business outcomes.\n\nBuild the first version quickly. A rough tree is better than no tree. It will be wrong in places. Update it when the errors become obvious. The value is in the process of building it, which forces clarity about which metrics actually matter and who owns them."
