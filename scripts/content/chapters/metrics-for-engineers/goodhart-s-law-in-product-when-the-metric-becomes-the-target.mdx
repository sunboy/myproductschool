  "hook_text": "Goodhart's Law: when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. The moment a metric gains organizational weight, the system starts optimizing for the metric instead of the thing the metric was measuring.",
  "body_mdx": "## The Formulation\n\nCharles Goodhart was a Bank of England economist. In 1975, he observed that monetary indicators used for policy targets lose their reliability once they become the target. The generalized form, popularized by Marilyn Strathern, is blunter: \"When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure.\"\n\nIn product, this plays out constantly. DAU becomes a target, so teams send daily push notifications to inflate it. App store rating becomes a target, so prompts are timed for moments of peak user satisfaction. Ticket closure rate becomes a target in support, so agents close tickets without resolving the underlying issue and wait for the user to reply.\n\nThe metric stops measuring the thing it was supposed to measure. It starts measuring how hard the team is gaming the metric.\n\n## Why This Happens\n\nMetrics are proxies. They are imperfect representations of something that matters. DAU does not measure user value; it measures a count of users who opened the app. App store rating does not measure satisfaction; it measures which users were prompted to rate the app and at what moment. These are useful proxies when the team is not optimizing directly for them. They break when the team is.\n\nThe mechanism is rational. If you tie engineer performance reviews to lines of code committed, engineers write more verbose code. If you tie product manager compensation to feature delivery, PMs deliver features without validating whether users want them. Every agent in the system responds to the incentive structure.\n\nThe proxy breaks because optimizing the proxy is usually cheaper than improving the underlying thing. It is easier to send push notifications than to build a product users open voluntarily. It is easier to prompt users for ratings after a positive event than to make every interaction positive. The gap between the metric and the thing it measures creates an arbitrage opportunity, and optimization closes that gap.\n\n```mermaid\nflowchart LR\n    A[\"Underlying thing\n    (user value)\"] -->|\"correlates with\"| B[\"Proxy metric\n    (DAU)\"]\n    B -->|\"becomes target\"| C[\"Optimization pressure\"]\n    C -->|\"finds cheaper path\"| D[\"Game the proxy\n    (push notifications)\"]\n    D -->|\"degrades\"| A\n    D -->|\"inflates\"| B\n    style D fill:#7f1d1d,color:#fff\n```\n\n## Product Examples\n\n**Daily Active Users.** The proxy for engagement. Teams ship daily streaks, daily challenges, daily push notifications. Users open the app to dismiss the notification and immediately close it. DAU increases. Time spent in the app drops. Actual engagement, measured by tasks completed or value derived, declines. Twitter and Facebook both received criticism for designs that were built to maximize opens while delivering less and less actual value per open.\n\n**Engagement rate on content.** A proxy for content quality. Optimizing it directly produces sensational, emotionally activating content that users engage with and then regret. The metric moved. The underlying thing (content users find valuable) degraded. YouTube's recommendation algorithm spent years optimizing for watch time and was criticized for driving viewers toward increasingly extreme content that held attention without providing value.\n\n**Sprint velocity.** A proxy for team productivity. When used as a target, teams inflate story point estimates, break work into smaller chunks that score higher, and deprioritize unglamorous but necessary work (documentation, technical debt, testing) that is hard to score. Velocity goes up. Actual output per developer may not.\n\n**Test coverage percentage.** A proxy for code quality. Optimizing it directly produces tests that cover lines without asserting meaningful behavior. 90% coverage with trivial tests is worse than 60% coverage with tests that actually catch bugs, because the 90% creates false confidence.\n\n## Detection Patterns\n\nGoodhart violations have identifiable signatures. Look for:\n\n**Metric moves without underlying reality moving.** DAU increases while revenue per DAU decreases. Ticket closure rate increases while repeat contact rate (the same user reopening a ticket) increases. The proxy moved in the right direction; the thing the proxy measured moved in the wrong direction.\n\n**Metric moves contradict qualitative signals.** NPS is rising but qualitative interviews surface consistent frustration. The metric is being gamed in a way that qualitative research is not.\n\n**Metric moves correlate with incentive changes.** Velocity increased after velocity became a performance metric. That is not a coincidence worth celebrating.\n\n**Edge cases in the metric definition are being exploited.** The definition of \"active\" in DAU was changed to include users who received a push notification, not just users who opened the app. The metric changed but the definition changed to protect it.\n\n## The Structural Fix\n\nGoodhart's Law does not mean metrics are useless. It means no single metric should be the sole target.\n\n**Pair metrics that counterbalance each other.** If DAU is a target, pair it with session quality metrics like tasks completed, time-to-value, or explicit satisfaction signals. Gaming DAU by sending more notifications will degrade session quality. The pair is harder to game simultaneously.\n\n**Separate the metric from the incentive.** Teams can track a metric without being rewarded for moving it. When the metric is used for monitoring and investigation rather than as a performance target, the optimization pressure decreases.\n\n**Rotate the proxy.** No proxy perfectly captures the underlying reality forever. Plan to revisit metric definitions annually. Ask whether the metric is still measuring what it was designed to measure or whether optimization pressure has degraded the relationship.\n\n**Use qualitative as a check.** User research, support ticket themes, and customer interviews are expensive to game. If quantitative metrics are moving positively and qualitative signals are not, the quantitative metric has been compromised.\n\n## The Deeper Point\n\nThe underlying thing you care about is usually not directly measurable. User value. Trust. Long-term sustainable engagement. These require proxies. The proxies are imperfect. When organizations forget that the proxy is not the thing, they optimize the proxy at the expense of the thing.\n\nGoodhart's Law is not a law of metrics. It is a law of incentives. The metric is fine. The problem is treating it as if moving the metric is the same as improving reality. These are different activities. One is product work. The other is metric management.\n\nBe suspicious of any metric that is moving consistently in the right direction. Ask what someone would have to do to make this metric look this good without improving the underlying thing. If the answer is \"easy,\" the metric is probably not measuring what you think."
