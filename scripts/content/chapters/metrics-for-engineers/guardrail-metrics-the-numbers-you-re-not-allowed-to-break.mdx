  "body_mdx": "## What Guardrail Metrics Are For\n\nEvery experiment has a primary metric: the thing you are trying to move. But unconstrained optimization of a single metric will, eventually, break something else. Activation experiments that push users through onboarding too fast produce confused users who churn at day 7 instead of day 1. Revenue experiments that reduce friction in checkout increase fraud. Engagement experiments that add notifications spike unsubscribe rates.\n\nGuardrail metrics are the numbers you agree not to break before the experiment starts. They are constraints, not goals. The goal is to improve the primary metric. The constraint is to do it without degrading any guardrails past their defined thresholds.\n\nA guardrail without a threshold is not a guardrail. \"We will monitor support tickets\" is not a constraint. \"Support tickets must not increase more than 10% versus control\" is a constraint. The threshold is what makes it enforceable.\n\n## The Anatomy of a Guardrail\n\nA well-formed guardrail has three components:\n\n1. **The metric.** What is being measured. Support ticket volume, response time, error rate, opt-out rate, revenue per user.\n2. **The direction.** Whether this metric should not increase or should not decrease.\n3. **The threshold.** The maximum tolerable movement before the experiment is stopped.\n\n```\nGuardrail: Support ticket volume\nDirection: Must not increase\nThreshold: No more than +10% versus control\nConsequence: Experiment halted, changes reverted if breached\n```\n\nThe consequence clause matters. A guardrail with no consequence is theater. Teams need to agree in advance that a breach stops the experiment. Making that decision during a breach, when the pressure is to ship, produces rationalization instead of discipline.\n\n## Choosing Guardrail Metrics\n\nThe question for selecting guardrails is: what could a successful experiment on the primary metric accidentally break?\n\nThink systematically about second-order effects. An activation experiment might harm:\n\n- **Support ticket volume** (users confused by a new flow seeking help)\n- **Day-30 retention** (users pushed through onboarding before they understood the product)\n- **Revenue per user** (users who activate on a simplified flow may be lower-value)\n- **Error rate** (new code paths introduced instability)\n\nAn engagement experiment might harm:\n\n- **Opt-out rate** (too many notifications)\n- **Session length on subsequent visits** (high engagement in one session depletes intent)\n- **App store rating** (users who feel manipulated leave negative reviews)\n\n```mermaid\nflowchart TD\n    A[\"Primary Metric: Activation Rate\"] --> B{\"Second-order effects?\"}\n    B --> C[\"User confusion\\n→ support tickets\"]\n    B --> D[\"Rushed onboarding\\n→ 30-day churn\"]\n    B --> E[\"Simplified flow\\n→ lower ACV users\"]\n    B --> F[\"New code paths\\n→ error rate\"]\n    C --> G[\"Guardrail: Support tickets ≤ +10%\"]\n    D --> H[\"Guardrail: Day-30 retention ≥ -1%\"]\n    E --> I[\"Guardrail: Revenue per user ≥ -5%\"]\n    F --> J[\"Guardrail: Error rate ≤ +0.1%\"]\n```\n\nA useful exercise before any significant experiment: list every team that could be affected by the change. Each affected team's primary metric is a candidate guardrail.\n\n## Guardrails Versus Secondary Metrics\n\nThe difference between a guardrail and a secondary success metric is behavioral: guardrails stop experiments, secondary metrics inform decisions.\n\nA secondary metric is something the team tracks alongside the primary metric to understand the experiment better. If it moves, the team updates its understanding. If the guardrail moves past its threshold, the experiment is stopped.\n\nThis distinction matters for organizational culture. When everything is a secondary metric, teams rationalize away negative signals. \"Yes, support tickets went up 30%, but activation improved significantly and support volume will normalize.\" That rationalization may be correct. It may also be wrong, and you will not know for 90 days when churn shows the cost.\n\nGuardrails exist to prevent rationalization. They are pre-commitments.\n\n## Setting Thresholds: The Two Failure Modes\n\nSet thresholds too tight and every experiment fails its guardrail. Practical optimization becomes impossible because noise in low-volume metrics triggers false halts.\n\nSet thresholds too loose and guardrails are meaningless. A 50% increase in support tickets is not a guardrail; it is a post-hoc excuse.\n\nThe calibration method: look at the historical variance of the metric over the experiment period. If support ticket volume varies by plus or minus 8% week over week due to normal seasonality, a 10% threshold catches meaningful degradation while allowing for noise. If it varies by plus or minus 20%, a 10% threshold will produce false positives constantly.\n\nTypical thresholds in practice:\n\n| Metric Type | Common Threshold Range |\n|---|---|\n| Error rate | ≤ +0.1 to +0.5 percentage points |\n| Support ticket volume | ≤ +5% to +15% |\n| Opt-out / unsubscribe rate | ≤ +5% to +20% |\n| Retention (30-day) | ≥ -1% to -2% |\n| Revenue per user | ≥ -3% to -5% |\n\nThese are starting points, not rules. Calibrate against your product's noise levels.\n\n## The Organizational Difficulty\n\nGuardrails are politically uncomfortable because they give other teams veto power over your experiments. If the support team's ticket volume is a guardrail on the growth team's activation experiment, the growth team's experiment can be stopped by a metric the growth team does not own.\n\nThis is the point. It is also why guardrails require organizational buy-in before they are useful. A team that can override a guardrail breach by claiming it is not significant has not actually committed to the constraint.\n\nThe flip side: teams whose metrics are used as guardrails gain visibility into experiments that affect them. That visibility is valuable for coordination and often surfaces dependencies that would otherwise be invisible.\n\n## Guardrails in Incident Response\n\nThe same logic applies outside experiments. Operational guardrails are the system health metrics that trigger escalation. Error rate above 1% for more than five minutes: page the on-call. P99 latency above 500ms for more than two minutes: automatic circuit breaker trips.\n\nThese are guardrails on deployment and configuration changes. Without them, the primary metric (uptime, feature completeness) gets optimized while latency and error rates degrade gradually until they become incidents.\n\nThe pattern is identical. Define the constraint. Define the threshold. Define the consequence. Commit in advance.\n\n## The Pre-Experiment Checklist\n\nBefore any experiment ships, confirm:\n\n- Primary metric is defined and measurable.\n- Guardrail metrics are listed with explicit thresholds.\n- Monitoring is in place to surface guardrail breaches in real time.\n- The team has agreed that breaching a guardrail stops the experiment.\n- Review date is set to confirm guardrails held for the full experiment window.\n\nThe last point matters. A guardrail that is not breached in 48 hours but is breached on day 12 is still a breach. Monitor for the entire experiment duration, not just the launch window."
