  "body_mdx": "## The Shape of the Problem\n\nTransformers don't read the way humans do. Attention in causal models is biased toward the beginning and end of a sequence. Liu et al. (2024) demonstrated this with a simple key-value retrieval task: models answered correctly over 95% of the time when the target was near the start or end of the context, but dropped to roughly 20% accuracy when the same information sat in positions 5-6 of a 10-item list. This U-shaped attention curve holds regardless of window size. A 200K token context window doesn't flatten the curve; it just moves the dead zone further from both ends.\n\nThis has a direct consequence for how you truncate. Naive suffix truncation, dropping text from the end, preserves the beginning but destroys the recency signal. Naive prefix truncation, dropping text from the start, preserves recent context but loses setup and instructions. Middle truncation is the least popular option but often the most defensible for retrieval tasks, because it keeps both boundary zones where the model reliably attends.\n\n```\nContext attention distribution (U-shaped)\n\nHigh  |*                               *\n      |  *                           *\n      |    *                       *\n      |      *                   *\nLow   |        *  *  *  *  *  *\n       [START]         [MID]       [END]\n       → put your most critical content here, not in the middle\n```\n\n## The Four Truncation Strategies\n\n**Suffix truncation** is what most developers reach for first. Keep everything up to some token limit, drop the rest. It works when the end of the content is expendable, like chat history beyond the last N turns. It fails when recent context is load-bearing.\n\n**Prefix truncation** reverses that logic. Keep the tail, drop the head. Useful for sliding-window conversational memory, where older turns matter less. `ConversationBufferWindowMemory` in LangChain implements this directly: track the last k interactions, discard the oldest when the limit is hit. The tradeoff is real: you lose the original framing and any instructions that were front-loaded.\n\n**Middle removal** keeps both ends and removes the center block. This is the right default for RAG pipelines where you've retrieved multiple documents and need to rank them. Put your highest-confidence documents at positions 1 and N. Put weaker candidates in the middle where the model will partially discount them anyway, or drop them entirely.\n\n**Semantic truncation** is more expensive but more precise. Instead of position-based cuts, score each chunk by relevance to the current query and drop the lowest scorers regardless of position. This requires a fast bi-encoder or a cross-encoder reranker. The Elasticsearch pattern for this is retrieve 100 candidates with dense embeddings, rerank with a cross-encoder to get the top 10, then apply positional ordering on those 10 before injecting into context. The cost is roughly 110 scoring operations instead of 1 million, a 10,000x reduction.\n\n```mermaid\nflowchart LR\n    Q[Query] --> EMB[Bi-encoder\\nembedding retrieval\\n~100 candidates]\n    EMB --> RERANK[Cross-encoder\\nreranking\\ntop 10]\n    RERANK --> ORDER[Positional ordering\\nhighest score → position 1\\nsecond highest → position N\\nrest in middle]\n    ORDER --> CTX[Context window\\nready for LLM]\n```\n\n## The Budget Before You Truncate\n\nTruncation decisions only make sense inside a token budget. A practical budget for a Claude Sonnet model with a 200K token window:\n\n- System prompt: 10-15K tokens (cached after the first call, costing 10% of base input price on reads)\n- Retrieved context: 60-80K tokens\n- Conversation history: 20-30K tokens\n- Reserve for output: 40-60K tokens\n- Safety buffer (never exceed 75-80% of max): 10-20K tokens\n\nThe last item is not optional. Context beyond roughly 80% capacity degrades response quality in ways that are hard to detect, models become repetitive, contradict earlier turns, and lose track of instructions. Claude Code users regularly hit this ceiling within 10-15 development interactions on large codebases. The window filled, not empty, is the bug.\n\nCache your system prompt. A 10,000-token system prompt against 1,000 daily requests is 10 million input tokens per day at full price. With Anthropic's 5-minute cache at 10% of base input cost on reads, that drops to roughly 1 million effective tokens once the cache is warm. The write cost is 25% above base, but break-even comes after 1-2 cache reads, which happen within the first minute of a session with multiple turns.\n\n## What to Keep When Space Runs Out\n\nPrioritization in descending order for most applications:\n\n1. System prompt and task instructions (always, cached if possible)\n2. The user's most recent query\n3. The highest-relevance retrieved chunks, placed at positions 1 and N\n4. Recent conversation turns (last 2-3 exchanges)\n5. Earlier conversation history, summarized if it must be preserved at all\n\nSummarization is worth the cost when history contains causally load-bearing facts, a decision made five turns ago that still governs behavior now. A sliding window without summarization loses that entirely. A summary trades fidelity for persistence.\n\nOne thing to verify in any pipeline: your retrieved chunks are ordered by relevance, not by the order they came back from the vector store. The default response order from most ANN indexes is internal to the index structure, not semantic rank. Reorder explicitly before you inject, or you're randomizing what the model attends to.\n\nThe lost-in-the-middle problem won't be solved by the next generation of models with larger context windows. Positional encoding is structural, not incidental. Work with the architecture: put what matters at the edges, keep the middle for supporting evidence, and truncate the rest before it crowds out the signal you actually need."
