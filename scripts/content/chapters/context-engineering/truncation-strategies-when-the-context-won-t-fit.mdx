  "body_mdx": "## The Problem Isn't Length, It's Position\n\nLiu et al. (2024) ran a clean experiment: give a model a list of key-value pairs, ask it to retrieve a specific value. When the target pair was at position 1 or 10, accuracy held above 95%. When it was at positions 5-6, accuracy fell to around 20%.\n\nThe context window wasn't full. The information was present. The model just didn't attend to it.\n\nThis is the lost-in-the-middle problem, and it persists across model sizes and context lengths. A 200K token window doesn't fix it, the U-shaped attention bias is architectural. Causal masking in transformers biases attention toward earlier positions, and deeper layers amplify this. More context can actually make it worse, because there's more middle to get lost in.\n\nThe practical implication: once you understand this, the question isn't just \"what do I truncate?\" but \"where does what I keep end up in the prompt?\"\n\n## Three Truncation Strategies\n\nEvery strategy makes a different bet about what information is most important.\n\n**Suffix truncation** (drop from the end) is the default in most naive implementations. It preserves the system prompt and early conversation turns, which is correct behavior when instructions and early context are load-bearing. The risk: in a long conversation, recent user messages get cut, and the model responds to stale context. For chat applications where the last few turns drive the reply, this is exactly backwards.\n\n**Prefix truncation** (drop from the beginning) keeps recent messages intact. It makes sense for sliding-window conversation management, the last k turns stay in, everything older drops. LangChain's `ConversationBufferWindowMemory` works this way. The cost: system prompts and early instructions disappear, causing subtle behavioral drift as the conversation progresses.\n\n**Middle removal** keeps both the system prompt and the most recent turns, deleting the middle of the conversation history. This is the right shape given the attention bias: preserve both ends, sacrifice the middle. The cost is implementation complexity and the risk of breaking narrative coherence if the removed context contained a key decision or reference.\n\n```\nSuffix truncation:    [SYSTEM][TURN1][TURN2][TURN3 ... CUT]\nPrefix truncation:    [CUT ... TURN4][TURN5][TURN6][TURN7]\nMiddle removal:       [SYSTEM][TURN1][TURN2] ... [TURN6][TURN7]\n```\n\nNone of these is universally correct. The right choice depends on what's in your context and what drives the quality of the response.\n\n## Placement as Strategy\n\nOnce you know where attention concentrates, you can design for it deliberately.\n\nPut the most retrieval-critical content at the top and bottom of your context. In a RAG pipeline, this means your reranker isn't just improving relevance, it's also determining position, which determines attention weight. The top-ranked document getting slot 1 matters twice: it's the best match *and* it gets the most attention.\n\nFor multi-document retrieval, a practical heuristic: place the single most relevant chunk first, fill the middle with supporting context, and put the second most relevant chunk last. The model's U-shaped attention curve works in your favor.\n\n```mermaid\ngraph LR\n    A[Query] --> B[ANN Retrieval\\nTop 100 candidates]\n    B --> C[Cross-encoder Reranker\\nTop 10]\n    C --> D{Position Assignment}\n    D --> E[Slot 1: Highest relevance]\n    D --> F[Slots 2-9: Supporting context]\n    D --> G[Slot 10: Second highest relevance]\n    E & F & G --> H[LLM Context Window]\n```\n\nElasticsearch's documentation for their RAG pipeline makes this explicit: approximate nearest neighbor search retrieves fast and cheap, cross-encoder reranking sorts by accuracy, and the position assignment step is distinct from both. Most teams collapse reranking and positioning into a single \"sort by score\" step and leave the second insight on the table.\n\n## The 75% Rule and Context Rot\n\nA 200K token context limit doesn't mean you can fill 200K tokens. Conservative practice reserves 25-30% as a safety buffer for output tokens and response quality. Filling past the 80% mark degrades accuracy, the model loses coherence in ways that are subtle enough to miss in offline evaluation but obvious to users in production.\n\nContext rot has a specific signature: the model starts repeating instructions it was given earlier, contradicts a constraint from 15 turns ago, or gives advice that was already discussed. Claude Code users report hitting this within 10-15 development interactions on a large codebase, well before the 200K limit. The fix isn't a bigger window; it's disciplined truncation that keeps the context dense and current.\n\nOne token approximates 4 characters, or 0.75 words. A 1,000-line Python file at 50 characters per line is roughly 12,500 tokens. A tool output returning a full API response adds more. The window fills faster than it looks.\n\n## Summarization as an Escape Hatch\n\nWhen truncation loses too much, summarization compresses the dropped content before removing it. The pattern: once conversation history exceeds a threshold, summarize the oldest N turns into a single paragraph, then replace those turns with the summary.\n\nThe tradeoff is real. Summarization costs an additional LLM call and introduces lossy compression, the summary contains what the summarizing model decided was important, which may not match what the later turns actually need. For high-stakes contexts (legal, medical, long-running coding sessions), this loss compounds.\n\nA calibrated approach: use sliding-window truncation for low-stakes chat, summarization for contexts where history matters but verbatim retention doesn't, and RAG (retrieving prior turns by relevance rather than recency) for sessions where any past exchange might be retrieved later.\n\n## Where RAG Doesn't Solve This\n\nA common assumption: RAG replaces truncation because you retrieve only what's needed. In practice, RAG shifts the problem downstream.\n\nRetrieval returns N documents. You still must truncate them to fit in the context window. The lost-in-the-middle risk reappears at the document composition stage. A system that retrieves 20 relevant chunks and stuffs them sequentially into the prompt has the same positional attention problem as a system that loads the full conversation history.\n\nThe correct pattern is retrieval, then reranking, then positional assignment. The reranker handles relevance sorting. The positional assignment step handles attention optimization. These are separate decisions.\n\nCross-encoder reranking at this stage costs roughly (candidates × query) rerank calls, not (corpus × query). Reranking 100 candidates from 1M documents is 100 calls, not 1M. The Elasticsearch cookbook documents this two-stage pattern explicitly, and the cost structure makes it viable even at scale.\n\n## The Honest Summary\n\nTruncation strategy is fundamentally a bet on what information drives response quality. Suffix truncation bets on instruction-following. Prefix truncation bets on recency. Middle removal bets on both ends. Each is wrong in some scenario.\n\nThe more durable insight is positional: attention concentrates at the edges. Design your context composition to put high-value content there, regardless of which truncation strategy you use. Reranking improves relevance. Position assignment improves attention. You want both.\n\nReserve 25% of your context for output. Keep your context below 80% capacity. And when a user reports that your AI is \"acting confused\" in a long session, check context composition before assuming model capability."
