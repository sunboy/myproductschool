  "body_mdx": "## The Attention Problem Nobody Talks About\n\nMost engineers think of prompt failures as wording failures. The instruction was ambiguous, or the example was off, or the model needed more constraints. So they rewrite the instruction. It still fails intermittently. They add more context. It gets worse.\n\nThe actual problem is often positional. Transformer attention exhibits a U-shaped bias: tokens at the beginning and end of the context window receive systematically higher attention weights, regardless of their semantic relevance. Research published at ACL in 2024 showed this bias amplifies with model depth, additional transformer layers attend to contextualized representations of earlier tokens more frequently, compounding the preference. The model is not ignoring your middle instruction because it is unclear. It is ignoring it because of where it sits.\n\nThis has a name in the literature: the lost-in-the-middle effect. For inputs longer than 2,000 tokens, information positioned in the middle of the context shows accuracy drops of 30% or more compared to equivalent information at the edges.\n\n![Prompt Structure and Attention Locality: Where You Put Things Matters](/content-images/context-engineering/prompt-structure-and-attention-locality-where-you-put-things-matters.png)\n\n## Where Critical Instructions Actually Belong\n\nThe practical implication is straightforward: your most important constraints belong in two places, the system prompt (beginning of context) and immediately before the final user turn (end of context). Not in the middle of a long document you retrieved. Not sandwiched between retrieved chunks. At the edges.\n\nThis shows up concretely in RAG pipelines. A common pattern is to retrieve 4-6 documents, concatenate them, and append the question. The instruction about how to answer, \"cite your sources,\" \"return JSON only,\" \"do not speculate beyond the provided context\", gets dropped between the documents and the question. That placement is the worst possible spot for an instruction that needs to hold.\n\nThe fix is mechanical:\n\n```\n[system prompt, stable constraints here]\n[retrieved document 1]\n[retrieved document 2]\n[retrieved document 3]\n[constraints REPEATED here, end of context]\n[user question]\n```\n\nRepeating the critical constraint at the end costs almost nothing in tokens. It reliably outperforms a single placement in the middle.\n\n## The Effective Window Is Smaller Than Advertised\n\nThere is a second positioning problem that compounds the first. Models claiming 200K-token context windows become unreliable around 130K tokens. For 1M-token models, accuracy drops noticeably at 60-70% of the claimed window. Chroma's 2025 research tested 18 frontier models including GPT-4.1, Claude Opus 4.6, and Gemini 2.5, every single one exhibits performance degradation as input length increases, even within the nominally supported range.\n\nWhat this means in practice: if you are sending 180K tokens to a 200K model and wondering why responses are inconsistent, the window is not the problem. The position of your critical content within that window is.\n\nThe question to ask before building any long-context workflow is not \"does this model support enough tokens?\" It is \"where will the information I actually need the model to use end up in the assembled context?\"\n\n## Structural Patterns That Hold\n\nA few structural choices consistently improve reliability across long-context tasks:\n\n**Front-load the contract.** State the output format, the constraints, and the persona once, clearly, at the top of the system prompt. Do not bury format requirements in examples.\n\n**Repeat critical constraints at the tail.** Any instruction that must hold, \"output only valid JSON,\" \"do not hallucinate names,\" \"respond in Spanish\", should appear immediately before the final turn.\n\n**Keep retrieved content short and ranked.** Retrieval quality matters more than retrieval volume. Limiting to 2-3 tightly relevant chunks instead of 4-8 long documents cuts input tokens by 50% or more with no accuracy loss, and keeps your actual instruction content closer to the edges. The Microsoft Research LLMLingua work showed 20x prompt compression with only 1.5% performance loss on reasoning tasks, aggressive pruning of middle content is almost always safe.\n\n**Test with content at different positions.** If you have a constraint that must hold, write a test that places triggering content at position 10%, 50%, and 90% of the context. Failures at 50% that pass at 10% and 90% are positional failures, not wording failures.\n\n## What This Changes About How You Debug\n\nWhen a prompt-based feature fails intermittently, sometimes following instructions, sometimes not, the standard debugging instinct is to rewrite the instruction. That is often the wrong move.\n\nBefore rewriting, check where the instruction lives in the assembled context. If it is in the middle of a long retrieval dump, move it to the end and test again. The failure rate will drop before you change a single word.\n\nPosition is not a soft preference. It is a structural property of how transformer attention works, and it is load-bearing in any prompt that exceeds a few hundred tokens."
