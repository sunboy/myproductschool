  "body_mdx": "## Why Human Error is Never the Root Cause\n\nIn October 2012, when AWS degraded and took Netflix down with it, Netflix's engineers didn't write \"infrastructure failure\" and close the ticket. They wrote a 2,500-word postmortem that traced the failure through every layer of their own system. The lesson wasn't \"AWS is unreliable.\" It was: why had Netflix built something that couldn't survive an AWS failure?\n\nThat's the discipline behind blameless postmortems. When something breaks, a system created the conditions for it. People operate within systems. Finding the person who made the last mistake before the outage feels like closure, but it changes nothing about the system. The next engineer in that seat will hit the same failure.\n\n---\n\n## The Five Whys in Practice\n\nThe Five Whys technique is deceptively simple: ask \"why\" repeatedly until you reach a root cause you can actually fix. The tricky part is knowing when to stop and where to go.\n\nGoogle's SRE book documents an incident that illustrates this cleanly. A newly discovered Shakespeare sonnet caused a traffic spike 88x above normal. The service fell over. The immediate cause was clear: too much traffic. But that's not a root cause.\n\n**Tracing the failure chain:**\n\n1. Why did the service fail? Traffic exceeded capacity by 88x.\n2. Why did traffic spike that high? A rare term in the new sonnet triggered millions of searches.\n3. Why did those searches overwhelm the service? Each search for a term not in the corpus leaked a file descriptor.\n4. Why was there a file descriptor leak? A latent bug triggered when the query result was empty.\n5. Why wasn't the bug caught? Normal query volumes never hit the empty-result code path at scale.\n\nThe fix wasn't \"add more servers.\" It was regression tests for queries that return nothing. The latent bug had existed for years. It just needed the right condition to surface.\n\nA Five Whys trace typically looks like this:\n\n```mermaid\ngraph TD\n    A[Service Down] --> B[88x Traffic Spike]\n    B --> C[Rare Term in New Content]\n    C --> D[File Descriptor Leak on Empty Result]\n    D --> E[Latent Bug in Empty-Result Path]\n    E --> F[No Regression Test for Empty Corpus Queries]\n    F --> G[ROOT CAUSE: Missing test coverage for edge-case query paths]\n```\n\nThe rule: keep going until you reach something you can change. \"Operator made a mistake\" is not a root cause. \"The system gave the operator no indication that the action was irreversible\" is.\n\n---\n\n## Writing a Blameless Postmortem\n\nGoogle's SRE book defines the standard: \"A blameless postmortem focused on identifying contributing causes of the incident without indicting any individual or team for bad or inappropriate behavior.\" The underlying assumption is that everyone involved had good intentions and did the right thing with the information available to them.\n\nThis isn't soft. It's precise. You can't fix a person, but you can fix the system that put them in a position to fail.\n\nA postmortem has five mandatory sections:\n\n**1. Summary and Impact**\nQuantify the damage. Duration, affected users, error rate, revenue impact if known. \"The payment API returned 503s for 14 minutes, affecting approximately 40,000 transactions.\" Not: \"Some users experienced issues.\"\n\n**2. Timeline**\nUTC timestamps. What happened, when, and who noticed. Don't smooth out the confusion, the 20 minutes where nobody knew what was failing is part of the story.\n\n**3. Root Cause**\nThis is the Five Whys output. The contributing factors that made the failure possible, traced to system-level causes. Stripe's 2022 latency outage traced to an unbalanced connection pool that saturated a database cluster when traffic spiked. The root cause wasn't the traffic spike; it was the connection pool configuration that made the spike fatal.\n\n**4. Contributing Factors**\nThe conditions that made the root cause possible. Missing alerts. No runbook for this failure mode. Config drift between staging and production.\n\n**5. Action Items**\nOwner, due date, specific outcome. Not \"improve monitoring.\" Instead: \"Add p99 latency alert for payment-api with threshold 500ms, owner: @eng-payments, due: 2026-05-15.\"\n\nAn unreviewed postmortem might as well not exist. Build a regular review cadence with senior engineers evaluating completeness, root cause depth, and action item quality.\n\n---\n\n## SLIs, SLOs, and Error Budgets\n\nSLIs (Service Level Indicators) are the metrics you track. For a web service, the three canonical ones are:\n\n- **Availability**: successful requests / total requests over a rolling window\n- **Latency**: percentage of requests completing below a threshold (e.g., 95% of requests under 200ms)\n- **Error rate**: 5xx responses / total responses\n\nSLOs (Service Level Objectives) are the targets. A 99.9% availability SLO means you tolerate 0.1% failure. Over four weeks of a service handling 1 million requests, that's 1,000 acceptable errors.\n\nThe **error budget** is that 0.1%. Spend it intentionally (planned maintenance, risky deploys) or watch it drain through incidents. When the budget runs out before the window closes, deployments stop until it refills. This is the mechanism that gives reliability a hard business constraint.\n\nFor a 99% SLO over 28 days, the total downtime allowance is about 7 hours 20 minutes. A 2-hour maintenance window consumes 27% of the quarterly budget. Teams that track this stop treating downtime as free.\n\n---\n\n## Circuit Breakers: Stopping Cascade Failures\n\nA circuit breaker prevents a slow or failing dependency from taking down everything downstream. Netflix's Hystrix library (which ran 10+ billion command executions per day at Netflix scale) popularized the pattern.\n\nThree states:\n\n```\nCLOSED → normal operation, requests flow through\n   ↓  (failure rate > threshold)\nOPEN → reject all requests immediately, return fallback\n   ↓  (after sleep window)\nHALF-OPEN → let one request through to test recovery\n   ↓  (success)                ↓  (failure)\nCLOSED                       OPEN\n```\n\nNetflix's defaults: open the circuit when more than 20 requests arrive in a 10-second window AND more than 50% fail. These aren't magic numbers, tune them to your latency SLO. A circuit that trips on the first two errors is useless noise. A circuit that requires 500 failures before tripping lets a cascade run for minutes.\n\nThe half-open state is what separates a circuit breaker from a rate limiter. After the sleep window (typically 5 seconds), one test request goes through. Success closes the circuit. Failure reopens it. Without this, you need a human to manually restore traffic.\n\n---\n\n## Oncall Alerts That Fire on User Impact\n\nThe worst oncall rotations are the ones where half the pages don't require human action. PagerDuty distinguishes an alert (requires action) from a notification (informational). Notifications should never page anyone at 3am.\n\nDesign alerts around the four golden signals: traffic, latency, errors, saturation. But fire on user-facing SLI violations, not system metrics. \"CPU at 80%\" is not an alert. \"p99 latency above 500ms for 5 minutes\" is, because it's burning your error budget and users are feeling it.\n\nEvery alert needs: what triggered, what the impact is if unresolved, and a runbook link. A vague \"Something went wrong\" page trains engineers to ignore pages.\n\nFor new services, start with three alerts: error rate above SLO threshold, p99 latency above target, and availability check. Add specificity as you learn what breaks in production. Don't model all possible failure modes in advance. You'll page on things that don't matter and miss things that do.\n\nEtsy's opsweekly tool exists specifically because alert fatigue is an engineering problem, not a discipline problem. If a team is ignoring pages, the alerts are wrong, not the team.\n\n---\n\n## The Discipline That Scales\n\nA single blameless postmortem teaches you what failed. A hundred of them, reviewed and acted on, teach you how your systems fail. That pattern recognition is the actual value. Netflix didn't build Chaos Monkey because they were afraid of failure; they built it because they knew failures were coming and wanted to discover them on their terms, not their users'.\n\nRun the Five Whys until you reach something you can change. Write the postmortem in public, with names attached to action items. Track error budget against SLOs. Page on user impact. The organizations that do this consistently don't have fewer incidents, they just stop having the same incident twice."
