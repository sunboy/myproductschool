  "body_mdx": "## The Question Every System Avoids\n\nDistributed systems fail in ways their designers didn't anticipate. The honest question isn't \"will something fail?\" but \"do we actually know what happens when it does?\" Most teams think they know. Most teams are wrong.\n\nChaos engineering is the discipline of forcing that question into a controlled experiment before production forces it into an incident. You inject failure deliberately, you observe the system, and you fix what breaks on your terms rather than at 3am with customers watching.\n\nNetflix started this in 2010 with Chaos Monkey, a tool that randomly terminated production EC2 instances. The reasoning was blunt: if failure was inevitable, better to practice handling it during business hours than discover your gaps during a real outage. By 2014, when an AWS regional event rebooted 10% of EC2 servers, Netflix stayed up. Twenty-plus other AWS services went down.\n\n---\n\n## SLIs, SLOs, and the Error Budget\n\nBefore you can design a chaos experiment, you need to know what \"working\" means. That means SLIs and SLOs.\n\nAn **SLI** (Service Level Indicator) is a measurable signal of user experience. For most web services, three SLIs cover 90% of what matters:\n\n- **Availability:** `(successful_requests / total_requests) * 100`. What counts as \"successful\" is your first design decision. HTTP 500 is a failure. HTTP 200 with a corrupted payload is also a failure, but harder to catch.\n- **Latency:** Usually measured at p99 or p99.9, not mean. If 1% of your users wait 10 seconds, mean latency of 120ms hides that entirely.\n- **Error rate:** The complement of availability. Sometimes more useful when normalized by endpoint.\n\nAn **SLO** (Service Level Objective) is the target you commit to. Google publishes theirs: Google Compute Engine at 99.95% availability. AWS promises 99.99% per region per month. The gap between 100% and your SLO is your **error budget**.\n\n```\nError budget (minutes/month) = (1 - SLO) * 43,200\n\n99.9% SLO  → 43.2 minutes/month\n99.95% SLO → 21.6 minutes/month\n99.99% SLO →  4.3 minutes/month\n```\n\nThe error budget is the key concept. It turns reliability into a resource. Your team can spend that budget on risky deployments, experiments, or feature work. When the budget is gone, you slow down and fix things. When you have budget left, you can move fast. This is why error budgets make the \"reliability vs. velocity\" argument concrete rather than philosophical.\n\nTo calculate how much budget an incident consumed:\n\n```python\ndef error_budget_consumed(incident_duration_minutes, slo_target):\n    monthly_budget = (1 - slo_target) * 43200\n    return (incident_duration_minutes / monthly_budget) * 100\n\n# Example: 25-minute outage against a 99.9% SLO\n# error_budget_consumed(25, 0.999) → 57.9% of monthly budget gone\n```\n\nA 25-minute incident against a 99.9% SLO consumes 58% of your monthly error budget. That's a number you can take to a planning meeting.\n\n---\n\n## Circuit Breakers\n\nA circuit breaker is the primary pattern for stopping cascading failures. When a downstream service degrades, without a circuit breaker your service keeps sending requests, queuing threads, and eventually exhausting its own resources. With a circuit breaker, you fail fast.\n\n```\nCLOSED → (failure rate exceeds threshold) → OPEN → (timeout elapsed) → HALF-OPEN → (success) → CLOSED\n                                                                              ↓ (failure) → OPEN\n```\n\nNetflix ran Hystrix at scale for years. Their defaults reflect production learning:\n\n- Minimum 20 requests in the rolling window before the circuit can trip\n- Trips when error rate exceeds 50% over a 10-second window\n- Half-open after a configurable sleep window, allows one test request through\n\nHere's a minimal circuit breaker implementation that captures the critical behavior:\n\n```python\nimport time\nfrom enum import Enum\n\nclass State(Enum):\n    CLOSED = \"closed\"\n    OPEN = \"open\"\n    HALF_OPEN = \"half_open\"\n\nclass CircuitBreaker:\n    def __init__(self, error_threshold=0.5, window=10, min_calls=20, recovery_timeout=30):\n        self.error_threshold = error_threshold\n        self.window = window\n        self.min_calls = min_calls\n        self.recovery_timeout = recovery_timeout\n        self.state = State.CLOSED\n        self.calls = []  # (timestamp, success)\n        self.opened_at = None\n\n    def _recent_calls(self):\n        cutoff = time.time() - self.window\n        self.calls = [(t, s) for t, s in self.calls if t > cutoff]\n        return self.calls\n\n    def call(self, fn, *args, **kwargs):\n        if self.state == State.OPEN:\n            if time.time() - self.opened_at > self.recovery_timeout:\n                self.state = State.HALF_OPEN\n            else:\n                raise Exception(\"Circuit open\")\n\n        try:\n            result = fn(*args, **kwargs)\n            self._record(True)\n            if self.state == State.HALF_OPEN:\n                self.state = State.CLOSED\n            return result\n        except Exception as e:\n            self._record(False)\n            self._maybe_trip()\n            raise\n\n    def _record(self, success):\n        self.calls.append((time.time(), success))\n\n    def _maybe_trip(self):\n        recent = self._recent_calls()\n        if len(recent) < self.min_calls:\n            return\n        error_rate = sum(1 for _, s in recent if not s) / len(recent)\n        if error_rate >= self.error_threshold:\n            self.state = State.OPEN\n            self.opened_at = time.time()\n```\n\nThe half-open state is where most implementations cut corners. Without it, a circuit that opens never recovers automatically. A single test request in half-open state is the right mechanism: success closes the circuit, failure reopens it.\n\n---\n\n## Designing a Chaos Experiment\n\nA chaos experiment has three parts: a steady-state hypothesis, a failure injection, and a measurement.\n\n```mermaid\nflowchart TD\n    A[Define steady-state\\ne.g. p99 latency < 200ms] --> B[Inject failure\\ne.g. kill 50% of pods]\n    B --> C{Steady-state\\npreserved?}\n    C -->|Yes| D[System is resilient\\nto this failure mode]\n    C -->|No| E[Identify gap\\nfix before production finds it]\n    E --> F[Implement fix]\n    F --> A\n```\n\nAmazon ran 733 AWS Fault Injection Service experiments before Prime Day 2024, validating that systems handling DynamoDB's 146 million requests per second and Lambda's 1.3 trillion monthly invocations would degrade gracefully rather than collapse. Uber ran 180,000 automated chaos tests across 47 critical flows in early 2024, surfacing 23 resilience risks, 70% of which were architectural dependencies their engineers hadn't mapped. Shopify runs GameDays before every Black Friday, testing network fault injection and cache-busting against search and checkout flows. The 2025 campaign found monitoring gaps and incomplete runbooks that teams closed before peak traffic hit.\n\nStart with the smallest experiment that has a real answer. \"Kill one replica in a three-replica deployment\" is a valid first experiment. \"Simulate an AZ failure\" is a valid fifth experiment. Don't start with AZ failures.\n\n---\n\n## Blameless Postmortems\n\nWhen an experiment reveals a real failure, or when production beats you to it, the postmortem is where organizations either learn or repeat the same incident.\n\nGoogle's postmortem for the Shakespeare search outage is the canonical example. On the day Reddit sent 88x normal traffic to a search endpoint, the system went down for 66 minutes, losing 1.21 billion queries. The postmortem identified contributing causes: a resource leak under unknown search terms, insufficient capacity monitoring, and no traffic-shaping at the edge. It did not name the engineer who deployed the resource leak. It named the system properties that allowed the leak to cascade.\n\nA blameless postmortem has five components:\n\n1. **Timeline**, minute-by-minute from first signal to resolution. Gaps in the timeline are findings.\n2. **Impact**, specific, measured. \"Some users were affected\" is not a finding. \"64% of checkout requests returned HTTP 503 for 18 minutes\" is.\n3. **Contributing causes**, plural, systemic. The proximate cause (the thing that failed) is almost never the real finding. The real finding is why the system allowed that failure to cascade.\n4. **Action items**, owned, dated, and scoped. \"Improve monitoring\" is not an action item. \"Add p99 latency alert on /checkout with 500ms threshold, owned by @alice, due 2026-06-01\" is.\n5. **What went well**, what detection, response, or mitigation worked. Postmortems that only document failures train teams to hide near-misses.\n\nThe blameless part is load-bearing. When postmortems assign blame, people stop writing them honestly. When people stop writing them honestly, you lose the organizational learning that makes the next incident shorter.\n\n---\n\n## Alerts That Fire on User Impact\n\nThe last piece is alerting. Most engineering teams over-alert on system signals (CPU at 80%, memory at 75%) and under-alert on user signals (error rate rising, latency degrading). The result is alert fatigue: pages fire constantly for things that don't matter, and the real incidents get noticed when a user tweets about them.\n\nThe rule: alert on what users experience, not what servers feel.\n\nFor a web service, three alerts cover the critical path:\n\n- **Error rate:** Alert when `error_rate_5m > 1%` for five minutes. Not \"CPU at 85%.\"\n- **Latency:** Alert when `p99_latency_5m > 500ms` sustained for five minutes. Not \"memory at 70%.\"\n- **Availability:** Alert when `availability_1h < 99.9%`. This ties directly to error budget burn.\n\nAdd burn rate alerts for error budgets. If you're consuming your monthly error budget at 6x the normal rate, you're on track to exhaust it in five days, not thirty. That's worth waking someone up for. Burning budget at 1x is not.\n\n---\n\nChaos engineering pays off not in the experiments that go as expected but in the ones that reveal something you didn't know your system was doing. Netflix's Chaos Kong exercises simulating full AZ failures weren't routine maintenance. They were the reason Netflix survived the September 2015 DynamoDB outage while 20 other services didn't. The experiment was designed to find gaps. It did. That's the whole point."
