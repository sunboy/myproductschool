  "body_mdx": "## The Three Layers\n\nStart with the distinction, because conflating these layers causes concrete, expensive mistakes.\n\nAn **SLI** (Service Level Indicator) is a ratio: good events divided by total events. Not a raw number. Not a percentage of time. A ratio. \"Number of HTTP requests that returned 2xx or 3xx / total HTTP requests\" is an SLI. \"Uptime\" by itself is not, because it doesn't account for traffic volume.\n\nAn **SLO** (Service Level Objective) is the target you set for that ratio. 99.9% availability SLO means you're committing internally that your SLI will meet or exceed that threshold over a rolling window. The SLO lives inside your team. No customer signs it.\n\nAn **SLA** (Service Level Agreement) is the SLO you've put in a contract with a penalty. AWS's [EC2 SLA](https://aws.amazon.com/compute/sla/) promises 99.99% availability per region; breach it and they issue service credits. Google Cloud's [Run SLA](https://cloud.google.com/run/sla) follows the same pattern. The key word is \"penalty.\" Without one, you have an SLO, not an SLA.\n\nThe practical implication: your SLOs should be tighter than your SLAs. If you promise customers 99.9% and your internal target is also 99.9%, you have zero runway. Teams at Google typically set internal SLOs 0.1-0.5% tighter than their external SLAs. That gap is where the engineering work happens before customers notice.\n\n## Defining SLIs That Actually Measure Pain\n\nThe instinct is to measure what's easy: host CPU, disk I/O, memory. These are symptoms, not signals. An SLI should track what the user experiences.\n\nFor a typical web service, three SLIs cover most of the signal:\n\n**Availability SLI:**\n```\ngood_requests / total_requests\n```\nWhere \"good\" means HTTP 2xx or 3xx, not just \"the server responded.\"\n\n**Latency SLI:**\n```\nrequests_served_under_threshold_ms / total_requests\n```\nPick a threshold that reflects the user experience. Google's [SRE Workbook](https://sre.google/workbook/implementing-slos/) uses \"p90 < 100ms\" and \"p99 < 900ms\" as separate SLIs for a mobile game backend, because p50 tells you nothing about the tail.\n\n**Error rate SLI:**\n```\nrequests_without_errors / total_requests\n```\nDistinct from availability. A 500 is a bad availability event. A 200 with a corrupted payload is a bad error rate event. Both matter.\n\n## Error Budgets: The Number That Changes Behavior\n\nThe error budget is what makes SLOs operationally useful. It's simple arithmetic: `error_budget = (1 - SLO) × total_requests`.\n\nWith a 99.9% availability SLO over four weeks on a service handling 3.6 million requests, you have roughly 3,600 allowed failures (0.1% × 3.6M). That's your budget for the entire window, across deployments, incidents, and maintenance.\n\nGoogle's SRE workbook gives a concrete example: a four-hour API rollback caused 14,066 errors, consuming 13% of a quarterly budget of ~109,897 failures. After an incident like that, the engineering team has a quantitative answer to \"how risky can our next deploy be?\" The answer is \"less risky than last time.\"\n\nThe budget changes team behavior in two directions. When budget is ample (>50% remaining), ship features aggressively. When it's tight (<25%), freeze risky changes and focus on reliability work. This resolves the classic product-vs-reliability argument because the tradeoff is now explicit.\n\n```mermaid\nflowchart LR\n    A[Error Budget\\n>50% remaining] -->|Ship| B[Feature velocity]\n    C[Error Budget\\n25–50%] -->|Review| D[Slow risky changes]\n    E[Error Budget\\n<25%] -->|Freeze| F[Reliability focus]\n    G[Budget exhausted] -->|Stop| H[No new features\\nuntil recovery]\n```\n\n## Burn Rate Alerts\n\nThe mistake most teams make: alerting on raw SLI values. \"Alert when availability drops below 99.9%\" will fire long after users are already angry and miss slow burns that won't trip the threshold until week three.\n\nBurn rate fixes this. A burn rate of 1 means you're consuming budget at exactly the rate that depletes it by end of window. A burn rate of 14.4 means you'll exhaust the entire month's budget in two hours.\n\nGoogle's alerting recommendations from the [SRE Workbook](https://sre.google/workbook/implementing-slos/):\n\n| Burn rate | Budget consumed in | Severity |\n|---|---|---|\n| >14.4 | 2 hours | Page immediately |\n| >6 | 5 hours | Page (ticket) |\n| >1 | Full window | Create ticket |\n\nImplement this with a short window (5 minutes) for the critical tier and a longer window (1 hour) for the high tier. Short windows catch sharp spikes; long windows catch slow bleeds.\n\n## Circuit Breakers: Protecting the Budget\n\nWhen a downstream service is failing, continuing to send traffic at full rate does two things: it burns your error budget faster and it prevents the downstream service from recovering. The circuit breaker pattern stops both.\n\n[Resilience4j](https://resilience4j.readme.io/docs/circuitbreaker) is the reference implementation for the JVM. The state machine has three states:\n\n```mermaid\nstateDiagram-v2\n    CLOSED --> OPEN: failure_rate >= threshold\\n(default: 50%)\n    OPEN --> HALF_OPEN: after wait_duration\\n(default: 60s)\n    HALF_OPEN --> CLOSED: test calls pass\n    HALF_OPEN --> OPEN: test calls fail\n```\n\nThe half-open state is the part that trips most implementations. The circuit lets through a configurable number of test calls (default: 10 in Resilience4j) before deciding whether to close or reopen. Without a minimum call count requirement (default: 100 for the initial window), the circuit can oscillate on early low-volume failures before there's enough signal to make the threshold meaningful.\n\nA practical configuration for a payment service hitting a credit bureau API:\n\n```java\nCircuitBreakerConfig config = CircuitBreakerConfig.custom()\n    .failureRateThreshold(40)           // open at 40% failure rate\n    .slowCallRateThreshold(80)          // open if 80% of calls are slow\n    .slowCallDurationThreshold(Duration.ofSeconds(2))\n    .waitDurationInOpenState(Duration.ofSeconds(30))\n    .permittedNumberOfCallsInHalfOpenState(5)\n    .minimumNumberOfCalls(20)\n    .build();\n```\n\nThe 40% threshold is tighter than the default because payment calls are high-value. The 30-second wait is shorter because payment flows have strict user-facing timeouts.\n\n## Blameless Postmortems\n\nA blameless postmortem has two root cause sections, not one. Netflix codified this distinction: the proximate cause is the specific technical event that triggered the incident; the systemic cause is the architectural or process condition that made the proximate cause possible.\n\nCloudflare's [2023 control plane outage](https://blog.cloudflare.com/post-mortem-on-cloudflare-control-plane-and-analytics-outage/) is instructive. The proximate cause was that some newer product services hadn't been migrated to the high-availability cluster. The systemic cause was that there was no automated check preventing services from going to production without HA configuration. One sentence in the postmortem. Fixing it means writing a gate, not training engineers to remember.\n\nStructure that surfaces systemic causes:\n\n1. **Timeline**, exact timestamps, no narratives, no blame\n2. **Impact**, user impact in concrete terms (X% of requests affected for Y minutes)\n3. **Proximate cause**, what broke\n4. **Systemic cause**, what process, tool, or architecture allowed it to break\n5. **Action items**, owners, deadlines, JIRA tickets. Not \"improve monitoring.\" \"Add burn rate alert for the payments service by 2024-02-15, owner: @maya.\"\n\nThe distinction between proximate and systemic matters because it determines whether you fix the incident or fix the system. Fixing only the proximate cause means the same failure mode returns with a different surface.\n\n## Tying It Together\n\nSLIs measure user pain. SLOs set the target. Error budgets convert that target into a number that teams can reason about, allocate, and fight over in the right way. Circuit breakers protect the budget when dependencies fail. Postmortems close the loop by ensuring incidents improve the system, not just the configuration.\n\nWhen you're designing your oncall rotation, the test is simple: does each alert correspond to a user-visible impact, or is it a system metric that might or might not affect users? An alert that fires on \"CPU > 80%\" is noise. An alert that fires on \"burn rate > 6 for the past 15 minutes\" is signal. Build toward signal."
