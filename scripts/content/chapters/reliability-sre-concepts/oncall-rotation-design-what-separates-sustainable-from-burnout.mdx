Alert quality is a product of your SLO design, not your monitoring tool.", "body_mdx": "## The Pager Is a Symptom, Not a Problem\n\nWhen an engineer dreads Sunday afternoon because they're starting oncall, the rotation is broken. The pager is not the problem; the alerts driving it are. And the alerts are broken because the team never defined what user impact actually looks like.\n\nGoogle's SRE book documents a rule that teams repeatedly rediscover through attrition: **no more than two significant incidents per oncall shift**. Beyond that, the engineer cannot do quality incident response. They context-switch, skip postmortems, and start ignoring alerts that fire too often. Within a quarter, your best engineers are rotating out of oncall or leaving the company.\n\nFix the alerts, and the rotation becomes manageable. Fix the rotation design, and the alerts stop being the only lever you have.\n\n## What Makes a Page Legitimate\n\nA legitimate page has three properties:\n\n1. It represents a condition a human must act on right now\n2. It is actionable, meaning the oncall engineer can do something specific to address it\n3. It was not already firing when the engineer started their shift (chronic alerts are noise, not signal)\n\nMost alert libraries fail all three tests simultaneously. A page for \"CPU utilization > 80% for 5 minutes\" fails the first test unless your service is CPU-bound in a way that directly degrades user experience. CPU is a proxy metric; latency and error rate are user-facing metrics. Alert on the latter.\n\nA page that fires and resolves before the engineer opens their laptop fails the second test. Auto-resolving alerts indicate either that the underlying system self-healed (great) or that your threshold is wrong (common). Either way, it woke someone up for nothing.\n\nPagerDuty's 2024 State of Digital Operations report found that 45% of alerts are either acknowledged and closed immediately or auto-resolve before acknowledgment. Those are pure noise. Teams that get this below 10% report significantly lower burnout on qualitative surveys.\n\n## Alert Design as SLO Enforcement\n\nThe correct mental model: **your alert thresholds are derived from your SLOs, not configured independently in your monitoring tool.**\n\nIf your availability SLO is 99.9% over a rolling 30-day window, your monthly error budget is 43.8 minutes of downtime. An alert should fire when you are burning through that budget faster than it replenishes.\n\nThe formula SRE teams use is burn rate alerting:\n\n```\nerror_rate / (1 - SLO_target) = burn_rate\n```\n\nA burn rate of 1 means you are consuming your budget at exactly the rate it replenishes. A burn rate of 14.4 means you will exhaust your monthly budget in 2 hours. That warrants a page. A burn rate of 2 over 6 hours also warrants a page, but not an urgent one.\n\nGoogle recommends a two-window alert approach:\n\n```yaml\n# Page immediately: fast burn over short window\n- alert: HighBurnRate\n  expr: |\n    (\n      job:request_errors:rate5m{job=\"api\"}\n      / job:request_count:rate5m{job=\"api\"}\n    ) > (14.4 * (1 - 0.999))\n  for: 2m\n  labels:\n    severity: page\n\n# Ticket: slow burn over longer window (will exhaust budget in ~3 days)\n- alert: SlowBurnRate\n  expr: |\n    (\n      job:request_errors:rate1h{job=\"api\"}\n      / job:request_count:rate1h{job=\"api\"}\n    ) > (3 * (1 - 0.999))\n  for: 60m\n  labels:\n    severity: ticket\n```\n\nThe fast-burn alert catches acute incidents. The slow-burn alert generates tickets during business hours, not pages at 3 AM. This is the distinction most teams miss.\n\n## Rotation Structure\n\nEven with well-designed alerts, rotation structure matters. The common failure modes:\n\n**Rotation too small.** A five-person team with weekly rotations means each engineer is oncall once every five weeks. That sounds fine until two people go on vacation simultaneously or one person leaves. The rotation collapses to every other week and becomes the dominant source of attrition.\n\nThe minimum viable rotation is eight people on a weekly cadence, or six people on a follow-the-sun model where each engineer covers only their waking hours. Shopify moved to a follow-the-sun model in 2022 for their core platform team and saw a 40% reduction in pages outside business hours per engineer.\n\n**Rotation too diffuse.** A 20-person rotation sounds like it reduces burden, but it means no one maintains enough context to respond effectively. The engineer oncall on week 17 has not touched the service in four months. Runbooks become the only safety net, and runbooks rot.\n\nAim for a rotation size that gives each engineer meaningful oncall experience without saturating their calendar. Eight to twelve is the practical range for most services.\n\n**No primary/secondary structure.** A flat rotation where one engineer handles everything alone creates a single point of failure. When the primary is overwhelmed or unreachable, incidents extend. A secondary acts as a backup for escalation and provides redundancy without adding to the regular rotation burden.\n\n## Runbook Quality as a Forcing Function\n\nRunbooks should be written by the engineer who just fought the incident, not by the engineer who designed the system six months ago. The best time to write a runbook entry is immediately after an incident resolves, while the context is fresh.\n\nA runbook entry that just says \"restart the service\" is not a runbook. It is documentation theater. A good runbook entry for a production incident answers:\n\n- What does the alert tell you?\n- What does this usually mean?\n- What do you check first, and what are you looking for?\n- What actions can you take, in what order, and what are the side effects?\n- When do you escalate, and who do you escalate to?\n\nNetflix's chaos engineering team enforces runbook quality by requiring that any alert without a linked runbook entry be deleted. The reasoning: if you cannot write down what to do when it fires, the alert should not exist. This is aggressive but directionally correct.\n\n## Reducing Toil Through Automation\n\nToil is manual, repetitive work that scales with traffic rather than with engineering investment. The oncall engineer who manually restarts a worker process twice a week is doing toil. That process should either not restart at all (fix the root cause) or restart itself automatically.\n\nThe discipline is distinguishing toil from engineering work:\n\n- **Toil:** Manually rolling back a bad deploy because the pipeline does not have an automatic rollback gate\n- **Engineering work:** Building the automatic rollback gate\n\nTracking toil is how you justify engineering investment. Atlassian's SRE team tracks \"toil hours per week\" as a metric in their oncall retrospectives. When toil exceeds 50% of oncall time, they treat it as a reliability bug and schedule engineering work to address it in the next sprint.\n\n## The Postmortem Feedback Loop\n\nRotation health degrades when postmortem action items do not ship. The oncall engineer files the postmortem, the action items go into the backlog, and three months later the same incident recurs. That engineer now trusts neither the postmortem process nor the team's commitment to improvement.\n\nTwo structural changes that close this loop:\n\nFirst, assign action items to specific engineers with due dates, not to the team. \"Team to investigate X\" dies in the backlog. \"Alice to add latency histogram to dashboard by Friday\" gets done.\n\nSecond, review open action items at the start of every sprint. If an item is more than 30 days old and not in progress, it either needs to be prioritized or explicitly closed as not worth doing. The worst outcome is a zombie action item that sits in the backlog indefinitely, signaling that postmortems are performative.\n\n\n\nThe feedback loop from alerts to rotation health connects the entire system. SLO-derived burn rate thresholds drive which pages fire; page quality and MTTR determine whether engineers trust the rotation; postmortem action item completion determines whether the rotation improves over time.\n\n```mermaid\nflowchart LR\n    A[\"SLO target\\n(e.g. 99.9%)\"] --> B[\"Burn rate threshold\\n(14.4x = page now)\"]\n    B --> C[\"Alert fires\\n(fast or slow burn)\"]\n    C --> D{\"Legitimate\\npage?\"}\n    D -->|Yes| E[\"Incident response\\nMTTR tracked\"]\n    D -->|No - noise| F[\"Alert review\\nraised in retro\"]\n    E --> G[\"Postmortem\\naction items\"]\n    G --> H{\"Shipped\\nwithin 30d?\"}\n    H -->|Yes| I[\"Rotation health\\nimproves\"]\n    H -->|No| J[\"Toil accumulates\\nBurnout risk rises\"]\n    I --> A\n    J --> F\n```\n\n## Measuring Rotation Health\n\nThe metrics that matter:\n\n| Metric | Target | Danger zone |\n|---|---|---|\n| Pages per oncall shift | < 2 significant | > 5 |\n| Alert noise rate | < 10% auto-resolve | > 30% |\n| MTTR (mean time to resolve) | Depends on service | Growing quarter over quarter |\n| Toil percentage of oncall time | < 30% | > 50% |\n| Postmortem action item completion rate (30d) | > 80% | < 50% |\n\nTrack these per rotation, not per incident. An incident might be exceptional; the metrics tell you whether the rotation is structurally sound.\n\nA rotation that consistently hits the targets in the left column is one engineers want to be on. That is the goal: not zero incidents, but a system where incidents are manageable, postmortems are trusted, and the pager does not define Sunday."}
