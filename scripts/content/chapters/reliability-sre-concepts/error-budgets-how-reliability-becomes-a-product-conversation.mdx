  "body_mdx": "## The Insight Hidden in the Name\n\nAn SLO of 99.9% doesn't mean your service is up 99.9% of the time. It means your service is allowed to fail 0.1% of the time. That 0.1% is your error budget, and it's a spending account, not a ceiling.\n\nGoogle's SRE book introduced this framing, but the real insight is organizational, not mathematical. When error budget is the mechanism that halts feature releases, reliability stops being something the on-call team worries about alone. Product managers care about budget because it gates their roadmap. Engineers care about it because their next deploy depends on it.\n\nGmail's internal SLO is 99.99% availability. That's 4.32 minutes of allowable failure per month. Their policy: above 75% budget remaining, ship normally. Below 25%, freeze features. At zero, everything stops except P0 bugs and security fixes. That policy means a bad deployment in week one costs future deploys in week three.\n\n---\n\n## Defining SLIs That Actually Measure User Experience\n\nAn SLI is a ratio: good events divided by total events. The definition sounds simple; the hard part is defining \"good.\"\n\nFor a web service, three SLIs cover most of what users notice:\n\n**Availability SLI**\n```\navailability = successful_requests / total_requests\n```\n\"Successful\" means: HTTP 2xx, not 5xx. Exclude health checks, internal probes, and load test traffic from the denominator. Including them dilutes your signal.\n\n**Latency SLI**\n```\nlatency = requests_under_threshold / total_requests\n```\nDon't SLO on mean latency. Mean latency hides the tail. A service with 50ms mean and 8000ms p99 is slow for 1% of users, which at 10k RPS is 100 users per second experiencing timeouts. Google Search targets p99 under 1000ms globally. Pick a threshold that reflects the UX boundary where users abandon.\n\n**Error Rate SLI**\n```\nerror_rate = 5xx_responses / total_responses\n```\nThis is often redundant with availability but useful for distinguishing \"service unreachable\" (availability miss) from \"service answered but threw an error\" (error rate miss). Both consume budget.\n\n---\n\n## Calculating Budget Consumption from Incident Data\n\nA 30-day window with a 99.9% SLO gives you 43.8 minutes of allowable downtime.\n\nSuppose you have two incidents this month:\n\n| Incident | Duration | Error Rate | User Traffic at Impact |\n|---|---|---|---|\n| DB failover | 12 min | 100% | 800 req/s |\n| Payment timeout bug | 35 min | 8% | 1,200 req/s |\n\nBudget consumed by incident 1: 12 min × 100% = 12 minutes of full-outage equivalent.  \nBudget consumed by incident 2: 35 min × 8% = 2.8 minutes of full-outage equivalent.\n\nTotal: 14.8 of your 43.8 minutes consumed, roughly 34%. Still safe. One more incident of incident-1's size and you're frozen.\n\nThe calculation matters less than the discipline of doing it consistently. Teams that track this weekly catch budget drift early. Teams that only look at it post-freeze spend all their reliability work in reactive mode.\n\n---\n\n## Circuit Breakers: Controlling Budget Burn in Real Time\n\nA circuit breaker stops calling a failing dependency before it exhausts your budget. The three states:\n\n```\nCLOSED → [failure rate ≥ threshold] → OPEN → [wait duration expires] → HALF-OPEN\nHALF-OPEN → [probe calls succeed] → CLOSED\nHALF-OPEN → [probe calls fail] → OPEN\n```\n\n```mermaid\nstateDiagram-v2\n    [*] --> CLOSED\n    CLOSED --> OPEN: failure rate ≥ 50% (min 10 calls)\n    OPEN --> HALF_OPEN: wait 30s\n    HALF_OPEN --> CLOSED: 3 probe calls succeed\n    HALF_OPEN --> OPEN: probe calls fail\n    CLOSED --> CLOSED: request succeeds\n```\n\nThe half-open state is where most implementations get lazy. A single successful probe is not recovery. Resilience4j's production default is `permittedNumberOfCallsInHalfOpenState: 3`, meaning three consecutive successes before re-closing. That's not arbitrary: a single retry success under load often reflects luck, not recovery.\n\nConfiguring thresholds for production:\n\n```java\nCircuitBreakerConfig config = CircuitBreakerConfig.custom()\n    .failureRateThreshold(50)          // open at 50% failure rate\n    .minimumNumberOfCalls(10)          // don't open on first failure\n    .waitDurationInOpenState(Duration.ofSeconds(30))\n    .permittedNumberOfCallsInHalfOpenState(3)\n    .build();\n```\n\nSet `minimumNumberOfCalls` high enough to avoid flapping on cold starts or low-traffic periods. A service that sees 2 calls per minute will open its circuit on 1 failure without this guard.\n\n---\n\n## Blameless Postmortems That Find Systemic Causes\n\nCloudflare's November 2025 outage postmortem is a good template to study. A database permissions change caused their Bot Management \"feature file\" to receive corrupted entries, which cascaded into network failures. The postmortem structure they used:\n\n1. **Timeline**, what happened and when, in UTC\n2. **Root cause**, the mechanism, not the person\n3. **Contributing factors**, what made the system fragile enough for this to happen\n4. **Detection gap**, how long between failure start and alert fire\n5. **Action items**, concrete, owned, time-bound\n\nThe key discipline: human error is a symptom. \"Engineer pushed wrong config\" is not a root cause. \"Config change had no staged rollout or automatic rollback\" is. Blameless doesn't mean consequence-free; it means the postmortem is focused on the system, not on who.\n\nGoogle's policy triggers mandatory postmortems when a single incident consumes over 20% of quarterly error budget. That threshold forces the conversation before the pattern becomes a habit.\n\n---\n\n## Alert Thresholds That Fire on User Impact\n\nThe most common alerting mistake: alerting on symptoms instead of user experience. CPU at 80% is not an alert. P99 latency at 2× your SLO threshold is.\n\nFor a service with a 200ms p99 SLO:\n\n```\nALERT LatencyBudgetBurn\n  IF rate(request_duration_seconds_bucket{le=\"0.2\"}[5m]) / rate(request_duration_seconds_count[5m]) < 0.99\n  FOR 2m\n  SEVERITY page\n```\n\nThis fires when fewer than 99% of requests complete in 200ms, sustained for 2 minutes. It ignores CPU, memory, and queue depth unless those are already correlated to latency in your system. They often aren't.\n\nThe \"for 2m\" guard matters. Transient spikes don't page. Sustained SLO violations do. Noisy alerts train oncall engineers to ignore pages, which is worse than no alerts at all.\n\n---\n\n## What Stays With You\n\nError budgets work because they make an invisible contract visible. The 99.9% SLO isn't just a number in a dashboard; it's the agreement between your team and your users about how often you're allowed to let them down. When engineers understand they're spending a budget, not breaking a rule, reliability becomes part of the product conversation rather than a constraint imposed after the fact.\n\nDefine SLIs that measure what users feel. Calculate budget consumption from real incident data, not hypothetical models. Wire circuit breakers with enough probe calls to distinguish recovery from luck. Write postmortems that name system failures, not people. Alert on what users experience, not what your servers feel. That's the whole loop."
