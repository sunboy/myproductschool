  "body_mdx": "## The fix HTTP/2 actually shipped\n\nHTTP/1.1 had a simple problem: browsers opened 6 parallel TCP connections per origin to work around the protocol's one-request-per-connection limit. That worked, but it burned sockets, hammered servers with parallel TLS handshakes, and wasted bandwidth on redundant TCP slow-start cycles.\n\nHTTP/2 fixed this with multiplexing. All requests share one TCP connection. The browser can send hundreds of requests without waiting, and HAProxy data shows the share of connections carrying only a single transaction dropped from 74% (HTTP/1) to 25%. Fewer connections, less overhead, faster pages. On the numbers, HTTP/2 delivered.\n\nThen engineers deployed it and hit the wall.\n\n## The problem HTTP/2 didn't fix\n\nTCP is a byte stream. It guarantees that bytes arrive in order, complete. When a packet drops, TCP stops, retransmits, and waits before delivering anything downstream of that packet, even bytes for completely unrelated requests.\n\nHTTP/2 multiplexed all those requests onto one TCP connection. Which means a single dropped packet now blocks every request on the connection simultaneously. HTTP/1.1 with six parallel connections was partially immune: one connection stalling left five others running. HTTP/2's single-connection design turned that partial immunity into a total outage.\n\nThis is TCP-level head-of-line blocking, and it gets painful on wireless networks. Mobile packet loss rates of 5-15% are common. At 15% loss on 4G, HTTP/2 pages load 55% slower than HTTP/3 pages on the same content.\n\n![HTTP/2 and HTTP/3: What Multiplexing Actually Fixes](/content-images/networking-for-engineers/http-2-and-http-3-what-multiplexing-actually-fixes.png)\n\nThe right mental model: HTTP/2 is a highway where multiple cars coordinate internally in a single lane. If a pothole stops one car, every car behind it waits. HTTP/3 gives each request its own lane. One car hitting a pothole doesn't affect the others.\n\n## How QUIC changes the stack\n\nHTTP/3 runs over QUIC instead of TCP. QUIC moves the transport layer into user space and redesigns it from scratch around the assumption that multiplexed streams need independent loss recovery.\n\nA dropped QUIC packet blocks only the stream it belongs to. CSS continues arriving while a missing image packet retransmits. This is the core property that makes HTTP/3 meaningful on lossy networks, not a protocol spec detail.\n\nQUIC also collapses the connection setup cost. A new TCP + TLS 1.2 connection requires 3 round trips before the first byte of HTTP data. TLS 1.3 brings this to 2 RTTs (1 TCP + 1 TLS). QUIC combines the transport and cryptographic handshakes into 1 RTT, and for returning visitors with session tickets, 0 RTT, meaning request data ships in the same packet as the handshake. On an 80ms RTT mobile network, that's 160ms recovered per cold connection.\n\nGoogle measured QUIC at production scale: search result load times dropped 8% on desktop and 3.6% on mobile, with the slowest 1% of users seeing 16% improvement. YouTube saw 20% less video stalling in high-loss markets like India. Those numbers come from millions of real users on real networks, not lab conditions.\n\n## What this means when you're building\n\nFor most applications behind a CDN, HTTP/3 is a free win. Cloudflare, Akamai, and Fastly all support it by default. Cloudflare's own infrastructure shows a 12.4% reduction in TTFB: 176ms versus 201ms under HTTP/2. Enable it in your CDN config, instrument TTFB by protocol in your RUM data, and verify the improvement is showing up for your actual users.\n\nThe gains concentrate where packet loss concentrates: mobile users, geographic regions with congested last-mile infrastructure, and anyone connecting over WiFi. A UNH study found 69% of HTTP/3 connections hit 5 Mbps throughput (Netflix's Full HD floor) versus 56% for HTTP/2. The delta is bigger than it looks if your users skew mobile or international.\n\nOne tradeoff worth knowing: QUIC runs over UDP, and some corporate firewalls still block UDP traffic on port 443. HTTP/3 clients fall back to HTTP/2 automatically in this case. If your user base is primarily behind enterprise network proxies, measure actual HTTP/3 adoption in your telemetry before optimizing for it.\n\nThe practical upgrade path: confirm TLS 1.3 is already enabled (it reduces handshake RTT from 2 to 1 even over TCP), enable HTTP/3 at your CDN layer, verify QUIC isn't blocked for your audience, and track TTFB by protocol. The protocol does its work quietly. The measurement tells you whether it's working."
