  "body_mdx": "## The Cost of Reliability\n\nEvery TCP connection begins with a ceremony: SYN, SYN-ACK, ACK. Three packets, one round-trip, zero data moved. At 100ms RTT, that's 100ms of pure overhead before the connection is open. Then TLS 1.2 adds two more round-trips, putting you at 300ms before the first byte of application data. On a 200ms mobile link, TLS 1.2 alone costs 600ms at connection setup.\n\nUDP skips all of this. The client sends a packet. The server may or may not receive it.\n\nThe tradeoff is not \"fast vs. slow.\" It is \"guaranteed vs. opportunistic.\" The cost of the guarantee is real, measurable, and not always worth paying.\n\n## The Numbers Behind the Handshake\n\n```\nTCP + TLS 1.2 connection timeline (100ms RTT):\n\nClient                          Server\n  |------- SYN ----------------->|\n  |<------ SYN-ACK ---------------|   +100ms (TCP open)\n  |------- ACK + ClientHello ---->|\n  |<------ ServerHello + Cert ----|   +100ms (TLS round 1)\n  |------- Finished ------------->|\n  |<------ Finished + App Data ---|   +100ms (TLS round 2)\n  |                               |\nTotal: 300ms before first response byte\n\nTCP + TLS 1.3:\n  Same TCP open (100ms), then one TLS round-trip (100ms)\nTotal: 200ms, saves one full RTT\n\nUDP (QUIC/HTTP3):\n  Combined crypto + transport handshake: 100ms\n  Resumption (0-RTT): 0ms additional\n```\n\nTLS 1.3 reduced the handshake from 3 RTT to 2 RTT by combining the TCP and TLS handshakes into fewer messages. On an 80ms RTT network, that's 240ms for TLS 1.2 versus 160ms for TLS 1.3, a savings of 80ms per fresh connection. On mobile networks where RTT regularly hits 200-400ms, this difference becomes 400ms.\n\nSession resumption changes the equation further. About 40% of HTTPS connections reuse existing sessions via session IDs or session tickets, saving at least 22.6ms and reducing server-side CPU by 10 to 100 times. A full TLS handshake is asymmetric crypto heavy. Resumption is not.\n\nHeroku saw this directly. By optimizing their SNI TLS handshake logic, they cut connection latency from 100-120ms to 20-25ms, a 5x improvement, without changing anything about the cryptographic security of the connection.\n\n## When TCP's Overhead Disappears Into the Noise\n\nThe handshake cost is amortized. A single TCP connection can carry thousands of requests. HTTP/1.1 keep-alive, HTTP/2 multiplexing, and gRPC streaming all reuse connections, turning a 200ms setup cost into a one-time expense across the lifetime of a session.\n\nNetflix uses TCP for video streaming and achieves 98% delivery success. The architecture: multiple TCP flows download discrete video segments, not a continuous stream. The client pre-buffers aggressively, pulling at 40Mbps initially to fill the buffer, then 15-25Mbps in spikes as playback proceeds. Segment-based delivery over TCP means a retransmitted packet only delays that segment, not the entire stream. The delivery guarantees simplify the ABR algorithm's job.\n\nThe counterintuitive result: TCP's reliability is not a bug for Netflix, it is a feature that lets them run simpler client logic and still hit 98% delivery.\n\n## When UDP Wins\n\nUDP wins when the cost of waiting for a retransmit exceeds the cost of losing the data.\n\nOnline gaming and live video both have this property. A game state update that arrives 200ms late is worse than no update at all. The client can interpolate from the previous frame. It cannot use a delayed frame correctly. Online gaming requires packet loss below 0.5% to remain playable, but the alternative to UDP is not clean delivery, it is 100-200ms of additional latency per packet because TCP's minimum retransmission timeout on Linux is 200ms.\n\nDNS is the canonical case study in protocol selection under constraints. A DNS query is tiny (usually under 512 bytes), the answer is usually equally tiny, and the protocol has its own error handling. UDP adds ~20ms for a cache miss (one round-trip, two packets). TCP adds ~40ms (handshake, query, response, teardown, seven minimum packets). High-volume DNS resolvers handle 100,000+ queries per second with minimal file descriptors over UDP. TCP DNS at scale drops to 25% of UDP baseline throughput, because one file descriptor per connection becomes the bottleneck.\n\n```mermaid\ngraph LR\n    A[Choose TCP or UDP?] --> B{Is data integrity critical?}\n    B -- Yes --> C{Is latency < 200ms critical?}\n    B -- No --> D[UDP]\n    C -- Yes --> E{Is connection long-lived?}\n    C -- No --> F[TCP]\n    E -- Yes --> F\n    E -- No --> G{QUIC available?}\n    G -- Yes --> H[QUIC/HTTP3]\n    G -- No --> F\n```\n\n## QUIC: Collapsing the Handshake Layers\n\nQUIC, the protocol underlying HTTP/3, started at Google as an attempt to solve the handshake problem without waiting for TCP changes in operating systems. It runs over UDP and combines transport and crypto negotiation into a single round-trip.\n\nCloudflare offers HTTP/3 to all customers. The results: 176ms average TTFB versus 201ms for HTTP/2, a 12.4% improvement. At P95, HTTP/3 connections are 116ms faster. About 31% of Internet traffic now uses HTTP/3. Meta deployed QUIC for roughly 75% of all their Internet traffic.\n\nThe improvement is not just handshake speed. HTTP/2 over TCP has a head-of-line blocking problem: a single lost packet stalls all multiplexed streams because TCP delivers data in order. QUIC streams are independent. Lose a packet in stream 3 and streams 1, 2, and 4 continue uninterrupted.\n\nThe edge case: QUIC underperforms on fast, low-loss networks. At high bandwidth and near-zero loss, QUIC's measured RTT runs at 16.2ms versus HTTP/2's 1.9ms. The QUIC stack runs in user space, not the kernel, so it pays context-switching overhead that TCP avoids. On fiber with 0% packet loss, that gap is real. On any realistic mobile or WAN path, QUIC wins.\n\n## Congestion Control Is Not a Protocol Detail\n\nThe choice of congestion algorithm has measurable production impact. TCP's default algorithm, CUBIC, is loss-based: it expands the congestion window until it detects a lost packet, then backs off. This works but keeps network buffers full and creates background latency.\n\nBBR takes a different approach, measuring actual delivery rate and round-trip time to model the network path and stay just below the congestion point. Google switched all B4 traffic to BBR in 2016. YouTube throughput improved 4% globally, 14% or more in some countries. BBRv3, which Google now runs on all Google.com and YouTube traffic, delivers 0.2% further latency reduction.\n\n0.2% sounds trivial. At Google's scale, it is not, and the engineering effort to measure and A/B test at that precision is what distinguishes production network engineering from configuration changes.\n\n## What to Actually Do\n\nPick TCP when connections are long-lived, data integrity is required, or you need HTTP tooling. Pick UDP when you have per-packet latency budgets, built-in error tolerance, or are implementing something game-like. Use QUIC where you control the stack and your users are on realistic (non-lab) networks.\n\nThe conversation about TCP versus UDP usually happens too early. The real lever is further up: are your connections being reused, are sessions being resumed, is your congestion algorithm tuned for your actual network path? A CUBIC-based TCP connection on a well-configured connection pool will outperform a naive QUIC implementation every time. Protocol choice is one input. The rest is engineering."
