  "body_mdx": "## The Problem with Treating Symptoms as Problems\n\nWhen a metric drops, the instinctive response is to name the metric as the problem. Checkout conversion is low. Activation rate fell. Day-7 retention dropped three points. The team opens a sprint to 'improve' these numbers, and builds things in the direction of the symptom.\n\nThis works sometimes, by accident. The team ships a change that happens to touch the actual cause, and the metric recovers. But it fails systematically because symptoms are downstream of causes. Optimizing a checkout flow when the real issue is that users don't trust the pricing model will produce marginal gains and then stall.\n\nThe upstream framing move is the discipline of asking: what behavior, or absence of behavior, produces this metric reading? You are rewriting the problem statement from a measurement into a mechanism.\n\n---\n\n## Upstream vs. Downstream: A Structural View\n\nThink of a product metric as the output of a chain of user decisions and system behaviors. Each link in the chain is upstream of the metric.\n\n```\nUser intent\n    ↓\nDiscovery (can they find the thing?)\n    ↓\nMotivation (do they want to do it?)\n    ↓\nFriction (can they complete the action?)\n    ↓\nTrust (do they believe the outcome?)\n    ↓\n[Metric reading]\n```\n\nWhen a metric reads low, the cause lives somewhere in this chain. The metric tells you the output is wrong. It does not tell you where the chain is breaking.\n\nA team that sets out to 'improve checkout conversion' might build better progress indicators, simplify form fields, or add autofill. All of those address friction. But if the real break is at trust (users don't believe the refund policy), friction improvements return noise.\n\n---\n\n## The Restatement Test\n\nA well-framed problem statement has a specific shape:\n\n> [Person] is trying to [accomplish X]. [Specific blocker] is preventing them. If unresolved, [consequence].\n\nThis is not template-filling for its own sake. The constraint forces three things:\n- You must name who is affected (not 'users', but which users in what state)\n- You must identify a mechanism, not a measurement ('can't find the price until step 4' not 'low conversion')\n- You must connect the blocker to a consequence that matters\n\nHere is the test: can a new engineer reading your problem statement design a correct solution without asking clarifying questions? If the answer is no, the problem statement is still a symptom description.\n\n**Before (symptom):** Checkout conversion is 3.1%, below our 4.5% target.\n\n**After (cause):** First-time buyers are abandoning at the payment step because shipping cost appears for the first time after they've entered card details. They expected free shipping based on the promotional banner on the homepage.\n\nThe second version tells you where to look. The first version tells you there is a fire somewhere in the building.\n\n---\n\n## Why Teams Stay at the Symptom Level\n\nThere are three common traps:\n\n**The measurement is the deliverable.** If the team's success metric is 'improve checkout conversion by 1 point', and the metric is the goal, then the metric becomes the problem statement by default. There is no structural incentive to ask why.\n\n**The cause requires cross-team coordination.** If the cause is in a part of the system owned by a different team (the banner copy is owned by marketing, not product), naming it as the cause is inconvenient. Symptom framing lets the current team build something that is fully within their control, even if it does not address the actual break.\n\n**Speed pressure collapses investigation.** Under deadline pressure, root cause investigation feels like delay. The team knows it should dig, but the sprint planning meeting starts in an hour.\n\nNone of these traps are irrational. They are rational responses to bad incentive structures. But they produce a pattern where the team ships a lot and moves the metric a little, and leadership concludes that the metric is 'sticky' when what is actually sticky is the underlying cause.\n\n---\n\n## The Upstream Question Protocol\n\nA lightweight protocol for reframing:\n\n1. **State the metric reading.** Checkout conversion is 3.1%.\n2. **Ask who specifically is affected.** Which users? New vs. returning? Mobile vs. desktop? Users from a specific acquisition channel?\n3. **Ask what action they are taking (or not taking).** At what step do they leave? What do they do instead? Do they return later?\n4. **Ask what they would need to be true for them to complete the action.** What belief, information, or condition is missing?\n5. **State the mechanism.** Write the upstream cause in behavioral terms.\n\nStep 4 is the hardest and the most important. It requires you to simulate the user's decision at the point of abandonment, not from your perspective as the builder but from their perspective as someone with incomplete information and competing priorities.\n\n```mermaid\nflowchart LR\n    A[Metric reading\\n'Checkout conversion: 3.1%'] --> B[Who?\\nFirst-time buyers, mobile]\n    B --> C[What action?\\nAbandoning at payment step]\n    C --> D[What would need to be true?\\nExpected free shipping]\n    D --> E[Mechanism\\nShipping cost surprise at payment step]\n    E --> F[Problem statement\\nFirst-time buyers abandon at payment\\nbecause shipping cost appears late]\n```\n\n---\n\n## Upstream Framing as a Team Norm\n\nThe restatement test works as a team norm, not just a personal habit. When someone brings a problem to a planning session, the team's default question is: 'what is the mechanism?' not 'what should we build?'\n\nThis creates an uncomfortable gap between problem identification and solution design. That gap is productive. It is where investigation lives. Teams that collapse the gap immediately (symptom seen, solution decided) optimize for the appearance of decisiveness at the cost of accuracy.\n\nThe upstream framing move does not require a week of user research before any decision is made. It requires a few minutes of disciplined questioning to check whether the problem statement is pointing at a mechanism or a measurement. That is a different thing from 'move fast', not an obstacle to it."
