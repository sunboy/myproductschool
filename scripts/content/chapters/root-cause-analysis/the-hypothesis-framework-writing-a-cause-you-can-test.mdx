  "hook_text": "A hypothesis that cannot be wrong is not a hypothesis. It is a belief. The discipline of writing testable causes is not a research methodology. It is the only way to know whether your diagnosis is correct before you build something.",
  "body_mdx": "## Hypotheses vs. Beliefs\n\nAfter a metric investigation, most teams reach a conclusion that sounds like this: 'We think users are dropping off because the onboarding is too long.' This is a belief. It may be well-founded. It may be supported by qualitative signals. But it is not a hypothesis, because there is no stated condition under which it would be considered wrong.\n\nA testable hypothesis has four components:\n\n1. **A cause.** A specific, behavioral, upstream explanation for the observed metric movement.\n2. **A predicted effect.** What the metric would do if the cause is real.\n3. **A measurement.** The specific number or observation that would confirm or refute the prediction.\n4. **A falsification condition.** The outcome that would cause the team to conclude the hypothesis was wrong.\n\nWithout the fourth component, a hypothesis is unfalsifiable. Teams can confirm it by selectively interpreting results, and they frequently do.\n\n---\n\n## The Four-Part Structure\n\nHere is the template:\n\n> **Cause:** We believe [specific mechanism] is responsible for [observed metric change].\n> **Predicted effect:** If this is true, [intervention] will produce [measurable change].\n> **Measurement:** We will observe this via [metric] over [time period].\n> **Falsification:** We will conclude the hypothesis is wrong if [the metric does not move / moves in the wrong direction / we see specific counter-evidence].\n\nApplied to a retention investigation:\n\n> **Cause:** We believe users who do not complete the profile setup step in session 1 lack the context to find the core feature, causing low week-1 activation and subsequent churn.\n> **Predicted effect:** Prompting users to complete profile setup before accessing the main feed will increase week-1 activation for new users.\n> **Measurement:** Week-1 activation rate (users who complete at least one core action in their first 7 days) for the experiment group vs. control, over 4 weeks.\n> **Falsification:** If the experiment group's week-1 activation is not at least 5 percentage points higher than control, or if we observe that profile-complete users still churn at the same rate as incomplete users, the hypothesis is wrong.\n\n---\n\n## Why Specificity in the Cause Matters\n\nThe cause statement is where most hypotheses go vague. 'Onboarding is too long' is not specific enough to test. How long is too long? For which users? At which point in onboarding does the length become the problem?\n\nA specific cause points to a mechanism. Compare:\n\n**Vague:** Users churn because onboarding is unclear.\n\n**Specific:** Users who reach the 'connect your calendar' step but do not complete it (approximately 40% of users who reach this step) churn at 3x the rate of users who complete it. The step has a 6-field form that requires OAuth authorization from a separate window. We believe the interruption caused by the OAuth popup is breaking the flow for users on mobile.\n\nThe specific version tells you exactly what to test: simplify or defer the calendar connection step on mobile and measure the completion rate at that step, then the downstream activation and retention.\n\nSpecificity in the cause also makes the team more honest. A vague cause allows a team to claim success if any retention metric improves, even marginally. A specific cause produces a targeted prediction that either lands or does not.\n\n---\n\n## Confounding and What You Are Not Controlling For\n\nA testable hypothesis is designed to be run as an experiment, but not all hypotheses can be tested with a controlled A/B test. Observational tests are often the only option, and they introduce confounders.\n\nA confounder is a third variable that is correlated with both the proposed cause and the observed effect, producing the appearance of a causal relationship when the real cause is something else.\n\nExample: users who complete more onboarding steps retain better. Hypothesis: completing more steps causes better retention. Alternative explanation: users who are more motivated to succeed in the product complete more steps and also retain better. The motivation is the cause of both the step completion and the retention. Forcing all users to complete more steps (by removing the skip option) will not replicate the correlation, because the intervention changes the population (now including low-motivation users forced through the steps).\n\nThe hypothesis should state which confounders the team is aware of and how the test design controls for them. If confounders cannot be controlled, the results should be labeled as suggestive, not conclusive.\n\n```mermaid\nflowchart LR\n    A[More onboarding steps completed] --> B[Higher retention]\n    C[User motivation] -.->|confounder| A\n    C -.->|confounder| B\n    style C stroke-dasharray: 5 5\n```\n\n---\n\n## Setting the Falsification Threshold Before the Test\n\nThe falsification threshold must be set before the test runs, not after. Setting it afterward is a form of motivated reasoning: the team sees the results, then decides what threshold counts as success.\n\nA useful threshold has:\n\n- **A minimum detectable effect.** Based on the current baseline and the expected impact of the intervention. A 1-point change in activation rate when the baseline is 42% is likely noise; a 5-point change is detectable with reasonable sample sizes.\n- **A time window.** How long will the test run? Week-1 activation requires 1 week plus acquisition time. Day-30 retention requires 30 days plus acquisition time. The window must be long enough to measure the predicted effect.\n- **A secondary check.** What else would confirm or refute the mechanism? If the hypothesis is about the OAuth popup causing mobile abandonment, the completion rate at the calendar step (not just downstream retention) should also move. If downstream retention improves but the step completion rate does not change, the mechanism was probably wrong and something else drove the result.\n\n---\n\n## The Difference Between Testing a Hypothesis and Running a Feature Experiment\n\nFeature experiments test whether a specific change improves a metric. Hypothesis tests are narrower: they test whether a specific mechanism is the cause of a specific problem.\n\nThe distinction matters because feature experiments can produce false positives. A redesigned onboarding flow that improves activation may do so by being faster, more visual, more socially reinforced, or by shifting user expectations. If the team does not know which mechanism drove the improvement, the next iteration of onboarding improvements will be as exploratory as the first.\n\nHypothesis testing is tighter: the test is designed to confirm or refute one mechanism. This produces knowledge about the product and user behavior that accumulates, rather than a series of individual wins that do not compound into understanding.\n\n---\n\n## Hypothesis as a Communication Tool\n\nA well-written hypothesis is also a communication tool. When a team presents a root cause investigation to stakeholders, the hypothesis frame sets clear expectations:\n\n- This is what we believe the cause is (not a confirmed fact)\n- This is what we expect to happen if we are right\n- This is how we will know if we are wrong\n- This is when we will have results\n\nThis is a fundamentally different posture from 'we think the onboarding is too long so we are going to shorten it.' The hypothesis frame signals analytical rigor and creates a forcing function for honest evaluation of results.\n\nThe goal is not hypothesis-writing as ceremony. It is a discipline that prevents teams from mistaking activity for learning and changes for solutions."
