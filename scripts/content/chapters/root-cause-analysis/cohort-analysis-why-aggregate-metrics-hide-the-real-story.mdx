  "body_mdx": "## Why Averages Obscure\n\nA team tracking day-30 retention sees 35% for three months straight. They conclude retention is stable and move attention elsewhere. But underneath the flat average:\n\n- The January cohort started at 42% and has been declining month over month\n- A February product change acquired a new type of user who retains at 22%\n- The decline in old cohorts and the influx of low-retaining new users are exactly offsetting each other\n\nThe 35% average is telling the team everything is fine. The per-cohort view tells them two separate problems are developing simultaneously. Aggregate metrics can mask trends not just by blending, but by having trends that cancel each other.\n\n---\n\n## What a Cohort Is\n\nA cohort is a group of users defined by when they joined or took a first action. The most common definition: users who signed up in the same calendar week or month.\n\nCohort analysis tracks what those users do over time, relative to when they joined. This matters because:\n\n1. **Product changes affect cohorts at different points in their lifecycle.** A change shipped in March affects February users at month 2 of their lifecycle and January users at month 3. The effect can look different at different lifecycle points.\n\n2. **User populations shift.** The users signing up in Q4 are not the same population as the users who signed up in Q1. Acquisition channel mix, marketing creative, competitive dynamics, and seasonality all affect who shows up. Tracking them together produces an average of heterogeneous populations.\n\n3. **Problems compound silently.** If each cohort retains slightly worse than the previous one, the aggregate metric declines slowly. The slope looks manageable. The per-cohort view shows a systematic degradation that will eventually cause a cliff.\n\n---\n\n## Reading a Retention Curve Grid\n\nCohort retention is typically displayed as a grid. Rows are cohorts (signup week/month). Columns are periods after signup (week 1, week 2, ...). Cells show the retention percentage for that cohort at that period.\n\n```\n           W1    W2    W3    W4    W5    W6\nJan        100%   62%   48%   41%   38%   35%\nFeb        100%   59%   44%   37%   33%\nMar        100%   55%   40%   32%\nApr        100%   51%   36%\nMay        100%   47%\nJun        100%\n```\n\nReading down a column (same period, different cohorts) shows whether retention at that lifecycle point is improving or degrading over time. In this grid, W2 retention has gone from 62% to 47% across 6 cohorts. Each new cohort is retaining worse at week 2. Something changed in the product or the user population, and it is getting worse.\n\nReading across a row (same cohort, different periods) shows the decay curve for a specific group. The January cohort retained 62% at W2 and 35% at W6. Whether that decay rate is acceptable depends on the benchmark for that product type.\n\nReading diagonally shows what happened at a specific calendar date across all cohorts. If something changed at the W3 point for all cohorts simultaneously (a diagonal pattern), a product change at that calendar point is the likely cause.\n\n---\n\n## Diagnosing with Cohort Analysis\n\nCohort analysis produces three diagnostic patterns, each pointing to a different cause:\n\n**Same-period degradation (column trend):** Each new cohort performs worse at the same lifecycle point. This usually means the incoming user population is changing (different acquisition channels, different use cases), or a product change degraded the experience for new users.\n\n**Lifecycle decay (row trend):** A specific cohort's retention drops faster than expected after a certain period. This usually means something in the long-term product experience is failing: a feature users rely on broke, pricing changed, or a competitor launched something compelling.\n\n**Calendar-date effect (diagonal pattern):** Retention drops at the same real-world date for users at different lifecycle stages. This points to an external event: an outage, a policy change, a competitor announcement, or seasonal behavior.\n\n```mermaid\nflowchart TD\n    A[Retention metric flagged] --> B{Read cohort grid}\n    B --> C[Column trend: each cohort worse at same period]\n    B --> D[Row trend: one cohort decays faster after a point]\n    B --> E[Diagonal: all cohorts drop at same calendar date]\n    C --> F[Population mix or new-user experience change]\n    D --> G[Long-term product or competitive issue for that cohort]\n    E --> H[External event or product change on that date]\n```\n\n---\n\n## Cohort Analysis Beyond Retention\n\nRetention is the most common use case, but cohort analysis applies to any time-series behavior:\n\n**Conversion funnels by cohort:** Did the signup-to-purchase conversion rate change for recent cohorts? If a pricing change launched in February, the post-February cohorts will show it; pre-February cohorts will not. The cohort grid isolates the effect.\n\n**Revenue by cohort:** Are recent cohorts generating less revenue per user over their lifecycle than earlier cohorts? This is the unit economics question. LTV calculated from aggregate averages is unreliable if cohort LTV is trending down.\n\n**Feature adoption by cohort:** Did a feature launched in March get adopted differently by users who joined before vs. after the feature existed? New users who encounter the feature during onboarding may adopt at very different rates than existing users who see it as a change to a familiar product.\n\n---\n\n## When Cohort Analysis Misleads\n\nCohort analysis is not a universal solution. Three failure modes:\n\n**Small cohort sizes.** If weekly cohorts have fewer than a few hundred users, week-over-week retention percentages will have high variance. A W2 retention of 55% vs. 51% from cohorts of 80 users is noise. Aggregate to monthly cohorts or use confidence intervals.\n\n**Mixing acquisition channels in one cohort.** If January included a viral spike from a news article, the January cohort is a blend of high-intent organic users and low-intent curious users from the article. Splitting by channel before cohort analysis gives cleaner signal.\n\n**Confounding survival bias.** Users who reach longer lifecycle periods are, by definition, the users who stayed. Their behavior at later periods is not representative of the full cohort's behavior. This matters when trying to understand what drives long-term retention: you are studying survivors, not the general population.\n\n---\n\n## The Diagnostic Habit\n\nWhen a retention or engagement metric needs investigation, the first move is to pull the cohort grid, not the aggregate trend. The aggregate trend tells you there is something to investigate. The cohort grid tells you the structure of the problem.\n\nA team that builds this into standard practice finds that many 'mysterious' metric movements have clear structural causes once the aggregate is decomposed. The mystery is usually in the blending, not in the behavior."
