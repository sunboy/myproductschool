  "body_mdx": "## Metrics as Imperfect Proxies\n\nEvery product metric is a proxy. 'Daily active users' does not measure whether users got value from the product today. It measures whether they triggered a session event. 'Checkout conversion rate' does not measure whether users wanted to buy. It measures whether they completed the checkout form.\n\nThis distinction matters because the gap between what you measure and what you care about is exactly where misdiagnoses happen. When a metric moves, the instinct is to treat the metric change as the thing that needs explaining. But the metric is just the signal. The thing that needs explaining is the behavioral change the metric is tracking imperfectly.\n\n---\n\n## Three Things a Metric Movement Can Mean\n\nWhen a metric goes down, there are three structural possibilities:\n\n**The underlying behavior changed.** Users actually are doing something less (or more). This is what everyone assumes and what is sometimes true.\n\n**The measurement changed.** A tracking event stopped firing, a definition changed, a new platform or device is not instrumented. The behavior is the same; what you are counting changed.\n\n**The population mix changed.** The metric is an aggregate. The composition of users in the denominator shifted in a way that pulls the average without any individual's behavior changing.\n\nSkipping the first check (measurement integrity) is the most common analysis error. A team spends a sprint investigating user behavior and building changes, when the metric drop was caused by a missing tracking pixel on a new page variant.\n\n---\n\n## Measurement Integrity First\n\nBefore analyzing why users are behaving differently, confirm the measurement has not changed.\n\nChecks:\n- Did any instrumentation code deploy around the time the metric dropped?\n- Are all expected events still firing? Check raw event counts, not just derived metrics.\n- Did any page, flow, or platform change in a way that might not be instrumented?\n- Has the metric definition or calculation query changed?\n- Is the drop distributed across all data sources, or concentrated in one (e.g., one SDK version, one device type)?\n\n```\nDrop detected in checkout conversion\n           |\n    ┌──────┴──────┐\n    ▼             ▼\nMeasurement OK?  Population mix OK?\n    |                 |\n    ▼                 ▼\nBehavior           Segment\ninvestigation       investigation\n```\n\nIf the measurement is broken, the behavioral investigation is running on corrupted input. Fix the measurement first.\n\n---\n\n## Population Mix Effects\n\nSuppose activation rate (the share of new users who complete a core action within 7 days) is 42% this week, down from 48% last week. The team starts analyzing why users are less likely to activate.\n\nBut there is another explanation. Suppose a paid campaign launched last week, bringing in a large cohort of lower-intent users from a new channel. These users activate at 28%, vs. the baseline of 50% for organic users. The activation rate for each individual group did not change. The mix of groups did.\n\nThis is Simpson's Paradox in practice: an aggregate trend that reverses or disappears when you look at the subgroups.\n\n```\nWeek prior:  90% organic (50% activate) + 10% paid (28% activate)\n             → blended rate: 0.9 × 50 + 0.1 × 28 = 47.8%\n\nThis week:   70% organic (50% activate) + 30% paid (28% activate)\n             → blended rate: 0.7 × 50 + 0.3 × 28 = 43.4%\n\nNo behavior changed. Mix shifted.\n```\n\nThe diagnostic move: segment by acquisition channel, device type, geography, or user state (new vs. returning) and check whether the per-segment rates held steady. If they did, the aggregate change is a composition effect, not a behavior change.\n\n---\n\n## The Signal Interpretation Framework\n\nFor any metric movement, work through these questions in order:\n\n**1. Is the measurement intact?**\nCheck instrumentation, query definitions, and data pipeline logs before anything else.\n\n**2. Is the population mix stable?**\nDecompose by acquisition source, device, region, and user cohort. Check whether the numerator and denominator are shifting proportionally.\n\n**3. When did the change start, exactly?**\nPinpoint the start date to the hour if possible. Cross-reference with deploy logs, campaign launches, and external events. Correlation with a specific event is strong evidence of causation.\n\n**4. Who is affected?**\nIf the measurement is intact and the mix is stable, identify which segment is driving the change. Not all users changed behavior. Which ones did?\n\n**5. What changed for that segment at that time?**\nNow you have a specific population and a start time. Investigate what changed in the product or context for those users in that window.\n\n---\n\n## Timing as Evidence\n\nExact timing is underused evidence. If a metric started dropping at 14:32 on a Tuesday, and a code deployment landed at 14:15 on that Tuesday, that is significant. The probability that an unrelated behavioral shift would coincide with a deployment to within 20 minutes is low.\n\nConversely, if the metric started declining smoothly over three weeks with no corresponding events, the cause is likely something gradual: a competitor launched, content quality drifted, or a slow accumulation of users hitting a friction point.\n\nThe shape of the decline also carries information:\n\n- **Sudden step drop:** Usually an event. Deployment, campaign end, external outage.\n- **Gradual slope:** Usually a trend. Competitive pressure, compounding engagement decay.\n- **Periodic dip:** Usually correlated with a calendar pattern. Day-of-week, seasonal, or billing cycle effects.\n\n```mermaid\nflowchart LR\n    A[Sudden step drop] --> B[Check deploy logs,\\nexternal events]\n    C[Gradual slope] --> D[Check competitive,\\ncontent drift, engagement decay]\n    E[Periodic dip] --> F[Check calendar correlation:\\nday-of-week, billing cycle]\n```\n\n---\n\n## What the Number Is Not Telling You\n\nA single aggregate metric number tells you approximately one thing: whether the output of a system is above or below a threshold. It does not tell you:\n\n- Which part of the system changed\n- Which users are affected\n- Whether the change is behavioral or compositional or instrumentation\n- Whether the change matters (a drop in MAU caused by removing bad actors is good)\n\nTreating the metric number as the unit of analysis leads to responses that are directionally correct but poorly targeted. The response to 'activation rate dropped 6 points' is not a suite of activation improvements distributed across the funnel. It is an investigation into which segment's activation changed, when, and why, followed by a targeted response to that specific cause.\n\nThe number gives you permission to investigate. The investigation gives you the actual signal."
