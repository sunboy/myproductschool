  "body_mdx": "## What Five Whys Is Actually Trying to Do\n\nThe Five Whys protocol was developed at Toyota to trace manufacturing defects to root causes. The idea: ask why the problem occurred, then ask why that cause occurred, repeating until you reach a cause that a process change can address.\n\nIn product work, the protocol gets misapplied. Teams run Five Whys and end up at conclusions like 'users are not engaged' or 'our onboarding needs improvement.' These are not root causes. They are restatements of the original problem at a higher level of abstraction.\n\nThe failure mode is not the protocol. It is what counts as a valid answer to 'why.'.\n\n---\n\n## The Testability Standard\n\nEach answer in a Five Whys chain must meet this standard: it is a claim that could be confirmed or refuted with data or direct observation.\n\n**Not testable:** 'Users are confused.'\n**Testable:** 'Users visit the pricing help article an average of 2.3 times per session before completing purchase.'\n\n**Not testable:** 'Our onboarding is not effective.'\n**Testable:** 'Users who do not complete the profile setup step within the first session have a 14-day retention rate of 8%, vs. 41% for users who complete it.'\n\n**Not testable:** 'The product experience is unclear.'\n**Testable:** 'In usability sessions, 7 of 8 participants could not locate the export button without being prompted.'\n\nThe pattern: a testable claim names a specific behavior, population, and ideally a measurement or observation that could contradict it. A non-testable claim describes a state of affairs that everyone can agree sounds plausible without any evidence changing their minds.\n\n---\n\n## Walking a Chain That Holds\n\nHere is a Five Whys chain that stays testable at each step.\n\n**Observation:** Monthly active users (MAU) dropped 11% month-over-month.\n\n**Why #1:** Why did MAU drop?\nBecause day-30 retention fell from 38% to 27% in the cohort that joined six weeks ago.\n*(Testable: retention curve data confirms this.)*\n\n**Why #2:** Why did day-30 retention fall for that cohort?\nBecause users in that cohort complete an average of 1.2 core actions in their first week, vs. 3.4 for the previous cohort.\n*(Testable: event logs confirm action count per cohort.)*\n\n**Why #3:** Why did first-week action count drop?\nBecause the feature tutorial was removed from the onboarding flow in the deployment three days before that cohort started arriving.\n*(Testable: deploy log timestamps and cohort entry timestamps confirm the overlap.)*\n\n**Why #4:** Why was the tutorial removed?\nBecause it was flagged as 'low usage' in a dashboard review, and the team deprioritized it without checking whether low-usage correlated with low retention.\n*(Testable: the dashboard data and the meeting notes confirm the decision rationale.)*\n\n**Why #5:** Why was the connection between tutorial usage and retention not checked?\nBecause the team's standard dashboard shows tutorial completion as a standalone metric, not segmented by subsequent retention.\n*(Testable: the dashboard schema confirms this omission.)*\n\n**Root cause:** The metric reporting system does not connect onboarding step completion to downstream retention, so the team made a removal decision based on incomplete signal.\n\n```mermaid\nflowchart TD\n    A[MAU dropped 11%] --> B[Day-30 retention fell for new cohort]\n    B --> C[First-week action count dropped]\n    C --> D[Tutorial removed from onboarding flow]\n    D --> E[Tutorial flagged as 'low usage'\\nwithout retention check]\n    E --> F[Dashboard does not connect\\nonboarding steps to retention]\n    F --> G[Root cause: metric architecture gap]\n```\n\nThis chain ends somewhere actionable: fix the dashboard to show onboarding step completion segmented by downstream retention.\n\n---\n\n## Common Ways the Chain Breaks Down\n\n**Abstract conclusions:** When the answer to 'why' is a description of the original problem in different words, the chain is not making progress. 'Users churned because they didn't find value' is 'users churned' with extra syllables.\n\n**Causal claims without evidence:** 'Users probably found our competitor more compelling' may be true, but without data supporting it, it is a hypothesis dressed as a finding. Name it as a hypothesis, not as the next link in the chain.\n\n**Stopping at a person instead of a system.** 'Because the engineer who made the change didn't think about retention' ends the chain at blame rather than mechanism. The more useful question: why did the process not catch this? That points to a fixable system.\n\n**Branching into too many causes.** A single symptom often has multiple contributing causes. If Why #2 produces three competing answers, run separate chains. Do not try to merge incompatible branches.\n\n---\n\n## The Branching Problem\n\nReal product problems have multiple causes. Five Whys assumes a linear chain, which breaks down when causes are parallel.\n\nThe fix: identify the primary causal pathway first. Ask which single cause, if removed, would have the largest impact on the observed symptom. Run the full chain on that. Then run secondary chains on the remaining contributors.\n\nThis is not a claim that only one cause matters. It is a claim that you investigate one pathway at a time so the analysis stays falsifiable.\n\n---\n\n## From Root Cause to Action\n\nA Five Whys chain that stays testable produces a root cause with a specific property: it points to something a team can actually change. Not 'improve the user experience', but 'add retention segmentation to the onboarding metrics dashboard'.\n\nThe actionability test: can you write a task card for the root cause with a clear definition of done? If yes, the chain ended in the right place. If the card would read 'improve engagement', you stopped too early or your chain went abstract at some point.\n\nFive Whys is not a rigid ritual. Three whys is fine if the chain bottoms out at something testable and actionable. Seven whys is fine if the chain has not arrived at a fixable mechanism yet. The number is incidental. The standard is: each link is testable, and the final link is something you can address.\n\n---\n\n## A Checklist for Valid Chains\n\nBefore presenting a Five Whys analysis:\n\n- Each answer names a specific behavior, event, or measurement (not a general state)\n- Each answer could be confirmed or refuted with available data or observation\n- The final cause points to a process, system, or decision that can be changed\n- The chain does not stop at a person's fault without asking why the system allowed the fault\n- If the chain branched, the primary pathway is identified and the branch is noted separately\n\nA chain that passes this checklist produces a root cause. Everything else is diagnosis theater."
