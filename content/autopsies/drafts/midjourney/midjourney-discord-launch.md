<!-- beat: lede -->

In March 2022, David Holz launched a text-to-image AI generator inside a Discord server. There was no website to visit, no account to create, and no onboarding flow to click through. Users who wanted access joined the server, typed a prompt into a chat channel, and watched their image appear in public — visible to everyone else in the channel at the same moment.

This was not a soft launch or a technical shortcut. It was a considered decision about where creative work happens and who sees it. Holz had a specific theory about creative tools and the communities around them, and Discord was not a compromise — it was the point.

By July 2022, when Midjourney opened its beta to anyone with a Discord account, the server had become something that no AI art tool had been before: a space where the act of creating and the act of watching others create were the same activity. The resulting word-of-mouth was not a side effect of the product. It was the product's primary distribution mechanism, built into the interface from the first day.

<!-- beat: glance -->
## At a glance

1. **No website, just Discord.** Midjourney launched its closed beta in March 2022 inside an existing Discord server. Users typed prompts as chat messages; images appeared in public channels for all to see. [midjourney-verge]

2. **The distribution problem is harder than the AI problem.** Most AI creative tools in 2022 built their own platforms, found their own users, and struggled with both. Midjourney launched on a platform with hundreds of millions of existing users.

3. **The obvious wrong answer.** Every standard playbook said: build a web app, control the interface, own the sign-up flow. Holz made creation public and social instead of private and contained. [holz-economist]

4. **The mechanism: public creation as advertising.** Every image generated in a public channel was visible to every other user. Watching someone else's image generate was the most compelling demonstration the product could offer.

5. **The evidence.** By late 2022, Midjourney's Discord server had approximately three million members. By end of 2023, the company reportedly generated around $200M in annual recurring revenue with a team of roughly forty people and no dedicated sales function. [midjourney-growth]

6. **The takeaway.** Distribution is a design decision. Where creation happens determines who sees it, and who sees it determines whether the product grows.

## Background

![Hatch gesturing between an empty website and a busy Discord channel — see promptForCodex](/images/placeholder.png)

The AI image generation landscape in early 2022 was technically exciting and commercially awkward. DALL-E, OpenAI's text-to-image model, was operating under a restricted waitlist that gave access to researchers and selected creators. Stable Diffusion was preparing for a public release that would put model weights in users' hands but offer no particular interface or community. The tools existed. The problem was that they existed in isolation — you used them alone, saw results alone, and had no particular reason to tell anyone about them unless you were already deep in AI research circles.

David Holz came to this landscape from a different angle. Before Midjourney, he had co-founded Leap Motion, a hardware company building hand-tracking controllers, which gave him a decade of experience watching creative tools fail to find audiences. He had thought a great deal about why some tools generate communities and others don't, and his answer was something like: tools that let you see other people using them generate communities because demonstration is the most powerful form of advertising, and the only sustainable form.

Discord was the largest platform in 2022 where people gathered in communities organized around shared interests rather than social graphs. Its channel structure meant that creation in public was native to the experience. When Holz decided to build Midjourney's closed beta inside a Discord server, he was not making a pragmatic infrastructure choice. He was choosing a platform whose social architecture matched his theory about how creative tools spread.

<!-- beat: choice -->
## The obvious answer and what shipped instead

| The tempting move | What shipped |
|---|---|
| Dedicated web app with account system | Discord server with no website at launch |
| Private image generation (only creator sees output) | All image generation visible to all channel members |
| Invite-only drip with controlled growth | Open beta to anyone with Discord account by July 2022 |
| Curated gallery feature added later | Real-time public feed as the primary interface |

The tempting move would have given Holz control over the interface, the brand experience, and the user data from day one. It also would have required building distribution from scratch, which is the part most startups underestimate.

The choice Holz made outsourced distribution to the platform's existing social mechanics. He did not build distribution. He embedded creation inside a platform where distribution happened automatically.

<!-- beat: mechanism -->
## How it actually works

The core mechanic is simple to describe and surprisingly hard to replicate. When a user types a `/imagine` command in a Midjourney Discord channel, the image generation process runs visibly in the channel. Other users see the prompt appear, watch the image resolve from noise to clarity over thirty seconds or so, and see the result. They can react, ask questions, or type their own prompt immediately.

This produces two things simultaneously. First, it teaches by demonstration. A new user who has never typed an AI image prompt can watch ten other people's prompts and outputs before attempting their own — which means they arrive at their first generation with a much better mental model of what works than a user who arrived at a blank prompt field on a website. Second, it creates perpetual novelty. The channel feed is never the same twice, because every user's prompt is different. There is always something new to look at, which means there is always a reason to stay in the channel.

The viral loop embedded in this mechanic is not incidental. A user who sees an image they could not have imagined generating themselves has an obvious question: "how did you do that?" The answer — "just type a prompt in this channel" — is an invitation. The invitation requires no referral code, no marketing email, no landing page. It requires only a Discord account, which most of the target audience already had. [discord-strategy]

What Midjourney honored as a constraint was transparency: creation was public by default. What it did not honor was control: users could not generate images privately in the early months. That tradeoff was unusual in an industry where user data is treated as something to be collected privately before being aggregated. Midjourney made the opposite choice, and the result was a community where the aggregate behavior — the public feed — was more valuable than any individual user's data.

## Evidence

The public record for Midjourney's growth is somewhat imprecise because the company has never published official user or revenue figures. What the record does confirm: the open beta launched July 12, 2022, and the Discord server's growth in the months following was rapid enough to make it consistently the largest server of its type on the platform. [midjourney-verge]

The $200M ARR figure reported by The Information in late 2023 has not been officially confirmed, but it has not been denied, and multiple independent trade press sources cite similar figures. The detail that makes the revenue figure more credible than usual is the accompanying claim about team size: approximately forty people. A $200M business run by forty people with no dedicated sales function is unusual enough to be notable, and the Discord model — subscription revenue from a community that self-serves onboarding through the public feed — is a coherent explanation for how it happened. ## The takeaway

![Hatch showing creation and sharing as inseparable — see promptForCodex](/images/placeholder.png)

> **Distribution is a design decision — where creation happens determines who sees it.**
>
> — HackProduct autopsy

The Midjourney case resists easy summary because its most important decision looks like an infrastructure choice: "use Discord instead of building a website." But the infrastructure choice was downstream of a more fundamental judgment about what kind of product Midjourney was trying to be.

Holz was not trying to build a tool that users would adopt privately and recommend occasionally. He was trying to build a space where creative work was inherently social, where the act of making something was also the act of showing it, and where watching others create was itself a valuable activity. Discord was the platform that had already built that social architecture. Midjourney imported it instead of replicating it.

The lesson that travels is not "launch on Discord." Most products are not image generators, and most Discord communities are not trying to grow into AI product businesses. The lesson is the question Holz asked before choosing Discord: "where does my user already spend time doing something related to what I'm building, and what would it mean to build inside that space instead of asking them to leave it?" The answer to that question might be Discord, or it might be Slack, or Notion, or GitHub, or a newsletter, or a physical community. The question itself is what matters.
