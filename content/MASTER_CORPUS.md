## Part 1: What Is Product Sense (And Why Everyone Argues About It)

### The Consensus Definition
Product sense is "the ability to make correct product decisions even when faced with considerable ambiguity" (Shreyas Doshi). More practically: understanding what makes a product succeed — the right problems to solve, for the right users, with the right constraints.

The field largely agrees on three things:
1. **It's learnable** — not innate talent. "Intuition is just fast thinking from lots of practice." — Jackie Bavaro
2. **It's process made fast** — great product thinkers aren't magical; they've internalized a structured process until it feels instinctive
3. **Problem-first wins** — the single biggest failure mode is jumping to solutions. 70-80% of interview performance lives in user understanding and problem definition, not the feature idea

### The Decompositions (Pick One)

**Shreyas Doshi's Five Skills:**
1. Empathy — discover needs beyond what data shows
2. Simulation — envision futures grounded in domain knowledge
3. Strategic thinking — who's the target, what's the differentiation
4. Taste — choose the best option and articulate why
5. Creative execution — conceive solutions competitors can't easily copy

**Creator Economy's Three Skills (contrarian reframe):**
1. Empathy — customer research, feedback loops
2. Creativity — brainstorming, pattern matching across domains
3. Craft — obsessive attention to quality and detail

**Vincent Tong's Four Risks (PM on the job):**
1. Customer Value — will users find this valuable?
2. Usability — can they actually use it?
3. Feasibility — can we build it?
4. Business Viability — does it make business sense?

### The Contrarian Takes Worth Teaching

**"It's overrated" (Yahia Hassan):** Product sense without data and rigor is just opinion. The term implies you can develop it once and coast — false. It also implies exclusivity that discourages learning.

**"Replace it with skills that matter" (Creator Economy):** The vagueness is the problem. If you can't teach it, you can't improve it. Break it into empathy + creativity + craft and you can practice each.

**"Speed of conviction matters as much as quality" (Hassan again):** In the AI era, a B+ decision made quickly may beat an A+ decision made slowly. Product sense is necessary but not sufficient.

**Doshi's counter:** When execution is cheap, ideas are expensive. When everyone has the same AI tools, judgment is the differentiator. Product sense becomes *more* valuable, not less.

**The quote worth remembering:**
> "You can now create garbage at the speed of light. Most people are." — Productboard

## Part 2: How Engineers Specifically Fail (And Why)

This is the most important section for HackProduct's positioning. Our audience is engineers. They have a specific failure profile.

### Why Engineers Lack Product Sense (Structural)
- Traditional CS education trains on "how" — algorithms, efficiency, system design
- Interviews reward technical elegance, not user outcomes
- Engineers optimize for code quality, not problem-solution fit
- The gap isn't intelligence; it's vocabulary and reps

### The Nine Traits of a Product-Minded Engineer (Gergely Orosz, Pragmatic Engineer)
1. Proactive ideation — challenges specs, suggests alternatives
2. Business literacy — understands metrics, user behavior
3. Curiosity — asks "why" relentlessly
4. Communication — builds cross-department relationships
5. Trade-off analysis — balances engineering and product constraints
6. Pragmatic edge-case handling — knows when good enough is enough
7. Validation cycles — seeks early feedback through creative testing
8. End-to-end ownership — tracks real-world metrics after launch
9. Developed instincts — refined through deliberate learning loops

### Engineer-Specific Failure Modes in Interviews

**Solution Bias:** Jump straight to "let's add dark mode" before understanding what's wrong. Engineers are trained to build; they skip the diagnosis.

**Design for Self:** Build the product *they* would want. Forget older users, non-technical users, users with entirely different contexts.

**Over-engineering in isolation:** Work solo for too long before involving stakeholders. Write complex solutions that could have been redirected with one conversation.

**Conflating intuition with research:** When asked how they'd design something, say "I'd gather data first" — without demonstrating any actual reasoning. The interview wants to see your thinking, not your process.

**Framework-sounding:** Saying "Using the CIRCLES method, I will first comprehend..." immediately loses the interviewer. Sounds like a recitation, not a conversation.

### The "Why" Mindset Gap
Engineers ask *how*. Product-minded engineers ask *why* first.
- Why is this the problem worth solving?
- Why do users behave this way?
- Why would this solution be chosen over alternatives?
- Why would this matter to the business?

This is the core reps HackProduct should build. Not framework memorization — the *why before how* habit.

## Part 3: The Interview Landscape

### Question Type Breakdown (By Frequency)
- **Product Design** (~40%): "Design X for Y users"
- **Product Improvement** (~30%): "How would you improve [existing product]?"
- **Strategy/Vision** (~20%): "Should Netflix enter gaming?"
- **Growth/Metrics** (~10%): "How would you grow WhatsApp in India?"
- **Favorite Product** (~10%): "What's your favorite product and why?" (often opener)

### Top 20 Questions to Know (By Company)

**Meta:**
- Design a fitness app for Meta
- How would you improve Facebook Groups?
- Facebook Events is struggling. How would you turn it around?
- How would you improve WhatsApp with a goal to earn revenue?
- Design a jobs product for Facebook

**Google:**
- Design a Maps feature for cyclists
- How would you improve Google Search?
- How would you increase engagement on Google Maps?

**Amazon:**
- Design a product for a blind person
- How would you improve Amazon Prime?
- How would you reduce cart abandonment on Amazon?

**Apple:**
- What's your favorite Apple product and how would you improve it?
- Design an accessibility feature for iOS

**Airbnb:**
- Name 3 common problems guests have with their houses and what Airbnb can do
- How would you improve host trust on Airbnb?

**Netflix:**
- How would you improve Netflix's recommendation algorithm?
- How would you measure the success of Netflix's recommendation feature?

**Cross-company design/improvement:**
- Design a parking feature for Maps
- Design a camera for the elderly
- Design a phone call app for people who are hard of hearing
- Design a voice assistant for kids
- How would you improve Spotify?
- How would you improve LinkedIn's job search?
- How would you improve YouTube's content moderation?

### What Interviewers Are Actually Scoring (Meta's Framework, Propagated Across FAANG)

Meta originated the product sense interview in 2008. Their five criteria:
1. **Product landscape & motivation** — Do you understand why this product exists and what it's competing against?
2. **User identification** — Who actually matters? (Including second-order stakeholders in marketplace products)
3. **Problem identification & prioritization** — Did you brainstorm multiple problems and justify why you chose one?
4. **Creative, impactful solutions** — Multiple ideas, not one; differentiated from obvious answers
5. **Intentional design choices** — Design IS product sense; don't skip it

**Overall rubric across companies:**
- Systematic thinking (do you have a repeatable process?)
- Clear communication (does the interviewer follow your logic?)
- User empathy (do you demonstrate genuine curiosity about users?)
- Adaptability (can you adjust if challenged?)
- Business acumen (do you consider constraints and tradeoffs?)
- Problem-first mindset (70% time on users/problems, 30% on solutions)

## Part 4: The Major Frameworks

### Ben Erez's Five-Part Framework (Lenny's Newsletter — Industry Standard)
The most widely adopted interview framework, developed after studying 50+ candidates at Meta.

**Step 1: Clear Communication (3-5 min)**
- State role assumptions, geography, constraints
- Announce your game plan: "I'll describe the product, then segment users, then identify problems, then propose solutions"
- Check in before proceeding

**Step 2: Product Motivation (3-5 min)**
- Why does this product exist?
- Value proposition, competitive positioning
- Write a mission statement that guides subsequent decisions

**Step 3: Segmentation (8-10 min)**
- Identify ecosystem players (beyond just end users)
- Create 2-3 behaviorally distinct segments (NOT demographics alone)
- Prioritize one using "reach × underserved degree"
- Develop a specific persona

**Step 4: Problem Identification (8-10 min)**
- Map user journey across stages
- Identify pain points, prioritize by frequency × severity
- Connect back to mission statement

**Step 5: Solution Development (8-10 min)**
- Multiple approaches (not one feature)
- Evaluate with impact vs. effort matrix
- Outline v1 with risk mitigation

**Key principle:** 70% of time on users and problems. Excellence in one area can't compensate for weakness in another.

### Lewis Lin's CIRCLES Method
**C** — Comprehend the situation (goal, constraints)
**I** — Identify the customer (personas)
**R** — Report customer needs (beyond surface needs)
**C** — Cut through prioritization (narrow the backlog)
**L** — List solutions (at least 3)
**E** — Evaluate tradeoffs (pros/cons per predefined criteria)
**S** — Summarize recommendation (connect to user needs + business goals)

### Pshimanshu's SPACE Framework (Story-First)
An alternative that feels more natural in conversation:
**S** — Story Hook (0-5 min): Personal anecdote showing observed user behavior
**P** — Problem Definition (5-12 min): Functional + emotional + social job analysis
**A** — Audience Lens (12-20 min): Behavioral segments, not demographics
**C** — Core Approach (20-30 min): Multiple philosophical approaches before features
**E** — Evaluation & MVP (30-40 min): Success metrics, MVP definition, go-to-market

Why it works: transforms rigid prep into a natural conversation that reveals real thinking.

### Shreyas Doshi's 10-30-50 Rule
You don't need to be top 10% in all five product sense skills. Aim for:
- Top 10% in one (your natural strength)
- Top 30% in another
- Top 50% in the third

This makes product sense attainable, not overwhelming.

## Part 5: Famous Product Decisions Worth Teaching

These are the cases that appear repeatedly in teardowns, interviews, and coaching. Each teaches a specific lesson.

### Instagram Stories — Execution > Innovation
- **What happened:** Instagram copied Snapchat Stories exactly, feature-for-feature
- **Result:** 100M users in 2 months; Snapchat's growth stalled permanently
- **Lesson:** Distribution and integration beat originality. Snapchat invented the format; Instagram had the network. Being second with better distribution wins.
- **Product sense angle:** "Build the right thing" sometimes means "build what users already want, for users you already have"

### Gmail 1GB Storage — Constraint Removal
- **What happened:** In 2004, email storage was measured in MBs. Gmail launched with 1GB free.
- **Result:** Changed user behavior permanently; made "deleting email" obsolete
- **Lesson:** Sometimes the best product decision is removing a constraint users had accepted as normal. Ask: what do users *tolerate* that they shouldn't have to?
- **Product sense angle:** User pain points aren't always what users complain about — sometimes they're what users have stopped noticing

### Spotify Wrapped — Engineered Virality
- **What happened:** Wrapped makes 400M users feel like the main character for 48 hours, every December
- **Result:** 100M+ shares annually; taught in business schools; highest organic reach of any Spotify initiative
- **Lesson:** Virality isn't luck. It requires combining three things: personalization (it's about *you*), identity (it says something about *who you are*), and timing (it happens at the right cultural moment)
- **Product sense angle:** The "job" Wrapped does isn't "show listening stats." It's "let me show the world who I am through music."

### TikTok Algorithm — Creator Democracy
- **What happened:** TikTok showed content based on engagement signals, not follower counts
- **Result:** New creators could go viral on day one; incumbents couldn't coast on audiences
- **Lesson:** Choosing your optimization metric is a values decision. Follower-based metrics entrench winners; engagement-based metrics democratize reach.
- **Product sense angle:** What are the second-order social effects of your ranking algorithm?

### Netflix $1M Algorithm Prize — Quantifying the Metric
- **What happened:** Netflix paid $1M to improve its recommendation algorithm by 10%
- **Result:** 80%+ of Netflix viewing now comes from recommendations; saves ~$1B/year in content costs
- **Lesson:** Not all metrics are equal. A 10% improvement in the right metric is worth $1M research investment.
- **Product sense angle:** Do you know which metric actually drives your business outcomes, vs. which metric *feels* important?

### Slack — Network Effect Trap
- **What happened:** Slack adoption is all-or-nothing — one person can't use it alone
- **Result:** $1M revenue in 2 weeks; $24B IPO; nearly impossible to displace once adopted
- **Lesson:** Network effects create moats but also create bottlenecks. The product strategy must account for the adoption curve: who's the wedge user?
- **Product sense angle:** Design for the first user to join, not just the power user

### Figma — Architectural Lock-In
- **What happened:** Figma made design files collaborative and browser-native
- **Result:** Not just a better Sketch — an entirely different coordination layer for product teams
- **Lesson:** The most durable moats come from architectural choices that change how people work together, not from better features
- **Product sense angle:** What would make your product irreplaceable rather than just preferred?

### Threads — Network Portability
- **What happened:** 100M users in 5 days — fastest growing app ever
- **Result:** Stalled after initial spike; retention proved much harder than acquisition
- **Lesson:** Distribution (Instagram) can fill the top of funnel, but it can't substitute for genuine product-market fit. Acquisition ≠ retention.
- **Product sense angle:** Launching is the easy part. What problem does a user have *on day 7* that you need to solve?

## Part 6: The Analytical Lenses (For Teardowns and Coaching)

These are the frameworks that appear in product teardowns and that Luma should use when coaching.

### AARRR (Pirate Metrics — Dave McClure)
- **Acquisition** — How do users find you?
- **Activation** — Do they have a good first experience?
- **Retention** — Do they come back?
- **Revenue** — How do you make money?
- **Referral** — Do they tell others?

*Use when:* Diagnosing growth problems, measuring product success, designing metrics frameworks

### Jobs to Be Done (Christensen/Ulwick)
"People don't hire products — they hire them to get a job done."
- A fitness app isn't hired to "track calories." It's hired to "feel less guilty after dinner" or "prove discipline to my social circle."
- Forces you to ask: what's the actual outcome the user wants?

*Use when:* Segmenting users, writing personas, evaluating whether a feature addresses real need

### North Star Metric
- One metric that best captures the value the product delivers to users
- Netflix: hours streamed. Airbnb: nights booked. Spotify: time listening.
- Forces alignment on what "success" actually means

*Use when:* Defining product strategy, prioritizing features, setting team goals

### Aggregation Theory (Ben Thompson / Stratechery)
- Internet companies that aggregate demand can dictate terms to suppliers
- Google aggregates search attention → media companies must optimize for Google
- Applies to platform strategy questions

*Use when:* Strategy questions about platform businesses, "should X enter Y market"

### Network Effects Lenses
- **Direct:** Each user makes the product better for others (WhatsApp, Facebook)
- **Cross-side:** Two groups make each product better for the other (Airbnb, Uber)
- **Indirect:** More users attract better third-party services (iOS app ecosystem)

*Use when:* Analyzing moats, growth strategy, platform questions

## Part 7: How to Develop Product Sense (Practical Reps)

### Jules Walter's Four Exercises (via Lenny's Newsletter)
1. **Observe users interacting with products** (2-4x monthly) — attend user research, watch face expressions, ask open-ended questions
2. **Deconstruct everyday products** (1-2 hours monthly) — try new products, analyze your own emotional reactions, compare competitors
3. **Learn from great product thinkers** — attend product reviews, take notes on feedback patterns, reverse-engineer mental checklists
4. **Stay curious about technology trends** — ask "if this trend reaches full potential, what opportunities emerge?"

### Edward Huang's Four Steps for Engineers
1. Understand the bigger vision (why does your team exist?)
2. Empathize with end users (pair with support; learn during incidents)
3. Propose product suggestions (balance effort with business impact)
4. Seek feedback regularly (ask PMs to evaluate your thinking)

### The Reps HackProduct Should Build
- **"Why before how" habit** — challenge yourself to ask why a problem is worth solving before proposing solutions
- **Behavioral segmentation** — practice creating user segments based on motivations, not demographics
- **Problem prioritization** — given 5 pain points, pick one and defend the choice
- **Tradeoff articulation** — take a product decision and argue both sides
- **Teardown practice** — analyze one product per week: what job does it do, who hired it, what trade-offs were made?
