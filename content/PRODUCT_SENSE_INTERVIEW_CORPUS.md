## Evaluation Frameworks Mentioned in Interviews

### Popular Frameworks Used in Product Sense Interviews:

1. **CIRCLES Method** - Used for product design questions
   - Clarify: Understand the product and objectives
   - Identify: Define users and their needs
   - Report: Articulate the problem
   - Cut through: Prioritize solutions
   - List: Brainstorm features
   - Evaluate: Assess tradeoffs
   - Summarize: Present recommendation

2. **GAME Framework** - For structuring answers
   - Goals: Define success objectives
   - Action: Outline approach/strategy
   - Metrics: Identify KPIs
   - Evaluation: Assess results

3. **AARM Framework** - For metrics questions
   - Acquisition: How users discover/join
   - Activation: First key actions
   - Retention: Keep users coming back
   - Monetization: Generate revenue

4. **AARRR Framework** - For growth metrics
   - Acquisition: Getting users
   - Activation: Initial engagement
   - Retention: Keep returning
   - Referral: Viral/word-of-mouth
   - Revenue: Monetization

5. **RICE Method** - For prioritization
   - Reach: How many users affected
   - Impact: How much it moves the needle
   - Confidence: Certainty of impact
   - Effort: Resources required

6. **BUS Framework** - For product design
   - Begin: Clarify scope and target users
   - Understand: Identify user needs
   - Suggest: Propose solutions

7. **SBI Framework** - For behavioral responses
   - Situation: Context
   - Behavior: What you did
   - Impact: What resulted

8. **5 Whys** - For root cause analysis
9. **Jobs to be Done (JTBD)** - User motivation framework
10. **Fishbone Diagram** - For problem analysis

## Key Evaluation Dimensions

Interviewers assess candidates across these dimensions (must pass ALL):

1. **Clear Communication** - Articulate ideas logically and concisely
2. **Product Motivation** - Demonstrate empathy for users and understanding of business goals
3. **Segmentation** - Identify distinct user groups and prioritize target segment
4. **Problem Identification** - Define the core user problem worth solving
5. **Solution Development** - Propose thoughtful, feasible solutions
6. **Structural Thinking** - Organize approach systematically
7. **Creativity** - Show innovative thinking while remaining practical
8. **Data-Driven Mindset** - Reference metrics and data, not just intuition

## What Interviewers Look For

Across all product sense interviews, top performers demonstrate:

- **User Empathy**: Deep understanding of who users are and what they need
- **Problem Framing**: Ability to identify root problems, not just surface-level complaints
- **Structured Thinking**: Following a clear methodology, not jumping to solutions
- **Balanced Decision-Making**: Considering user needs, business goals, and constraints
- **Communication**: Explaining reasoning step-by-step so interviewers can follow your logic
- **Prioritization**: Making defensible choices about what matters most
- **Market Awareness**: Understanding competitive landscape and company positioning
- **Metric Literacy**: Comfort with KPIs, OKRs, and data-driven analysis

## Company-Specific Patterns

### Meta/Facebook
- Heavy emphasis on user engagement and retention metrics
- Often asks about improving their own products (Instagram, WhatsApp, Messenger, etc.)
- Frequently includes AI/ML product questions
- Tests cross-product thinking (Messenger vs. WhatsApp parity)
- Asks candidates to consider encryption and privacy constraints

### Google
- Frequently asks "design a feature for X user group" (e.g., blind users, cyclists)
- Heavy focus on Maps and Search product questions
- Emphasizes user accessibility
- Tests ability to think about enterprise vs. consumer products
- Strong emphasis on data and metrics

### Amazon
- Often includes business/strategy elements (not just product)
- Tests leadership principles (customer obsession, bias for action, etc.)
- Asks about marketplace dynamics and two-sided platforms
- Includes questions about new business lines (pharmacy, healthcare, etc.)
- Emphasizes customer satisfaction metrics

### Uber/Lyft
- Focus on two-sided marketplace dynamics (drivers and riders)
- Tests understanding of logistics and operations
- Often asks about driver/rider experience tradeoffs
- Includes market expansion questions
- Tests growth metrics and unit economics

### Airbnb
- Tests marketplace platform thinking
- Asks about guest and host needs/problems
- Heavy focus on trust and safety
- Tests ability to handle global/cultural differences
- Includes pricing and monetization questions

### Netflix
- Emphasis on engagement and content strategy
- Tests understanding of subscriber retention
- Asks about content curation and personalization
- Often includes AI/recommendation system thinking
- Tests understanding of competitive landscape (Disney+, etc.)

### Pinterest
- Strong focus on user discovery and recommendations
- Tests understanding of visual search and pins
- Asks about creator economy and incentives
- Tests engagement and retention metrics
- Often includes international expansion questions

### LinkedIn
- Tests B2B vs. B2C thinking
- Emphasis on professional use cases
- Tests understanding of multi-sided platform (job seekers, recruiters, companies)
- Includes content distribution and virality questions
- Tests business/enterprise thinking

## Interview Timing & Format

- **Duration**: 45-60 minutes (typically 35-40 minutes for the core exercise)
- **Structure**: Introduction → Question posed → Candidate thinks aloud → Interviewer probes → Closing questions
- **Think-Aloud**: Good candidates explain their reasoning at each step
- **Follow-Up Questions**: Interviewers will dig deeper on unclear points or push back on weak logic
- **Written vs. Verbal**: Some companies ask you to write answers; most are verbal with note-taking

## Critical Do's and Don'ts

### DO:
- Ask clarifying questions about scope, constraints, and definitions
- Define your target user clearly and early
- Use a structured framework (CIRCLES, GAME, etc.)
- Think out loud so the interviewer follows your logic
- Include both quantitative and qualitative thinking
- Acknowledge tradeoffs instead of pretending they don't exist
- Reference real products and user behaviors you've observed
- Ask for feedback if you're unsure of direction

### DON'T:
- Jump to solutions before understanding the problem
- Design for "everyone" (segment and choose a specific user)
- Ignore business constraints and profitability
- Make up metrics instead of reasoning about what matters
- Assume the interviewer knows what you mean without explaining
- Change direction frequently without explaining why
- Spend the whole time on one aspect (need balance across dimensions)
- Memorize answers; instead, practice the process
